Bottom Line

Among the strongest non-pharma interventions on this list. Dose-response is striking. Even if causality is partial, the downside is small. If you have sauna access, use it 3-4x/week. Hot tub may approximate.

What this means for you

If you can get to a Finnish sauna 3–4 times a week, do it. The data linking frequent sauna use to lower heart-disease and overall death rates is some of the strongest non-drug evidence on this site. Hot tub may be a partial substitute. Cost-of-life from doing it: roughly zero.

How to actually do this

  • Aim for 4 sessions per week of 20 minutes each at 80–100°C (175–210°F). Frequency drives the benefit more than length.
  • Hydrate well before and after. Add electrolytes if you sweat heavily.
  • Cool down gradually. Sit for a minute before standing (orthostatic dizziness is the main risk).
  • Don't use a sauna while drunk. Don't use one alone if you have any cardiovascular history.
  • Infrared saunas are easier to install at home but evidence is weaker than for traditional Finnish. Use traditional if you have access.
  • Hot tub at 40°C / 104°F for 30+ minutes has emerging similar-but-smaller signal. Better than nothing.
  • If you have heart disease, control hypertension, or take BP meds, talk to your doctor before starting.
Rough cost: Free if your gym has one. Home sauna: $3,000–$10,000+ one-time. Hot tub: $4,000–$15,000.

Worth considering if

Pretty much everyone with access. Especially people with hypertension or moderate cardiovascular risk (after medical clearance).

Probably skip if

Severe aortic stenosis, pregnancy (heat caution), active fever/infection, severe orthostatic hypotension, recent heart attack.

Helps with

Heart disease Other neurodegenerative Cognitive decline Chronic inflammation

Why it works

Sitting in 80–100°C heat for 20 minutes is, physiologically, surprisingly close to moderate exercise. Your heart rate climbs to 120–150 bpm, blood vessels dilate, and your body produces 'heat shock proteins' — molecular tools that help every cell repair damaged proteins and resist future stress. Done repeatedly, it lowers blood pressure, improves how your blood vessels function, and seems to add up to real cardiovascular benefit.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Repeated heat exposure (Finnish-style sauna ~80–100°C for 15–30 min) elevates heart rate similar to moderate exercise, induces heat-shock proteins, improves endothelial function, and reduces BP over time. Frequency-dependent.

Evidence Summary

Laukkanen et al. (KIHD cohort, 2015 onward) showed dose-response: 4-7 sauna sessions/week vs 1/week associated with 40% lower all-cause mortality, 50% lower CV mortality, 66% lower dementia risk over 20+ years. Replicated in subsequent papers and other Finnish cohorts. RCTs on BP and endothelial function support mechanism.
