Bottom Line

Don't take this for longevity alone. If you're taking it for hair loss or BPH, the mortality data is reassuring; if you're not, the evidence isn't strong enough to start.

What this means for you

Finasteride is a hair-loss and prostate drug that turned out to have an intriguing mortality signal in a big cohort study. Don't start it just for longevity — the sexual side effects (in 2–5% of men, sometimes persistent) are a real cost. If you're already taking it for hair loss or BPH, the longevity data is at least reassuring.

How to actually do this

  • Don't start this purely for longevity. The evidence isn't strong enough.
  • If you're taking it for hair loss (1 mg/day) or BPH (5 mg/day), the longevity cohort data is reassuring — you're not paying a hidden mortality cost.
  • Topical finasteride (1% solution applied to scalp) is an alternative for hair loss with much lower systemic exposure and side-effect rate.
  • Discuss sexual side-effect risk with your doctor before starting. Most men have no issues; a minority have lasting changes.
  • If you develop sexual side effects, stop the drug — most resolve within months.
  • Pregnant women must NEVER handle crushed/broken tablets — the drug is teratogenic to male fetuses through skin contact.
  • If you have liver disease, get cleared by your doctor.
Rough cost: $10–$30/month generic.

Worth considering if

Men with male-pattern baldness or moderate-to-severe BPH symptoms.

Probably skip if

Anyone considering it solely for longevity. Men trying to conceive (effects on semen possible). Any pregnant woman should avoid handling the drug.

Helps with

Cancer

Why it works

Finasteride blocks an enzyme that converts testosterone into a stronger form called DHT. Less DHT means less prostate growth (good for older men with BPH) and slower male-pattern hair loss. The same enzyme has roles in metabolism and cardiovascular tissue, which is the proposed mechanism for the unexpected mortality signal. The trade-off: some men get sexual side effects (libido, erectile, ejaculatory) that occasionally persist after stopping the drug — 'post-finasteride syndrome' — which remains controversial but real for the men who experience it.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Inhibits 5α-reductase → reduces conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT). Lowers prostate volume, slows male-pattern hair loss. Possible anti-androgenic effects on multiple aging-relevant tissues.

Evidence Summary

PCPT (2003) showed reduced overall prostate cancer incidence (24%), but increased high-grade tumors (later attributed largely to detection bias). 18-year follow-up of PCPT showed no increase in mortality. A 2020 retrospective cohort suggested lower all-cause mortality on finasteride users vs non-users (~25%); not RCT-validated. Mouse studies show minimal lifespan effect. Side effect concerns (post-finasteride syndrome) remain debated.

Key Studies

RCT follow-up n=18882 2019 NEJM
Goodman et al.
Finding: No difference in 18-year all-cause mortality between finasteride and placebo arms. High-grade cancer signal did not translate to mortality difference.
Limitations: Original PCPT confounded by detection bias.
Use of finasteride and risk of cause-specific mortality (cohort) ↗
Cohort n=4068 2024 Andrology
Sakai et al.
Finding: Lower all-cause mortality in finasteride users (HR 0.75) over follow-up.
Limitations: Healthy-user bias likely; observational.

Studied Dose / Protocol

1mg/day (hair loss); 5mg/day (BPH).

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • All-cause mortality (observational)
  • Cardiovascular events

Side Effects

  • Sexual dysfunction (~2-5%, sometimes persistent)
  • Gynecomastia
  • Mood changes
  • Post-finasteride syndrome (controversial)

Contraindications / Cautions

  • Pregnancy (crumbled tablets exposure to women is teratogenic)
  • Liver disease
