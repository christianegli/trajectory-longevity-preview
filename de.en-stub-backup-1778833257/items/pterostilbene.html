Pterostilbene

Methylated resveratrol analog with better bioavailability. Limited human longevity data.

Bottom Line

Skip. The polyphenol-via-pill thesis is weak. Get them from food.

What this means for you

Pterostilbene is essentially resveratrol's better-absorbed cousin. Marketed as a longevity supplement; the human data is thin. The whole 'polyphenol supplement' thesis has not aged well. Skip it. Eat blueberries (where pterostilbene naturally occurs in tiny amounts), grapes, nuts, dark chocolate, EVOO — and call it a day.

How to actually do this

  • Honestly: skip the supplement. Eat the foods (blueberries, grapes, almonds) instead.
  • If you must: 50–250 mg/day, but expect little.
  • If you also take resveratrol, you don't need both — same general pathway.
  • May raise LDL slightly at higher doses; check lipids if you take it long-term.
  • Avoid if on anticoagulants (theoretical bleeding risk).
Rough cost: $20–$50/month.

Worth considering if

Probably nobody, given the cost-to-evidence ratio.

Probably skip if

Most people. On anticoagulants without medical advice.

Helps with

Mitochondrial decline

Why it works

Pterostilbene is structurally similar to resveratrol but with two methyl groups attached, which makes it much better absorbed and longer-lasting in the body. It activates similar antioxidant and energy-sensing pathways. The mechanistic story is fine; the human longevity outcome data is essentially non-existent. Same general critique as resveratrol: looks good in cells and rodents, doesn't translate compellingly to humans.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Dimethylated analog of resveratrol; more bioavailable and longer half-life. Similar antioxidant and AMPK-modulating profile.

Evidence Summary

Better bioavailability than resveratrol, but the question is whether the resveratrol bet was worth taking in the first place. Some animal data on cognitive function and metabolic markers. Small human trials on lipids and BP are mixed. No long-term outcome trials.
