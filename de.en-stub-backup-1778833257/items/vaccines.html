Vaccines (flu, shingles, pneumococcal)

Annual flu vaccine reduces CV mortality 28% in recent MI patients (IAMI 2021). Shingles vaccine reduces dementia risk 20%. Among the most evidence-supported foundation interventions.

Bottom Line

Foundation tier. Stack the vaccines you're eligible for. Annual flu shot has a CV mortality signal larger than most pills on this site. Shingles vaccine 50+. Pneumococcal sequence per current guidelines for 50-65+. RSV vaccine for 60+ if available. None require a doctor's appointment beyond a primary-care visit; most are free or under $50 even without insurance. The single most under-utilised foundation in the catalogue.

What this means for you

Vaccination is one of the highest-leverage longevity interventions there is, but it almost never appears on longevity-site lists because it isn't a supplement or a protocol. The flu vaccine, given annually, reduces cardiovascular mortality in the year following — the IAMI 2021 trial in recent MI patients showed 28% reduction in CV death and 41% reduction in all-cause death. The shingles vaccine reduces both the disease and (separately) dementia incidence — the natural-experiment data from Wales is one of the strongest pieces of evidence we have that infection contributes to neurodegeneration. Pneumococcal vaccination prevents pneumonia, which is one of the leading causes of death in older adults. None of these cost much; coverage is poor; the mortality benefit is comparable to taking a statin.

How to actually do this

Annual flu vaccine — every year, ideally September-October before flu season. Standard-dose for under 65; high-dose or adjuvanted vaccine for 65+. Shingles vaccine (Shingrix, recombinant zoster) — 2-dose series at age 50+; very effective (>90%) and durable. Don't skip the second dose. Pneumococcal vaccines for 50+ (varies by guideline) — PCV20 single dose or PCV15 followed by PPSV23. Discuss schedule with primary care. RSV vaccine for 60+ where available (Arexvy, Abrysvo) — newer, growing evidence base. Tdap booster every 10 years (covers tetanus and pertussis). COVID-19 boosters per current guidelines.

Rough cost: Free–$50/yr

Worth considering if

Every adult, especially 50+. Anyone with diabetes, cardiovascular disease, COPD, or immunosuppression has stronger relative benefit. Recent MI/heart surgery patients have especially strong indication for flu vaccine. Adults caring for or living with infants, elderly, or immunocompromised people.

Probably skip if

Severe allergic reaction to a previous dose of the same vaccine. Active acute illness with high fever (delay until recovered). Severe immunosuppression: live vaccines (e.g., live attenuated flu nasal spray, MMR, varicella) should be avoided; inactivated/recombinant vaccines (current flu shot, Shingrix, pneumococcal) are still safe. Specific contraindications: prior Guillain-Barré within 6 weeks of a flu vaccine warrants caution.

Why it works

Vaccines work by training your immune system to recognise pathogens before you encounter them. Beyond preventing the specific infection, recent research shows that systemic infections (flu, shingles, pneumonia) trigger inflammatory responses that destabilise atherosclerotic plaques (causing heart attacks), accelerate neurodegeneration (contributing to dementia), and trigger frailty cascades in older adults. Preventing infection prevents these knock-on effects.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Influenza infection is associated with 4-10x increased MI risk in the 7 days following infection (Kwong 2018 NEJM self-controlled case series, n=20,000). Mechanism: systemic inflammation, IL-6/TNF-α elevation, plaque instability, prothrombotic state. Vaccination prevents infection (~40-60% in healthy adults, lower in elderly) and also produces partial protection against post-infection cardiovascular sequelae. Shingles (VZV reactivation) involves viral neurotropism; the natural-experiment evidence (Eyting 2024 Nature using Welsh date-of-birth eligibility cutoff) suggests reactivation of latent VZV may contribute to dementia pathogenesis directly via neurovascular and inflammatory mechanisms. Pneumococcal vaccination (PCV13/PCV15/PCV20 conjugate vaccines + PPSV23 polysaccharide) prevents invasive pneumococcal disease and reduces pneumonia hospitalisations. Composite mortality benefit when stacked.

Evidence Summary

Flu and CV outcomes — IAMI trial (Frobert 2021 Circulation): RCT of 2,571 patients within 72h of acute MI or high-risk CAD randomised to flu vaccine vs placebo. At 12 months: 28% reduction in CV death, 41% reduction in all-cause mortality, NNT to prevent one CV death = 71. Effect comparable to dual antiplatelet therapy for the post-MI population. Kwong 2018 (NEJM) self-controlled case series found 4-10x increased MI incidence in 7 days following lab-confirmed flu, supporting causal mechanism. Cochrane reviews (Demicheli 2018) confirm modest direct flu prevention (~40% in healthy adults, lower in elderly) but the cardiovascular signal is the dominant longevity benefit. Shingles and dementia — Eyting 2024 (Nature) used Welsh date-of-birth eligibility cutoff for shingles vaccine as natural experiment (n=282,541): vaccinated cohort had 20% relative reduction in dementia diagnosis over 7 years. Confirms similar US/Australian observational studies. The natural-experiment design eliminates many usual confounders. Pneumococcal — Falkenhorst 2017 meta-analysis: PCV13 in adults 65+ reduced vaccine-type pneumonia 45%, all-cause pneumonia ~12%. CAPiTA trial (Bonten 2015 NEJM) was the foundational RCT (n=84,496) showing 45% efficacy against vaccine-type pneumococcal CAP. Mortality benefit established but smaller than flu/MI benefit. RSV — RENOIR trial (Papi 2023 NEJM) showed Arexvy 82% effective against severe RSV LRTD in 60+. Newer vaccine; longer-term data still accumulating.

Key Studies

Influenza Vaccination after Myocardial Infarction (IAMI trial, Frobert 2021) ↗
Multicenter RCT n=2571 2021 Circulation
Frobert et al.
Finding: Influenza vaccination within 72h of acute MI reduced CV death by 28%, all-cause mortality by 41%, and MI/stent thrombosis by 27% over 12 months. NNT for CV death = 71.
Limitations: Single-blind; trial stopped early during COVID-19 pandemic affecting follow-up; specific high-risk population (post-MI).
A natural experiment on the effect of herpes zoster vaccination on dementia (Eyting 2024) ↗
Quasi-experimental natural experiment (regression discontinuity) n=282541 2024 Nature
Eyting et al.
Finding: Welsh shingles vaccination eligibility cutoff used as instrument: vaccinated cohort had 20% relative reduction in dementia diagnosis over 7 years. Effect concentrated in women.
Limitations: Quasi-experimental (not RCT); 7-year follow-up; older Zostavax (live attenuated) — Shingrix benefit may differ; cohort-specific.
Acute Myocardial Infarction after Laboratory-Confirmed Influenza Infection (Kwong 2018) ↗
Self-controlled case series n=19729 2018 New England Journal of Medicine
Kwong et al.
Finding: Risk of MI was 6.05x higher (95% CI 3.86-9.50) in the 7 days following lab-confirmed influenza vs control intervals.
Limitations: Self-controlled (subject is own control); confined to lab-confirmed flu cases; risk window definition affects estimate.
Polysaccharide Conjugate Vaccine against Pneumococcal Pneumonia in Adults (CAPiTA, Bonten 2015) ↗
Multicenter RCT n=84496 2015 New England Journal of Medicine
Bonten et al.
Finding: PCV13 was 45.6% effective against vaccine-type CAP and 75% against vaccine-type invasive pneumococcal disease in adults 65+ over median 4 years.
Limitations: Older PCV13 (now superseded by PCV20); Netherlands-based; specific endpoints.
Respiratory Syncytial Virus Prefusion F Protein Vaccine in Older Adults (RENOIR, Papi 2023) ↗
Multicenter RCT n=24973 2023 New England Journal of Medicine
Papi et al.
Finding: Arexvy (RSVPreF3-AS01E) 82.6% effective against RSV-associated lower respiratory tract disease in adults 60+; 94.1% effective against severe disease.
Limitations: Single-season efficacy; durability beyond 2 years still accumulating.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Annual flu vaccine; 2-dose Shingrix series (50+); single PCV20 or PCV15 + PPSV23 sequence; Arexvy or Abrysvo for RSV (60+); standard schedules per public-health authorities
