Folate (Vitamin B9)

Essential B-vitamin. Mandatory in pregnancy (prevents neural tube defects). Modest stroke-prevention evidence in folate-naive populations. Mostly handled by fortified foods.

Bottom Line

Mandatory periconceptionally — non-negotiable. Otherwise: covered by fortified flour and leafy greens for most Western diets. If you live in a non-fortified country and have hypertension, low folate intake may be a real lever (per CSPPT). Always test/supplement B12 alongside folate to avoid masking B12 deficiency.

What this means for you

Essential B-vitamin. THE one mandatory supplement during pregnancy and pre-pregnancy — it prevents about 70% of neural tube birth defects. For everyone else, fortified flour and leafy greens cover most needs. There's modest stroke-prevention evidence (21% reduction) in populations that don't have folate-fortified food, but the U.S. and most of Europe fortify flour so the marginal benefit of supplements is small.

How to actually do this

If pregnant or trying: 0.4-0.8 mg folic acid (or methylfolate) daily, starting 1+ months before conception, continuing through 12 weeks gestation. Higher dose (4mg) if prior NTD pregnancy. For everyone else: leafy greens (spinach, romaine), legumes, fortified bread/cereal cover requirements. Supplement only if intake is poor or you live in a non-fortified country with hypertension. Always co-test B12 if supplementing (folate can mask B12-deficiency anemia).

Rough cost: $3-8/month for generic folic acid 0.4-0.8mg. Methylfolate slightly more.

Why it works

Your cells need folate to copy DNA when they divide. The cells dividing fastest in your body — bone marrow making blood cells, and a fetus's developing nervous system — are most vulnerable to folate deficiency. That's why folate matters most in pregnancy.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Folate, after intestinal absorption and conversion to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF), donates methyl groups for: thymidylate synthesis (DNA bases), purine synthesis, and homocysteine remethylation to methionine (then SAM, the universal methyl donor). MTHFR polymorphisms (C677T) reduce conversion efficiency, making the active 5-MTHF form preferable in some individuals.

Evidence Summary

Periconceptional folate supplementation reduces neural tube defects ~70% (MRC Vitamin Study 1991, large pre-fortification cohorts) — this is the strongest established benefit. Folate-fortified flour mandates (US 1998, China later) reduced NTD rates by ~30%. CSPPT trial (n=20,702): folate 0.8mg + enalapril vs enalapril alone reduced first stroke by 21% in Chinese hypertensive adults (most folate-naive). HOPE-2 trial (Western, mostly folate-replete) was negative. So benefit depends on baseline status.

Limitations: Folate-naive population (no fortification). May not generalize to fortified populations.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Pregnancy/preconception: 0.4-0.8 mg/day (4 mg/day if prior NTD pregnancy). Adults general: 0.4 mg/day. Hypertensive folate-naive: 0.8 mg/day.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Neural tube defects (definitive)
  • Stroke (CSPPT positive, others negative)
  • Homocysteine (consistently lowered)

Side Effects

  • Generally none at recommended doses
  • May mask B12 deficiency (correcting the anemia while neuropathy progresses)

Contraindications / Cautions

  • High-dose unmasking B12 deficiency without simultaneous B12 testing
  • Some chemotherapies (consult oncology)
