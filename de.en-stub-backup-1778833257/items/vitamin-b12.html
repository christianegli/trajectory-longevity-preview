Vitamin B12

Essential vitamin. Deficiency is common in older adults and vegans, causes irreversible neuro damage. Supplementation only matters if you're deficient.

Multiple human RCTs with mortality/major-event endpoints. Regulatory consensus.

Bottom Line

Critical if you're deficient — and you might be without knowing. Test before supplementing if you're unsure (serum B12, methylmalonic acid). Mandatory for vegans. Don't take 'energy' shots — those are placebos for non-deficient people. The CV-prevention story didn't hold up in trials.

What this means for you

Essential vitamin you can only get from animal foods (or supplements). Deficiency is common (10-30% of people over 65, and most strict vegans without supplements). Symptoms — fatigue, brain fog, tingling in hands/feet — can be subtle until permanent nerve damage occurs. Supplementing only matters if you're actually deficient; for everyone else, it's an expensive yellow stream in the toilet.

How to actually do this

First: test if you might be deficient. Get serum B12 and methylmalonic acid (MMA — more sensitive). Test if: you're 60+, vegan/vegetarian, on metformin or PPIs long-term, have unexplained fatigue/numbness, or have macrocytic anemia. If deficient: 1000 mcg/day sublingual or oral for 8 weeks, then 1000 mcg/week or 2500 mcg twice weekly maintenance. If vegan but not deficient: 250-500 mcg/day or B12-fortified foods.

Rough cost: $5-10/month for generic methylcobalamin 1000 mcg. Sublingual or capsule equally effective.

Why it works

Your body needs B12 to make red blood cells and to maintain the protective coating around nerves. As you get older, your stomach makes less of the protein that absorbs B12 from food, so deficiency becomes increasingly common. Vegans have to supplement because B12 only exists naturally in animal foods.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Cobalamin is a cofactor for methionine synthase (transfers methyl group from 5-methyltetrahydrofolate to homocysteine, generating methionine and tetrahydrofolate) and methylmalonyl-CoA mutase (converts methylmalonyl-CoA to succinyl-CoA in the propionate pathway). Deficiency raises both homocysteine and methylmalonic acid (MMA). Absorption requires gastric intrinsic factor and ileal cubilin receptors.

Evidence Summary

B12 deficiency affects 10-30% of adults over 65 (Allen 2009 review) and most strict vegans without supplementation. Correction reverses anemia and early neuropathy; chronic deficiency causes irreversible spinal cord damage (subacute combined degeneration). Replacement is mandatory in deficiency. For non-deficient individuals: minimal evidence of cognitive or longevity benefit. The B-vitamin studies for CV disease prevention (HOPE-2, VITATOPS) were neutral or negative.

Key Studies

How common is vitamin B-12 deficiency? ↗
Review 2009 Am J Clin Nutr
Allen LH
Finding: B12 deficiency affects 10-30% of older adults globally; rates higher in vegan/vegetarian populations and those with atrophic gastritis. Subclinical deficiency much more common than overt anemia.
Limitations: Review article. Definitions of deficiency vary across studies.
Effects of homocysteine-lowering with folic acid plus vitamin B12 vs placebo on stroke and other vascular events in patients with stroke or transient ischemic attack ↗
RCT (3.4 year) n=8164 2010 Lancet Neurol
VITATOPS Trial Study Group
Finding: Folate + B12 + B6 lowered homocysteine but did not reduce stroke recurrence or composite vascular events vs placebo over 3.4 years.
Limitations: Negative result — directly tested whether B-vitamin supplementation prevents events in non-deficient population. Did not.

Studied Dose / Protocol

If deficient: 1000 mcg sublingual daily for 4-8 weeks, then 1000 mcg/week, OR IM injection 1000 mcg monthly. Maintenance for vegans: 250-500 mcg/day or 2500 mcg/week.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Macrocytic anemia
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Cognitive function (in deficient)
  • Homocysteine

Side Effects

  • Generally none — water soluble, excess excreted in urine
  • Acne flare in some users at high doses

Contraindications / Cautions

  • Leber's hereditary optic neuropathy (controversial)
