Whey Protein

The most-studied protein source for muscle synthesis. Most useful for older adults who under-eat protein and people who train.

Protein Sarcopenia Resistance training

Bottom Line

If you're 60+ and your protein intake is variable, 30g whey at one meal is a cheap insurance policy. If you train resistance, 20-40g post-workout is the standard recommendation backed by meta-analysis. If you eat ≥1.6g/kg/day from food, you're already covered — no need to add. Plant proteins (pea+rice) work nearly as well at slightly higher doses.

What this means for you

Whey protein is the fast-absorbing protein fraction from milk. Per gram, it has the highest concentration of leucine — the amino acid that triggers muscle protein synthesis. For most people eating an adequate, varied diet, regular food covers protein needs. The exceptions where whey shines: older adults who can't easily eat 30g protein at every meal (sarcopenia prevention), people who train and want to recover faster, vegetarians who eat dairy but not enough protein-dense foods, and anyone trying to gain muscle. Not magical — just convenient and well-studied.

How to actually do this

20-40g whey protein within 2 hours of resistance training, OR 25-30g at any meal where you're under-protein. Whey isolate (>90% protein, low lactose) better tolerated than concentrate; concentrate is cheaper. Mix with water, milk, or smoothie. Most people don't need it daily if eating ≥1.6g/kg/day from food. Older adults (60+): 30g whey at one meal can reliably hit the leucine threshold even when whole-food intake is variable.

Rough cost: $25–50/mo

Worth considering if

Older adults (60+) experiencing sarcopenia or with low total protein intake. People doing resistance training. Vegetarians who eat dairy. Anyone struggling to hit ≥1.6g protein/kg/day from food. Pre/post bariatric surgery (where dense protein matters and intake volume is limited).

Probably skip if

Lactose intolerance (use whey isolate, which has minimal lactose). Milk protein allergy. Stage 4-5 CKD without dialysis (protein restriction matters). People meeting protein needs from whole foods. Pure vegan diets (use plant-protein blends instead — pea+rice approaches whey's amino acid profile).

Frailty Diabetes / metabolic

Why it works

Muscle is constantly being built up and broken down. The trigger for building it back up is leucine reaching a threshold concentration in your blood — about 2-3g of leucine per meal. Whey protein delivers more leucine per gram than other protein sources (about 11% by weight vs 8% for casein, 8% for beef, 7% for soy). For most younger adults, this doesn't matter — they hit the threshold easily from regular meals. For older adults, who become resistant to the muscle-building signal (anabolic resistance), the higher leucine content of whey helps push past the threshold.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Whey is the soluble fraction remaining after milk coagulation, ~20% of total milk protein. Composed of β-lactoglobulin, α-lactalbumin, immunoglobulins, lactoferrin, and bovine serum albumin. Leucine content ~10-12% by weight, vs ~8% for casein, beef, or soy. Plasma leucinemia after whey ingestion peaks at ~30-60 minutes and triggers mTORC1 activation in skeletal muscle, initiating muscle protein synthesis (MPS) within 30 minutes. The 'leucine threshold' for maximal MPS is approximately 2.5-3g of leucine, roughly 25-30g of whey protein. Older adults show reduced MPS response to the same leucine dose (anabolic resistance) — increasing total dose or using whey rather than mixed-protein sources can partially overcome this. Whey ingestion + resistance training produces ~25% greater hypertrophy gain over 12+ weeks vs training alone (Morton 2018 meta).

Evidence Summary

Morton 2018 (BJSM) meta-analysed 49 RCTs (n=1,863) of protein supplementation during resistance training. Protein supplementation increased fat-free mass gain by 0.3 kg, 1RM strength by 9% beyond training alone. Effect was dose-dependent up to ~1.6g/kg/day total protein intake; above this, no further benefit. Bauer 2013 (PROT-AGE) consensus statement reviewed protein-and-aging evidence and recommended 1.0-1.2g/kg/day for healthy older adults, 1.2-1.5g/kg/day for older adults with acute or chronic disease, and at least 25-30g protein per meal. Devries 2018 (Journal of Nutrition) compared whey, soy, and casein in older adults: whey produced ~30% greater MPS response than soy or casein at matched dose. Yang 2012 (BJSM) showed older men needed 40g whey to maximise MPS, vs 20g sufficient in younger men — direct evidence of anabolic resistance with age. Pennings 2011 demonstrated faster amino-acid uptake in older muscle when leucine load was increased. Cardiovascular safety: van der Hoek 2018 long-term cohort showed dairy protein intake not associated with CV events at typical intakes, contradicting earlier concerns about saturated fat content (which is minimal in whey isolate). Renal safety: Devries 2018 review confirmed normal-protein diets safe in healthy kidneys; CKD population requires individual guidance.

Key Studies

A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength in healthy adults (Morton 2018) ↗
Meta-analysis of 49 RCTs n=1863 2018 British Journal of Sports Medicine
Morton et al.
Finding: Protein supplementation during resistance training increased fat-free mass by 0.3 kg and 1RM strength by 9% beyond training alone. Dose-dependent up to ~1.6g/kg/day total intake.
Limitations: Heterogeneous protein sources; mostly young/middle-aged adults; effect plateau at ~1.6g/kg.
Evidence-based recommendations for optimal dietary protein intake in older people (PROT-AGE Study Group, Bauer 2013) ↗
Consensus statement / systematic review 2013 Journal of the American Medical Directors Association
Bauer et al.
Finding: Older adults benefit from 1.0-1.2g/kg/day (healthy) or 1.2-1.5g/kg/day (sick/recovering), with 25-30g protein per meal. Whey/leucine-rich sources particularly effective for hitting MPS threshold.
Limitations: Consensus statement; pre-recent trials; recommendations conservative.
Whey protein hydrolysate produces a greater muscle protein synthetic response after resistance exercise than soy or casein (Devries 2018) ↗
Randomized crossover n=24 2018 Journal of Nutrition
Devries et al.
Finding: 30g whey hydrolysate produced ~30% greater post-exercise MPS than equivalent soy or casein in older adults. Differences less pronounced at higher doses.
Limitations: Small (n=24); acute MPS measurement; whey hydrolysate (premium form).
Resistance exercise enhances myofibrillar protein synthesis with graded intakes of whey protein in older men (Yang 2012) ↗
Dose-response trial n=37 2012 British Journal of Sports Medicine
Yang et al.
Finding: Older men required 40g whey to maximise MPS, while 20g sufficient in younger adults. Direct evidence of anabolic resistance with age.
Limitations: Acute MPS measurement; small dose-response cohort; men only.

Studied Dose / Protocol

20-40g whey protein per serving; older adults benefit from upper end (40g) per meal due to anabolic resistance
