Probiotics (Specific Strain Matters)

Effect is strain-specific. Strong evidence for antibiotic-associated diarrhea and a few specific GI conditions. Marketing claims about general 'gut health' are mostly hype.

Bottom Line

Match strain to indication. Buy probiotics for specific reasons (antibiotic course, IBS, traveler's diarrhea), not 'general gut health'. The evidence-based picks: L. rhamnosus GG (Culturelle) or Saccharomyces boulardii (Florastor) when on antibiotics. For IBS, look for studies of the specific strain in your formulation. Skip the multi-strain $50 'gut microbiome' supplements — most are unsupported by their own marketed claims. Fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut) are a cheaper, more pleasant alternative.

What this means for you

The effect of a probiotic depends entirely on which strain you take and what you're taking it for. Strong evidence: probiotics with Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG or Saccharomyces boulardii cut antibiotic diarrhea roughly in half. Specific strains help specific types of IBS. But the broad consumer marketing — 'gut health,' 'immunity,' 'mood' — is mostly not supported by good trials. Eat yogurt and fermented foods; reach for specific probiotic strains for specific medical reasons.

How to actually do this

If starting an antibiotic: Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG (Culturelle) 10+ billion CFU/day OR Saccharomyces boulardii (Florastor) 250-500 mg twice daily, starting day 1 of antibiotic, continuing 1 week after. For IBS-D: Lactobacillus plantarum 299v specifically. For general gut health: eat yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi — cheaper and more enjoyable. Avoid expensive multi-strain 'gut health' formulations without clinical-trial backing of the specific product.

Rough cost: $15-30/month for evidence-based single-strain products (Culturelle, Florastor). $40-80/month for multi-strain formulations of unclear benefit.

Why it works

Probiotics are live bacteria you swallow. They mostly pass through your gut without permanently moving in, but during the days you're taking them, they crowd out harmful bacteria, calm down your immune system in your gut, and produce useful chemicals. Different strains do different things — there's no general 'good probiotic.'

For the curious — technical mechanism

Mechanisms vary by strain: competitive exclusion of pathogens, modulation of tight junction proteins (claudin, occludin) to reduce intestinal permeability, secretion of bacteriocins, modulation of toll-like receptor signaling and Treg/Th17 balance, production of short-chain fatty acids (acetate, butyrate, propionate). Most strains transiently colonize without permanent engraftment — once you stop taking them, the original microbiome reasserts within ~1-2 weeks.

Evidence Summary

Strongest evidence: antibiotic-associated diarrhea prevention (Goldenberg 2017 Cochrane: probiotics reduced incidence ~37%, NNT ~13). Saccharomyces boulardii effective for C. difficile prevention (McFarland 2015 meta-analysis). Strain-specific evidence for IBS-C (Bifidobacterium infantis 35624), IBS-D (Lactobacillus plantarum 299v), and pediatric acute gastroenteritis (Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG). General-population claims about immunity, mood, longevity etc. mostly fail when tested in RCTs. The 'gut microbiome' field is exciting but most consumer probiotic marketing is far ahead of evidence.

Key Studies

Probiotics for the prevention of pediatric antibiotic-associated diarrhea ↗
Meta-analysis (33 RCTs) n=6352 2017 Cochrane Database Syst Rev
Goldenberg JZ, Yap C, Lytvyn L, Lo CK, Beardsley J, Mertz D, Johnston BC
Finding: Probiotics reduced antibiotic-associated diarrhea incidence in children from 19% to 8% (NNT ~9). Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG and Saccharomyces boulardii had strongest evidence.
Limitations: Heterogeneous strains and doses across trials. Pediatric population — adult evidence is similar magnitude in separate meta-analyses.
Use of probiotics for the prevention of Clostridium difficile diarrhea: a meta-analysis ↗
Meta-analysis (multiple RCTs) 2015 Antibiotics (Basel)
McFarland LV
Finding: Probiotics (especially Saccharomyces boulardii and Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG) reduced C. difficile-associated diarrhea by ~64% in hospitalized patients receiving antibiotics.
Limitations: Older meta-analysis. Hospital populations may not generalize to community.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Strain- and indication-specific. Antibiotic diarrhea prevention: Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG ~10 billion CFU/day OR Saccharomyces boulardii 250-500 mg twice daily, started with the antibiotic and continued 1 week after.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Antibiotic-associated diarrhea (well-established)
  • C. difficile incidence
  • IBS symptom scores (strain-specific)
  • Acute gastroenteritis duration

Side Effects

  • Mild gas/bloating in first week
  • Rare bacteremia in immunocompromised (avoid in those populations)

Contraindications / Cautions

  • Severe immunocompromise (chemotherapy, HIV with low CD4, organ transplant on heavy immunosuppression)
  • Central venous catheters (rare bacteremia risk)
  • Critical illness in ICU (avoid Saccharomyces in central-line patients)
