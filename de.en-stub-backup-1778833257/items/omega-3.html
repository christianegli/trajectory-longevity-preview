Bottom Line

If you have elevated triglycerides or established cardiovascular disease, prescription EPA at 4g works. For healthy people taking 1g fish oil for general health: probably doing little. Eat fatty fish 2x/week instead.

What this means for you

Eating fatty fish twice a week probably helps your heart and brain. Taking a basic 1-gram fish oil pill from the pharmacy is mostly waste-of-money for healthy people — the doses tested in real studies are 4–10x higher. If you have high triglycerides or heart disease, prescription-grade EPA at 4 grams is a different story and genuinely works.

How to actually do this

  • First choice: eat fatty fish twice a week — wild salmon, sardines, mackerel, herring, anchovies. This beats supplements for most people.
  • If you supplement: aim for 2g/day combined EPA+DHA, not the 300mg in cheap pills. Read the label — total grams of fish oil ≠ grams of EPA+DHA.
  • If you have high triglycerides or established heart disease: ask your doctor about prescription icosapent ethyl (Vascepa) at 4g/day. This has real RCT evidence.
  • Take with a meal that has fat (improves absorption). Refrigerate to slow oxidation.
  • Smell test: if it smells fishy/rancid, throw it out. Oxidized fish oil may be worse than no fish oil.
  • If you're on blood thinners, talk to your doctor before high doses.
Rough cost: Quality fish oil: $15–$40/month. Prescription icosapent ethyl: $300+ without insurance.

Worth considering if

People who don't eat fatty fish twice a week, anyone with elevated triglycerides, history of heart disease, or autoimmune/inflammatory conditions.

Probably skip if

Heavy fatty-fish eaters already getting 2g+ EPA/DHA from food. People on warfarin should consult their doctor.

Why it works

Omega-3 fats (EPA and DHA) get incorporated into the membranes of every cell in your body. That changes how cells signal, makes them less prone to inflammatory overdrive, and stabilizes the rhythm of your heart. Most modern diets are heavily skewed toward omega-6 fats (vegetable oils, processed food); omega-3 helps rebalance that ratio.

For the curious — technical mechanism

EPA and DHA incorporate into cell membranes, alter eicosanoid production toward less inflammatory profiles, reduce hepatic VLDL secretion (lowering triglycerides), and stabilize cardiac membrane potential. May modulate resolvins/protectins for resolution of inflammation.

Evidence Summary

Omega-3 evidence has shifted significantly in the last decade. Status (biomarker): Mozaffarian 2013 cohort: highest plasma EPA+DHA tertile had 27% lower mortality vs lowest over 16y in older adults. The 'omega-3 index' signal is real. Supplementation (RCT): VITAL 2019 (n=25K) showed no overall CV or mortality benefit of 1g/day EPA+DHA in vit-D-replete US adults. Abdelhamid 2020 Cochrane meta of 86 RCTs (n=163K) confirmed: little/no all-cause mortality benefit, modest 9% CHD-mortality reduction. Exception: REDUCE-IT (Bhatt 2019, NEJM, n=8K) — 4g/day icosapent ethyl in patients on statins with elevated triglycerides reduced major adverse CV events by 25%. High-dose, specific population, prescription. Mechanism: anti-inflammatory, triglyceride lowering, possible antiarrhythmic. Practical: if you eat fatty fish 2x/week, supplementation likely adds little. If not, 1-2g/day EPA+DHA is reasonable. High-dose icosapent ethyl is prescription-only for elevated triglycerides on statin.

Key Studies

Cardiovascular Risk Reduction with Icosapent Ethyl for Hypertriglyceridemia (REDUCE-IT) ↗
RCT n=8179 2019 NEJM
Bhatt et al.
Finding: 25% reduction in MACE in high-risk statin-treated patients with elevated TG. EPA 4g/day.
Limitations: Mineral oil placebo controversy; specific population.
Marine n-3 Fatty Acids and Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease and Cancer (VITAL) ↗
RCT n=25871 2019 NEJM
Manson et al.
Finding: 1g/day fish oil did NOT reduce major CV events or cancer over 5 years.
Limitations: Generally healthy population; low dose.
Omega-3 fatty acids for the primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular disease (Cochrane) ↗
Meta-analysis (86 RCTs) n=162796 2020 Cochrane Database
Abdelhamid et al.
Finding: Long-chain omega-3 supplementation produced little/no effect on all-cause mortality (RR 0.97, 95% CI 0.93-1.01) or CV events overall. Modest CHD-mortality benefit (~9% reduction). Earlier optimistic effect estimates have largely not held in modern trials.
Limitations: Very heterogeneous trials, doses, populations; many trials in low-omega-3-deficient populations have ceiling effects.
Plasma Phospholipid Long-Chain Omega-3 Fatty Acids and Total and Cause-Specific Mortality in Older Adults ↗
Prospective cohort n=2692 2013 Annals of Internal Medicine
Mozaffarian et al.
Finding: Highest tertile of plasma EPA+DHA had 27% lower all-cause mortality vs lowest over 16y (median 2.16 years longer life expectancy from age 65). Status biomarker, not supplement intake — supports the dietary fish signal.
Limitations: Observational; biomarker status reflects diet + biology + genetics; reverse causality possible.

Studied Dose / Protocol

1-4g/day EPA+DHA. Therapeutic: 2-4g.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • MACE
  • All-cause mortality
  • Triglycerides
  • Cognitive decline

Side Effects

  • Fishy burps
  • GI upset
  • Mild bleeding tendency at high doses
  • Atrial fibrillation signal at very high doses (debated)

Contraindications / Cautions

  • Anticoagulant therapy (caution)
  • Fish allergy
