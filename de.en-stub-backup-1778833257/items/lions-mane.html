Lion's Mane Mushroom

Edible mushroom with two small human RCTs in mild cognitive impairment. Promising but underpowered.

Bottom Line

Suggestive but unproven. Two small human RCTs, strong mechanistic plausibility, low risk profile. If you have cognitive concerns and want a low-risk addition to a diet-and-exercise foundation, it's reasonable. Don't expect dramatic effects — trial deltas were real but modest. Wait for larger replication before treating as foundational.

What this means for you

Lion's mane (Hericium erinaceus) is an edible mushroom that contains compounds called erinacines and hericenones. In cell and animal studies, these compounds increase nerve growth factor (NGF) — a protein that helps neurons survive and grow new connections. Two small human trials in older adults with mild cognitive impairment showed cognitive scores improved on lion's mane and declined again after stopping. The trials were small (~30 people each), so the data is suggestive rather than definitive. Worth knowing about; not yet a foundation.

How to actually do this

1–3 g/day of dual-extract powder (fruiting body + mycelium) or 500–1000 mg of standardized extract. Take with food. Effects in trials emerged after 8–16 weeks of continuous use; trials also showed effect disappeared 4 weeks after stopping. If you try it, plan for at least a 12-week trial.

Rough cost: $20–40/mo

Worth considering if

Adults 50+ with mild cognitive concerns, family history of dementia, or wanting low-risk cognitive support. People who prefer food-derived interventions over isolates.

Probably skip if

Pregnant or nursing (no safety data). Anyone with mushroom allergy. People on anticoagulants (theoretical bleeding risk; no trial confirmation but caution warranted). Don't expect dramatic effects — the trial deltas were modest.

Helps with

Cognitive decline

Why it works

Lion's mane contains molecules that cross the blood-brain barrier and stimulate nerve growth factor (NGF). NGF is what your brain uses to maintain and repair neurons. More NGF — at least in mice — means better learning and memory, and slower neurodegeneration. The human data is suggestive but not yet conclusive.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Hericenones (from fruiting body) and erinacines (from mycelium) are diterpenoid compounds that cross the blood-brain barrier and stimulate NGF synthesis in astrocytes via a TrkA-independent pathway. In rodent models, oral lion's mane increases hippocampal NGF expression, promotes neurite outgrowth, and improves spatial memory. Erinacine A specifically has shown protection against amyloid-β toxicity in cell culture and against MPTP-induced Parkinsonism in mice. Human bioavailability data is limited.

Evidence Summary

Two small Japanese RCTs and one open-label study form the human evidence base. Mori 2009 (Phytotherapy Research) randomised 30 older Japanese adults with mild cognitive impairment to 1g lion's mane fruiting body extract three times daily or placebo for 16 weeks. The lion's mane group showed significant improvement on the Revised Hasegawa Dementia Scale (a Japanese cognitive battery similar to MMSE), with scores returning toward baseline 4 weeks after stopping. Vigna 2019 (Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine) studied 77 overweight/obese adults with mood concerns; 8 weeks of lion's mane (1.05g/day) improved scores on anxiety, depression, and sleep questionnaires compared to placebo — effect size small-to-moderate. Saitsu 2019 (Biomedical Research) was a smaller open-label cognitive study in middle-aged Japanese adults showing improvement on Cognitrax tasks after 12 weeks. Mechanistic plausibility is strong (NGF stimulation in vitro and in mice), but the human RCTs are small, mostly Japanese, mostly using one extract type, and outcomes are cognitive scales rather than hard endpoints. Not yet at metformin/statin level of evidence; worth knowing about as a low-risk option.

Key Studies

Improving effects of the mushroom Yamabushitake (Hericium erinaceus) on mild cognitive impairment ↗
RCT, double-blind n=30 2009 Phytotherapy Research
Mori et al.
Finding: 16 weeks of lion's mane (1g x3/day) improved cognitive scores on Hasegawa Dementia Scale vs placebo in older adults with MCI. Effect disappeared 4 weeks after stopping.
Limitations: Small sample (n=30), Japanese cohort only, single extract preparation, no biomarker confirmation.
Effects of Hericium erinaceus on amyloid β(25-35) peptide-induced learning and memory deficits in mice ↗
Animal study (mice) 2011 Biomedical Research
Mori et al.
Finding: Mice fed lion's mane diet performed significantly better on Y-maze and novel object recognition after amyloid-β injection vs control. Suggests neuroprotective effect against AD-like pathology.
Limitations: Mouse study; doesn't translate directly to human dementia.
The effects of Hericium erinaceus on mood and sleep quality (Vigna 2019) ↗
RCT, double-blind n=77 2019 Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine
Vigna et al.
Finding: 8 weeks of lion's mane improved scores on anxiety/depression/sleep questionnaires (Beck Depression Inventory, Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index) vs placebo in overweight adults.
Limitations: Self-report scales; modest effect sizes; combined intervention with diet advice.
Erinacine A-enriched Hericium erinaceus mycelium ameliorates Alzheimer's disease-related pathologies in APPswe/PS1dE9 transgenic mice ↗
Animal study (mice) 2016 Journal of Biomedical Science
Tsai-Teng et al.
Finding: 30 days of erinacine A-enriched mycelium reduced amyloid plaque burden and improved spatial memory in transgenic AD mice.
Limitations: Animal model of AD; specific erinacine-A enrichment not present in most consumer products.

Studied Dose / Protocol

1g three times daily (Mori 2009, fruiting body extract); 1.05g/day (Vigna 2019)
