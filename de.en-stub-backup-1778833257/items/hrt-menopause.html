Menopausal Hormone Therapy (estrogen ± progesterone)

Started within 10 years of menopause, HRT reduces all-cause mortality ~30% and bone fractures substantially. Misunderstood for two decades after the WHI; the timing-hypothesis evidence is now strong.

Bottom Line

If you're a woman within 10 years of menopause with vasomotor symptoms or accelerated bone loss, HRT is reasonable to discuss seriously. The 2002 WHI scared a generation of clinicians and patients; the modern evidence is more nuanced. Transdermal formulations preferred when VTE risk matters. Don't start de novo >10 years past menopause without specific indication. The decision is individual; the modern guidelines (NAMS 2022 position statement, ESHRE/ECTS) support selective use.

What this means for you

Menopausal hormone therapy (HRT) was the most-prescribed medication for older women in the 1990s, then almost vanished after the 2002 Women's Health Initiative (WHI) reported small breast-cancer and CV-event signals. The story has since been substantially revised. The WHI enrolled mostly women 10+ years past menopause; subsequent analyses showed HRT *started within 10 years of menopause* (the 'timing hypothesis') has a different risk-benefit profile — including reduction in CV mortality, all-cause mortality, fractures, and possibly cognitive outcomes. The KEEPS, ELITE, and DOPS trials all support this. For most women starting HRT around the time of menopause for vasomotor symptoms, the absolute risks are small and the benefits are real. Decision is individual; the framing has changed.

How to actually do this

Discuss with a clinician familiar with the modern evidence — many primary-care providers were trained during the post-WHI period and may default to declining HRT despite updated guidelines. The North American Menopause Society and ESHRE both publish current position statements supporting selective use. Common regimens: transdermal estradiol patch (50-100 mcg/day) or estradiol gel + oral micronized progesterone (200mg/day if intact uterus, ~14 days/cycle for cyclic regimen or daily for continuous). For surgical menopause: estrogen alone (no uterus = no progesterone needed). Bioidentical compounded products are no better-evidenced than FDA-approved formulations and have less consistent dosing. Symptom-guided dose; many women do well on lower doses than originally tested.

Rough cost: $15–80/mo

Worth considering if

Women within 10 years of menopause with vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes, night sweats), genitourinary syndrome, or accelerated bone loss. Women with surgical menopause (especially under 45, where untreated estrogen deficiency causes substantial CV/bone harm). Women at high risk for osteoporotic fracture. Women whose quality of life is impaired by menopausal symptoms.

Probably skip if

Personal history of breast or endometrial cancer (modest absolute risk increase from prolonged use). History of unprovoked VTE (DVT/PE) — relative contraindication for oral; transdermal may still be considered. Active liver disease. Severe uncontrolled hypertension. Pregnancy. >10 years past menopause without prior HRT (timing-hypothesis evidence suggests less benefit, possibly more risk; case-by-case discussion). Active or recent CV disease.

Helps with

Heart disease Bone loss / osteoporosis Frailty Cognitive decline

Why it works

Estrogen does many things in women's bodies — keeps blood vessels flexible, maintains bone density, supports cognitive function in middle age, regulates mood and temperature. After menopause, estrogen drops sharply (about 95% reduction). This drop accelerates atherosclerosis, bone loss, and possibly some neurodegenerative changes. HRT replaces what's been lost. It's not anti-aging; it's preventing some predictable consequences of estrogen withdrawal.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Estrogen acts via ER-α (predominant in reproductive tissues, vascular endothelium, bone) and ER-β (predominant in brain, lung, prostate). Cardiovascular effects: improves endothelial function (NO bioavailability), reduces LDL/oxidised LDL, modestly raises HDL, reduces inflammatory markers (CRP), maintains arterial elasticity. Bone effects: directly inhibits osteoclast activity and promotes osteoblast survival. CNS effects: estrogen receptor density high in hippocampus, prefrontal cortex; supports synaptic plasticity, BDNF expression, mitochondrial function in neurons. The 'timing hypothesis' posits that estrogen is vasculoprotective in healthy endothelium but pro-inflammatory and pro-thrombotic in already-atherosclerotic vasculature — explaining why early initiation appears protective and late initiation does not. Progesterone is added for women with intact uterus to prevent estrogen-induced endometrial hyperplasia. Transdermal estrogen avoids first-pass hepatic metabolism, reducing VTE risk vs oral; modern guidelines often prefer transdermal in women with VTE risk factors.

Evidence Summary

WHI re-analysis with timing stratification: Rossouw 2007 (JAMA) re-analysed WHI subjects by age at initiation: women 50-59 at randomisation had 30% reduction in all-cause mortality (HR 0.70) and trend toward reduced CHD; women 70-79 had increased CHD risk. This timing effect emerges across multiple datasets. ELITE trial (Hodis 2016 NEJM): RCT in 643 women randomised by years-since-menopause; estradiol slowed carotid intima-media thickness progression in early-postmenopausal but not late-postmenopausal women, providing mechanistic support for timing hypothesis. KEEPS trial (Harman 2014 Annals IM): 727 healthy recently-menopausal women randomised to oral CEE, transdermal estradiol, or placebo for 4 years — neither HRT arm progressed CIMT or coronary calcium more than placebo, but both improved menopausal symptoms and bone density. DOPS trial (Schierbeck 2012 BMJ): Danish RCT of 1,006 women near menopause, randomised to HRT (oral estradiol/norethisterone) vs no treatment, 11-year follow-up + 16-year extension. Primary endpoint composite of mortality + heart failure + MI: HR 0.48 (95% CI 0.26-0.87) favouring HRT. No significant increase in cancer or stroke. WHI extended follow-up (Manson 2017 JAMA): 18-year cumulative follow-up of original WHI cohort showed no significant difference in all-cause mortality between HRT and placebo overall, but signals consistent with timing hypothesis. Bone outcomes: WHI showed 33% reduction in hip fracture even in older cohort. Cognitive outcomes: WHIMS reported increased dementia risk in WHI cohort (but population was 65+ at HRT initiation); KEEPS-Cog at 4 years and KEEPS extension at 14 years show no cognitive harm and possibly modest benefit when initiated near menopause. Breast cancer: estrogen+progestin formulations have small absolute increase (~1-2 per 1,000 women per year over 5 years); estrogen-alone shows no consistent increase and possibly small reduction in WHI extended follow-up.

Key Studies

Vascular effects of early versus late postmenopausal treatment with estradiol (ELITE trial, Hodis 2016) ↗
RCT stratified by years-since-menopause n=643 2016 New England Journal of Medicine
Hodis et al.
Finding: Oral estradiol slowed carotid intima-media thickness progression in early-postmenopausal women (within 6 years) but not in late-postmenopausal women (10+ years). Mechanistic support for timing hypothesis.
Limitations: Surrogate endpoint (CIMT) not clinical events; oral formulation only; specific subset analysis.
Effect of hormone replacement therapy on cardiovascular events in recently postmenopausal women: 11-year follow-up (DOPS trial, Schierbeck 2012) ↗
Open-label RCT, 11-year follow-up n=1006 2012 BMJ
Schierbeck et al.
Finding: HRT initiated near menopause reduced composite of mortality, heart failure, and MI by 52% (HR 0.48). No significant increase in cancer or stroke. Sustained benefit at 16-year follow-up.
Limitations: Open-label; smaller than WHI; Danish population; specific oral regimen.
Postmenopausal hormone therapy and risk of cardiovascular disease by age and years since menopause (Rossouw 2007) ↗
WHI re-analysis stratified by timing n=27347 2007 JAMA
Rossouw et al.
Finding: Women 50-59 at HRT initiation: HR 0.70 for all-cause mortality, trend toward reduced CHD. Women 70-79: increased CHD. Strong timing effect emerged from re-analysis of original WHI.
Limitations: Post-hoc subgroup analysis; original trial wasn't designed to test timing.
Menopausal Hormone Therapy and Long-term All-Cause and Cause-Specific Mortality (WHI 18-year follow-up, Manson 2017) ↗
18-year extended follow-up of WHI n=27347 2017 JAMA
Manson et al.
Finding: No statistically significant difference in all-cause mortality (HR 0.99) or cause-specific mortality between HRT and placebo over 18 years. Earlier safety signals attenuated with longer follow-up.
Limitations: Original cohort skewed older; treatment crossover; intention-to-treat may underestimate effect of sustained therapy.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Oral CEE 0.625mg/day; transdermal estradiol 50-100 mcg/day; oral micronized progesterone 200mg/day if uterus intact (cyclic or continuous regimen); various trial-specific formulations
