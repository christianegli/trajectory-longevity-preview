Magnesium

Common deficiency. Replete it. Repleted state is associated with lower mortality.

Bottom Line

If you don't eat lots of leafy greens, nuts, and beans, supplement. Glycinate or citrate at 200-400mg. Cheap insurance with broad biological plausibility.

What this means for you

Most modern diets quietly run low on magnesium because we eat fewer leafy greens, nuts, and whole grains than our grandparents did. Repleting it is cheap, broadly useful, and worth doing if you don't eat those foods regularly. People with adequate magnesium have lower mortality in cohort studies.

How to actually do this

  • First try food: leafy greens, almonds, pumpkin seeds, black beans, dark chocolate, whole grains. If you eat these daily, you may not need to supplement.
  • If supplementing: 200–400 mg of elemental magnesium per day. Take with food.
  • Pick a chelated form: glycinate (gentle, calming, good for sleep) or malate (good for daytime). Avoid oxide — poorly absorbed, just causes diarrhea.
  • Citrate is fine but has a laxative effect at higher doses; some people use it deliberately for that.
  • Take it in the evening if you find it helps sleep. Anytime works otherwise.
  • Start with 200 mg; if you get loose stools, halve the dose or switch form.
  • If you have severe kidney disease or heart block, talk to your doctor first.
Rough cost: $5–$15/month. Cheapest insurance on this site.

Worth considering if

Most adults eating a Western diet. Anyone with muscle cramps, restless sleep, anxiety, constipation, or migraines (all can have magnesium-deficiency components).

Probably skip if

People already eating lots of leafy greens, nuts, and legumes daily. Severe renal impairment without medical supervision.

Helps with

Heart disease Diabetes / metabolic Bone loss / osteoporosis Cognitive decline

Why it works

Magnesium is a workhorse mineral your body needs to do over 300 different things — making energy from food, repairing DNA, regulating blood sugar, keeping blood vessels relaxed, helping muscles contract and relax. When you're chronically low, all of those run a bit worse. The effect is more 'fewer small things go wrong' than 'one dramatic benefit.'

For the curious — technical mechanism

Cofactor for >300 enzymes, including ATP synthesis, DNA repair, glucose metabolism, and vascular tone. Modern diets low in green leafy vegetables and whole grains often deliver below RDA.

Evidence Summary

Cohort studies show inverse association of dietary/supplemental Mg with all-cause mortality, CV events, and T2DM (e.g., Fang et al. meta-analysis). RCTs show modest BP reduction, improved insulin sensitivity, and sleep quality. Forms matter: glycinate, citrate, threonate are well-absorbed; oxide is poorly absorbed.

Key Studies

Dietary magnesium intake and risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality (Fang et al. meta-analysis) ↗
Meta-analysis n=1000000 2016 BMC Medicine
Fang et al.
Finding: Each 100mg/day increase in dietary Mg associated with 22% lower HF risk, 7% lower stroke, 19% lower T2DM, 10% lower all-cause mortality.
Limitations: Observational; food-based; confounding likely.
Effects of magnesium supplementation on blood pressure (Zhang et al. meta-analysis) ↗
Meta-analysis n=2028 2016 Hypertension
Zhang et al.
Finding: Mg supplementation reduced SBP 2 mmHg and DBP 1.8 mmHg in 11 trials.
Limitations: Modest effect size.
