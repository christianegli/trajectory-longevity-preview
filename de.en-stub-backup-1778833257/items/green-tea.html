Green Tea (drinking)

Multiple Japanese cohort studies link 3+ cups/day to lower CV and dementia mortality. Different from isolated EGCG capsules.

Bottom Line

If you like it, drink it. The Japanese cohort data is unusually strong for a beverage. 3-5 cups daily is realistic, low-cost, and likely contributes to the protective effects observed in long-lived Japanese populations. Don't take EGCG isolate capsules instead — they're not the same exposure profile. Brewed tea is safer and arguably more effective.

What this means for you

Green tea is one of the few beverages with consistent population-level evidence for both cardiovascular and dementia mortality. Japanese cohort studies (where green tea drinking is common and well-documented) repeatedly show that drinking 3-5 cups/day associates with 20-30% lower cardiovascular mortality and 10-20% lower dementia incidence vs non-drinkers. The evidence is observational, not RCT — but the consistency across multiple large prospective studies puts it ahead of most foods. *This is different from EGCG capsules*, which are concentrated extracts that occasionally cause liver injury at high doses. Drinking the tea is safe and probably worth doing if you like it.

How to actually do this

Drink 3-5 cups of green tea per day, brewed from loose leaf or quality tea bags (not bottled sweetened drinks). Steep 2-3 minutes in hot but not boiling water (~75-80°C; boiling destroys catechins). Matcha gives you the most catechins per serving but costs more. Avoid sweetening — sugar undoes the cardiovascular benefit. *Don't take EGCG isolate capsules* without medical supervision (rare hepatotoxicity at >800mg/day).

Rough cost: $10–25/mo

Worth considering if

Adults with elevated cardiovascular risk, family history of dementia, or who already drink coffee and want a calmer caffeine source. Worth trying if you don't like coffee.

Probably skip if

Iron-deficiency anaemia (catechins inhibit non-heme iron absorption — drink between meals if iron is a concern). Severe anxiety with caffeine sensitivity. Anyone on warfarin (theoretical vitamin K modulation; data weak). Pregnancy: ≤2 cups/day is safe; very high intake can deplete folate.

Helps with

Heart disease Cognitive decline Diabetes / metabolic

Why it works

Green tea contains a unique catechin called EGCG, plus theanine (a calming amino acid), and modest caffeine. The catechins are antioxidants that get into your bloodstream and may protect blood vessels and neurons. Theanine smooths out the caffeine — green tea gives you alertness without the jitter many people get from coffee. The combined effect on long-term cardiovascular and brain health appears to be real, even if the per-cup effect is small.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Major bioactive compounds: epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG, ~50% of total catechins), epicatechin, epigallocatechin, plus L-theanine and ~30mg caffeine per cup. EGCG inhibits matrix metalloproteinases, modulates NF-κB inflammation, and has direct vascular effects via eNOS-mediated NO production. L-theanine crosses the blood-brain barrier and increases alpha-wave activity, dampening the peak cortisol response to caffeine. EGCG bioavailability is poor when taken alone but improves with food (and dramatically with vitamin C). Plasma concentrations from 3-5 cups/day reach low-µM range, sufficient for cardiovascular but possibly subtherapeutic for direct neuronal effects — explaining why isolated high-dose EGCG capsules aren't necessarily better.

Evidence Summary

The strongest evidence base comes from Japanese prospective cohorts. Kuriyama 2006 (JAMA) followed 40,530 Japanese adults age 40-79 for up to 11 years. Compared to <1 cup/day, those drinking ≥5 cups/day had hazard ratios of 0.84 for all-cause mortality and 0.74 for CV mortality (women showed stronger effects than men). Tomata 2016 (American Journal of Clinical Nutrition) followed 13,645 Japanese adults age ≥65 for up to 5.7 years and found dose-dependent reduction in incident dementia: HR 0.86 for 1-2 cups/day, 0.80 for 3-4 cups/day, 0.74 for ≥5 cups/day vs <1 cup/day. Mineharu 2011 (Journal of Epidemiology and Community Health) showed similar CV protection. The Hisayama Study (2014, follow-up to 1,500+ residents over decades) confirmed lower dementia incidence in green tea drinkers. RCT evidence is shorter-term: Hartley 2013 (Cochrane Review) of 11 RCTs (n=821) found small reductions in LDL cholesterol (-7 mg/dL) and systolic BP (-3 mmHg) with 4-12 weeks of green tea. The data is observational at the long-term endpoint level, but consistently directional across multiple large cohorts and biologically plausible from the short-term RCTs. Tea, not extract: the Mazzanti 2009 case series and FDA hepatotoxicity reports involve isolated EGCG ≥600-800mg/day, not brewed tea consumption.

Key Studies

Green tea consumption and mortality due to cardiovascular disease, cancer, and all causes in Japan: the Ohsaki study ↗
Prospective cohort n=40530 2006 JAMA
Kuriyama et al.
Finding: ≥5 cups/day vs <1 cup/day: HR 0.84 for all-cause mortality, 0.74 for CV mortality over 11 years. Effect stronger in women (HR 0.69 CV mortality).
Limitations: Observational; potential confounding by lifestyle factors; Japanese diet/culture may not transfer.
Green tea consumption and the risk of incident dementia in elderly Japanese: the Ohsaki Cohort ↗
Prospective cohort n=13645 2016 American Journal of Clinical Nutrition
Tomata et al.
Finding: Dose-response reduction in incident dementia: HR 0.74 for ≥5 cups/day vs <1 cup/day over 5.7 years. Trend significant.
Limitations: Observational; dementia ascertainment via long-term care insurance records; confounding by overall lifestyle.
Green and black tea for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease ↗
Cochrane meta-analysis of 11 RCTs n=821 2013 Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews
Hartley et al.
Finding: Green tea reduced LDL cholesterol by ~7 mg/dL and systolic BP by ~3 mmHg over 4-12 weeks. Modest but consistent.
Limitations: Short trials; surrogate endpoints; heterogeneous doses (mostly extract not brewed).
Habitual coffee, tea, and caffeine consumption, circulating metabolites, and the risk of cardiometabolic outcomes (UK Biobank, 2024) ↗
Prospective cohort, UK Biobank n=209926 2024 European Journal of Preventive Cardiology
Long et al.
Finding: Tea drinkers (mostly black tea) showed dose-dependent lower all-cause mortality (HR 0.91 for 2-3 cups/day, 0.88 for 4-5 cups/day) over 11.2 years. Adds Western evidence to Japanese green-tea findings.
Limitations: UK cohort drinks mostly black tea, not green; effect sizes smaller than Japanese green-tea studies.

Studied Dose / Protocol

3-5 cups (240ml each) per day in cohort studies; 4-12 weeks at varying doses in short-term RCTs
