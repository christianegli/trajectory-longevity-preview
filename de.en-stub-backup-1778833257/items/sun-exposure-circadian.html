Sun Exposure / Circadian Alignment

Daily morning sunlight anchors circadian rhythm. UV trade-off: balance D production with skin cancer risk.

Bottom Line

Get bright light in your eyes within an hour of waking. Get some skin sun without burning. The 'sun-cancer' frame missed the all-cause picture.

What this means for you

Get bright light in your eyes within an hour of waking up. That single habit anchors your body's daily rhythm — better sleep at night, sharper alertness during the day, more stable mood. The skin cancer warnings about sun exposure are real but often overshoot: total sun avoidance has its own mortality cost. Aim for moderate, not zero.

How to actually do this

  • Within 30–60 minutes of waking: get outside for 5–15 minutes. Cloudy still works (cloudy outdoors is brighter than indoor).
  • Don't wear sunglasses for that morning window — your eyes need the light. Hat is fine.
  • On dark winter mornings: a 10,000-lux SAD lamp (look for 'bright light therapy') is a reasonable substitute — 15–30 min while you have coffee.
  • During the day: get bright light periodically. A walk at lunch is better than coffee.
  • Skin sun: 10–20 min of midday sun on arms and legs without sunscreen, a few times a week, before SPF goes on. Don't burn — burning is what drives most skin cancer risk.
  • Avoid bright light in the 2–3 hours before bed. Dim your environment, use warm-tinted bulbs, dim screens.
  • If you're on photosensitizing meds (some antibiotics, retinoids, etc.) be cautious — check with your pharmacist.
Rough cost: Free (going outside). $50–$200 for a SAD lamp if you need indoor backup.

Worth considering if

Everyone. Especially indoor workers, anyone with poor sleep, anyone with seasonal affective patterns, shift workers.

Probably skip if

Photosensitivity disorders. On photosensitizing medications without skin protection. Pale skin types should still get sun but more cautiously.

Helps with

Cognitive decline Diabetes / metabolic Other neurodegenerative Bone loss / osteoporosis

Why it works

Your brain has a master clock that needs a daily light signal to stay synchronized — without it, sleep timing, hormone release, and even gene expression in every cell drift out of phase. Morning bright light (especially direct sunlight, 10,000+ lux) is by far the strongest 'reset' signal. The window matters: light in the first hour after waking sets that day's rhythm and the timing of melatonin release 14–16 hours later. Skin sun exposure is a separate issue — moderate, sub-burning exposure has all-cause mortality benefits in cohort studies; aggressive avoidance loses some of that.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Morning bright light exposure (10,000+ lux) is the strongest zeitgeber for the suprachiasmatic nucleus, locking circadian rhythm. Drives melatonin onset 14–16h later, supports cortisol awakening, alertness.

Evidence Summary

Circadian alignment is mechanistically central to metabolism, sleep, mood, immunity. Lindqvist et al. (Sweden cohort) showed sun-avoiders had ~2x higher all-cause mortality than sun-exposers — consistent with vitamin D, circadian, or other UV-related mechanisms. Counter-balance: skin cancer risk requires UV protection in midday peak. Morning + late afternoon sun, midday sunscreen.

Studied Dose / Protocol

10-30 min morning sun (without sunglasses) daily; midday sun in moderation with skin protection.
