Protein after 50: age, sex, muscle, and the grams that actually matter

#nutrition#protein#sarcopenia

Protein is usually discussed in two unhelpful ways: gym culture treats it like a muscle-growth product, and public-health nutrition treats it like something most people already get enough of.

For longevity, neither frame is enough.

The useful question is: how much protein preserves strength, lean mass, bone, recovery, and independence as the body changes?

The short version

For most adults:

  • Under 50, sedentary, weight-stable: 0.8-1.2 g/kg/day is usually enough.
  • Training regularly: 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day is the sports-nutrition range.
  • Over 50: 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day is the practical longevity range.
  • Losing weight: lean toward 1.6 g/kg/day, sometimes higher if already lean and lifting.
  • Older, ill, injured, or recovering: 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day is commonly recommended unless medically restricted.
  • Advanced kidney disease: do not self-prescribe high protein; this is clinician territory.

The most useful daily target for many adults over 50 is:

1.2-1.6 g protein per kg body weight per day, split into 3-4 meals of 25-40 g.

Why age changes the target

Younger muscle responds strongly to smaller protein doses. Older muscle becomes less sensitive to the amino-acid signal, especially leucine. This is often called anabolic resistance.

That means the same small breakfast that was "fine" at 30 may be under-dosed at 65.

The PROT-AGE Study Group recommends at least 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day for healthy adults over 65, and 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day for many older adults with acute or chronic disease. ESPEN geriatric guidance similarly uses at least 1.0 g/kg/day as a floor for older adults.

Trajectory's practical interpretation: do not treat 0.8 g/kg/day as the target after midlife. Treat it as a deficiency-avoidance floor.

Why sex changes the context, not the biology

The per-kg protein target is not wildly different for men and women. What changes is the life context.

Men often need more total grams because they are larger. A 90 kg man at 1.4 g/kg needs 126 g/day.

Women often face sharper transition points:

  • Pregnancy and lactation change protein needs.
  • Perimenopause and menopause accelerate changes in body composition.
  • Estrogen decline worsens the muscle/bone/fat-distribution equation.
  • Women are more likely to undereat protein at breakfast and during calorie restriction.
  • Osteoporosis risk makes the protein + calcium + vitamin D + resistance-training stack more important.

For postmenopausal women, the biggest mistake is trying to solve body-composition change with calorie restriction alone. That often lowers scale weight while sacrificing lean mass.

The better frame is:

protein adequacy + heavy resistance training + fiber-rich cardiometabolic diet.

Protein during weight loss

Weight loss is a muscle-loss risk unless you defend lean mass.

Higher protein helps because it:

  • improves satiety,
  • raises the thermic effect of food,
  • preserves lean mass during energy deficit,
  • supports training recovery,
  • makes the diet less snack-driven.

If someone is losing weight after 50, the target usually shifts upward:

1.6 g/kg/day is a reasonable default, paired with resistance training.

For people who are already lean and training hard, sports-nutrition literature sometimes goes higher during aggressive cuts. That is not the average longevity reader. It is a niche athletic context.

Per meal matters

Total daily protein matters most, but distribution matters more with age.

Many people eat:

  • breakfast: 8 g,
  • lunch: 20 g,
  • dinner: 55 g.

That may hit the daily total but under-stimulates muscle protein synthesis earlier in the day.

Better:

  • breakfast: 25-40 g,
  • lunch: 25-40 g,
  • dinner: 25-40 g,
  • optional snack/shake if needed.

For older adults, about 0.4 g/kg per meal is a useful target. A 75 kg person would aim for roughly 30 g per meal.

Animal vs plant protein

Animal proteins are usually denser in leucine and essential amino acids. That does not make plant-based diets weak; it means they require planning.

High-quality plant options:

  • tofu,
  • tempeh,
  • edamame,
  • soy milk,
  • legumes plus grains,
  • seitan,
  • pea/rice protein blends,
  • higher-protein pastas,
  • nuts and seeds as supporting foods, not main protein.

For vegan diets, the common failure mode is not morality. It is arithmetic. You need enough total protein, enough leucine-rich meals, B12, iodine, calcium, vitamin D, omega-3 planning, and enough calories.

The kidney caveat

For healthy kidneys, higher protein in the ranges above is generally not treated as kidney-damaging.

But if someone has chronic kidney disease, especially eGFR below 30, protein targets can change. Some people need restriction; dialysis patients often need more. This is not a place for generic internet advice.

Rule:

Normal kidney function: protein adequacy is usually safe. Known kidney disease: individualize.

What this means practically

Pick your lane:

Adult under 50, not training

Use 0.8-1.2 g/kg/day. Focus more on food quality, fiber, ApoB, blood pressure, and exercise.

Adult under 50, training

Use 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day if muscle gain, performance, or recovery matters.

Adult 50+

Use 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day. Lift 2-3 times per week. Put 25-40 g protein at breakfast.

Postmenopausal woman

Use 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day, often toward the higher end if losing weight. Combine with heavy resistance training and bone-health basics.

Weight loss

Use about 1.6 g/kg/day and lift. Do not let the diet produce a smaller, weaker version of you.

Vegan or mostly plant-based

Use the same targets, but plan harder. Soy, legumes, seitan, and plant protein powders are tools, not cheating.

Kidney disease

Ask the clinician managing the kidney disease. Do not follow generic high-protein advice.
