New trial readout — creatine and cognitive performance in older adults

A new 24-week RCT in adults 60+ shows modest but real improvements in working memory and processing speed with 5g/day creatine. Effect was concentrated in vegetarians and sleep-deprived participants — consistent with the dietary-baseline hypothesis.

#creatine#cognitive#supplements#trial

The recent 24-week randomized trial of CreatineT1E 5C 9 in 220 adults aged 60–75 lands roughly where the meta-analytic literature predicted: modest, statistically reliable cognitive benefit, concentrated in subgroups with low baseline creatine availability.

What was tested

  • Population: 220 community-dwelling adults, 60–75 years old, no diagnosed cognitive impairment
  • Intervention: 5g creatine monohydrate daily vs placebo, 24 weeks
  • Primary endpoint: Change in working memory composite (Cambridge Brain Sciences battery)
  • Secondary endpoints: Processing speed, executive function, mood, fatigue
  • Stratification: Diet pattern (omnivore / vegetarian / vegan), self-reported sleep quality

What it found

Whole-cohort effects (small):

  • Working memory: +0.18 SD vs placebo, p = 0.04
  • Processing speed: +0.14 SD vs placebo, p = 0.07 (trend)
  • Executive function: no change
  • Mood: no change

Subgroup effects (where the action was):

  • Vegetarians/vegans: working memory +0.42 SD, processing speed +0.31 SD — both clearly significant
  • Self-reported short sleepers (<6.5h): working memory +0.36 SD
  • Omnivorous + good sleepers: essentially null

Why this matches the underlying biology

Creatine is synthesized endogenously and consumed in meat. Vegetarians and vegans run chronically low total body creatine — typically 25–40% below omnivores by muscle biopsy. They have the most "headroom" to benefit from supplementation.

Sleep deprivation depletes brain creatine pools. Anyone running a sleep deficit — see Sleep (7-9h, regularity, deep+REM)T1E 8C 8 — is likely to experience more cognitive benefit from supplementation than someone who's well-slept.

The corollary: omnivores who sleep well have less to gain. Not zero, but less.

How this changes the recommendation

The wizard already weighted creatine toward older adults and people with frailty/performance goals. We're updating the rationale text to add:

  1. Vegetarians and vegans of any age are now flagged as higher-priority candidates for creatine, regardless of athletic context.
  2. Cognitive concerns in short sleepers become a creatine indication while sleep is being fixed.
  3. Cognitive improvements typically take 8–24 weeks, which is longer than the muscle/strength response (3–6 weeks). Setting expectations correctly matters.

What we're not changing

  • The dose: still 5g/day monohydrate. The expensive forms (HCl, buffered, etc.) have no advantage and worse evidence.
  • The safety profile: 30 years of clean data on creatine in healthy adults remains the case.
  • The recommendation against the loading phase: redundant for steady users.

The contraindication for chronic kidney disease still stands — creatine raises creatinine independent of any actual kidney effect, but it complicates monitoring.

Bottom line

Creatine moves up our priority list for vegetarians, vegans, and short sleepers with cognitive concerns. It stays where it is for everyone else: a low-cost, well-tolerated, evidence-backed addition for adults 50+ who are training, particularly alongside Resistance TrainingT1E 7C 9 and Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)T1E 5C 7.
