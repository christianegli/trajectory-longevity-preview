Is X worth taking? — five questions before any new supplement

#methodology#supplements#framework

Every week brings a new supplement claiming longevity benefits. Most won't show up in our catalogue — we cover ~89 interventions and there are thousands of products marketed as "anti-aging." This post is the framework we use internally to decide whether something deserves catalogue space, and you can use it for any supplement you're personally considering.

Question 1: Is there a randomised controlled trial in humans?

Not a mouse study. Not a cell-culture study. Not a population observational survey that "associated" the supplement with longer life. Not a manufacturer-sponsored white paper.

A randomised controlled trial in humans, ideally with the actual product or compound at the dose being marketed.

If the answer is "no" — pause. Almost everything works in mice. Most things work in cell culture. Many compounds correlate with health in observational data because healthy people use more supplements. None of those tell you whether adding the supplement to your routine will help you.

The best evidence is convergent: multiple RCTs, ideally with hard endpoints (mortality, MI, fractures, cognitive decline) rather than surrogate markers. The next-best is a single large RCT with a real clinical endpoint. The minimum acceptable is at least one RCT in humans showing the relevant biological effect at a relevant dose.

If you can't find one — the supplement is hypothesis, not evidence.

Question 2: What's the effect size, and is it clinically meaningful?

A randomised trial that's "statistically significant" doesn't mean the effect is large. Many trials find effects that are real but tiny — and tiny effects rarely justify the cost, the time, the side effects, or the false sense of doing something useful.

Look for: how much does the marker move? How much does the actual outcome change? Is the absolute risk reduction more than 1 percentage point? Are the effect sizes consistent with what your existing interventions accomplish?

A statin reduces ApoB by 30-55%. Cocoa flavanols reduced CV mortality by 27% in COSMOS. Mediterranean diet reduced major CV events by 30% in PREDIMED. These are the kinds of effect sizes that deserve attention.

A supplement that reduced CRP by 0.2 mg/L in a 12-week trial isn't moving the needle on anything that matters. A supplement that improved a self-reported quality-of-life score by 5 points on a 100-point scale isn't either. The numbers should be substantial before you commit time and money.

Question 3: Does the trial use the form, dose, and duration you can replicate?

This is where most consumer supplements fall apart. The trial used 800mg of a specific patented extract; the bottle on the shelf has 200mg of a generic version. The trial dosed for 12 weeks; the bottle has 30 capsules. The trial used a sustained-release form; the supplement is regular.

Specific examples this matters:

  • EGCG / green tea extract: Beneficial trials use specific catechin-rich extracts at 200-300mg/day. Many supplements have variable catechin content; some have hepatotoxicity reports at the high doses needed for any benefit.
  • Curcumin: Plain curcumin is poorly absorbed. Trials use specific bioavailable formulations (with piperine, in liposomes, as phytosomes). Random "turmeric capsules" at the supermarket don't replicate the trial.
  • Omega-3: Effect size depends on EPA dose. Most consumer fish oils have 30% EPA; the REDUCE-IT trial used 99% pure icosapent ethyl at 4g/day. These are not interchangeable.
  • Probiotics: Effects are strain-specific and species-specific. "Probiotic for gut health" tells you essentially nothing — the relevant question is which strains, at what CFU count, in what delivery system.

If the supplement you're considering uses a different form, dose, or duration than the trial, you don't know what to expect. The default assumption: the trial doesn't apply.

Question 4: What's the safety profile?

Two parts:

Acute side effects. GI upset, headaches, sleep disturbance, etc. Read the label, read user reports. Many "natural" supplements have annoying-but-not-dangerous side effects in 10-30% of users.

Drug interactions. Many supplements interact with prescription medications:

  • Curcumin + anticoagulants → increased bleeding risk
  • St John's Wort + many drugs → reduced drug levels (CYP3A4 induction)
  • Garlic supplements + anticoagulants → increased bleeding risk
  • High-dose vitamin K + warfarin → unpredictable INR
  • 5-HTP + SSRIs → serotonin syndrome risk
  • High-dose vitamin E + statins → questionable interaction effects

If you're on prescription medications, run any new supplement past your prescriber. Most pharmacists will flag interactions if you ask.

Hepatotoxicity is the supplement-specific safety issue most people don't consider. Many "natural" compounds at the doses needed for benefit have liver toxicity signals. Examples include high-dose green tea extracts, some traditional Chinese medicine preparations, kava, and a long list of weight-loss supplements. Pretty much any supplement that promises substantial effects through the liver may carry liver risk.

Manufacturing quality matters for supplements in a way it doesn't for prescription medications. The FDA doesn't pre-approve supplement contents. Independent testing has found contaminants, undisclosed ingredients, and inaccurate dosing in 20-50% of supplements depending on category. Use products with third-party certification (NSF, USP, ConsumerLab) when possible.

Question 5: Are the basics in place first?

This is the hardest question because the answer is often "no."

The intervention ladder for almost any longevity goal starts with: don't smoke, exercise (resistance + cardio), Mediterranean dietary pattern, 7-8 hours sleep, manage stress, social connection, sunlight. These aren't supplements; they're behaviours. They produce 70-80% of available risk reduction for almost every chronic disease.

If you're skipping any of these and reaching for supplements as the alternative — you're optimising the 5% layer while ignoring the 75% layer. The supplement isn't dangerous, but the opportunity cost is. Time spent researching the latest nootropic could be time spent doing two more strength sessions per week. The strength sessions will move your trajectory; the nootropic probably won't.

If the basics are in place, then targeted supplements (omega-3 if EPA low, vitamin D if winter, creatine for muscle, fibre if dietary fibre low, etc.) make sense as compounding additions. They're not the foundation; they're the polish.

Putting the framework together

Five questions, in order:

  1. Is there a human RCT? No → stop.
  2. Is the effect size meaningful? No → unlikely worth your time.
  3. Does the supplement match the trial form/dose? No → trial may not apply.
  4. Is it safe given your situation? Check interactions, hepatotoxicity, third-party testing.
  5. Are the basics in place? No → fix those first, supplement second.

Most "longevity supplements" fail at question 1 or 2. Of the survivors, many fail at 3 (consumer formulation ≠ trial formulation). Of those that pass 1-3, many should still wait until 5 is satisfied.

This framework is restrictive. That's intentional. The result of being restrictive about supplements is more time, attention, and money for the things that actually move the needle: the basics, the well-validated medications when needed, the targeted compounds with clear evidence and clear formulations.

When in doubt, the right answer is usually "skip it." The list of supplements that have made population-level differences to health and longevity is short — and most of the time they're already on someone's catalogue, ours included. New "miracle" compounds usually fade.
