Resistance training — what to actually do

#exercise#resistance-training#sarcopenia#bone-health

The single biggest gap between the longevity literature and what most people actually do is resistance training. The literature is unambiguous: muscle mass, strength, and bone density at 50 predict your function and mortality at 70+ better than most blood markers. The cardio-only crowd is doing about half the work.

Here's a specific protocol that's evidence-supported, time-efficient, and works at every age including 70+.

What "evidence-supported" means in practice

Two trials worth knowing about specifically:

LIFTMOR (Watson 2018) — 101 postmenopausal women with low bone mass, randomised to a high-intensity resistance + impact protocol vs low-intensity exercise control. After 8 months: lumbar spine BMD up 2.9% in the intervention group (vs declining in controls), femoral neck BMD up 0.3% (vs decline), and most importantly, no increase in fractures. The protocol involved deadlifts, squats, overhead presses, and impact training (jumping). Twice a week, 30 minutes per session, supervised. This trial broke the long-standing fear that heavy lifting was unsafe in osteoporosis — done correctly with progressive loading and supervision, it's actually the most effective bone-density intervention available.

Norwegian Senior Strength Trial (Aagaard, multiple papers) — older adults (60+) doing 3x/week heavy resistance training showed gains in maximal strength of 30-50% over 12-16 weeks, plus power gains, plus measurable improvements in functional measures (chair-stand, gait speed, stair-climb). Even adults in their 80s showed significant strength gains.

The point: it works at every age. Including yours.

The minimum viable protocol

If you do nothing else, do this. Two sessions per week, 45-60 minutes each. Five movement patterns, in this order:

1. Squat pattern. Goblet squat, back squat, front squat, or leg press. 3 sets of 5-8 reps at challenging weight. Last set should be 1-2 reps from failure.

2. Hinge pattern. Conventional deadlift, Romanian deadlift, trap bar deadlift, or kettlebell swing. 3 sets of 5-8 reps. Same intensity as squat.

3. Push pattern. Bench press (barbell, dumbbell, or machine), overhead press, push-up. 3 sets of 5-10 reps.

4. Pull pattern. Row (barbell, dumbbell, machine, or cable), pull-up, lat pulldown. 3 sets of 5-10 reps.

5. Carry / loaded gait. Farmer's walk with heavy dumbbells, suitcase carry. 2 sets of 30-40m. This trains grip strength, core stability, and basic functional carrying capacity — all of which independently predict mortality in older adults.

That's 11 sets total per session. Done in 45-60 minutes including warm-up and rest. Twice a week.

Progress tracking that matters

Two numbers to track:

Weight on the bar / load lifted. Write it down every session. The single biggest predictor of long-term progress is whether you can demonstrate you're lifting more in 6 months than you are now. 5-10% increases in load every 4-6 weeks is realistic for most adults, faster early on.

Reps in reserve (RIR). On your last set, how many reps could you have done? Target: 1-3 RIR. If you're doing 8 reps and could do 15, the weight is too light. If you're hitting failure on every set, the weight is too heavy and your recovery will suffer.

You don't need a coach (though one helps). You don't need a gym (though one helps). You need a way to add load progressively. Bands, dumbbells, kettlebells, barbells — any of these work if you can incrementally increase the resistance.

Common mistakes

Doing isolation exercises and skipping compounds. A bicep curl does almost nothing for the longevity-relevant goals (muscle mass, strength, bone density). A deadlift does a lot. The big movements train more muscle, more neurological coordination, and more bone-loading per unit of time. Build the program around them.

Lifting too light forever. Most people who say "I do resistance training" do it at intensity that wouldn't qualify in any of the studies. The trials use loads that produce noticeable strain — 70-85% of your maximum. If you can do 15+ reps with the weight you're using, it's not heavy enough to drive the adaptations.

Avoiding heavy lifting after 50. This is wrong on the science and on the safety. Older adults respond to resistance training like younger adults respond — sometimes faster on a percentage basis. The fear of injury is reasonable but addressed by good form, progressive loading, and supervision early on. The injury risk of being weak at 75 is much higher than the injury risk of training properly at 55.

Inconsistent volume. Two sessions per week, every week, for years, beats four sessions per week for two months and then nothing. The adaptation window is forever; the consistency is what produces the effects.

Skipping the leg work. Lower-body strength is more predictive of mortality than upper-body strength in most cohort studies. The squat and the hinge are the most important exercises in this protocol. Do them.

Skipping the carry. Grip strength is one of the most consistent mortality predictors in epidemiology. Farmer's walks, suitcase carries, and dead hangs build grip in a way that bicep curls don't. Two minutes of heavy carries at the end of each session is plenty.

What about cardio?

This protocol doesn't replace cardio. The complete longevity exercise stack is:

  • 2 sessions/week resistance training (above)
  • 3-4 hours/week Zone 2 cardio (conversational pace)
  • 1-2 sessions/week high-intensity intervals (Norwegian 4x4 or similar)
  • 8,000-10,000 daily steps as a baseline

That's 6-8 hours of intentional movement per week. Less than most people spend on streaming services. The mortality data is unambiguous — this combination produces measurable life-expectancy gains.

If you have to choose two: resistance training + Zone 2 walks. That covers the most ground.

What if I'm already 70

Start. Slower, with supervision, with lower starting loads. Bodyweight squats progressing to weighted. Resistance band rows progressing to dumbbells. The Norwegian trial enrolled adults up to 86 and saw measurable gains.

A trial that's worth knowing about: the LIFE study (Pahor 2014) randomised older adults (mean age 79) with mobility limitations to structured physical activity vs health education. The structured-activity arm had 28% lower risk of major mobility disability at 2.6 years. This wasn't even heavy lifting — it was supervised walking + balance + strength. Even the lightest version of this protocol works.

What if I have an injury

Modify, don't skip. Most people with knee osteoarthritis can still hinge, push, pull, and carry — just not squat heavy. People with shoulder issues can still squat, hinge, pull, carry. Frozen shoulder doesn't mean no exercise; it means careful exercise selection.

A physiotherapist who's worked with athletes is the right resource. Most physiotherapists trained in the 2010s+ understand that strength training is rehabilitation, not contradiction.

The summary

Two sessions per week. Five movement patterns per session. Track load and reps in reserve. Progress over months and years. The program works at every age. The reasons people give for not doing it (too old, too risky, too time-consuming, don't know how) are mostly wrong on the evidence.

If you're going to do one thing this month after reading this, start a 2-day-per-week program with the five patterns above. Light first session. Add load weekly. By month 3, you'll be lifting meaningfully more than month 1, and the effect compounds for the rest of your life.
