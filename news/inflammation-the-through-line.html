Inflammation: the through-line that explains half the catalogue

#inflammation#mechanism#philosophy#methodology

If you read the catalogue end to end, you'll notice that the same word keeps appearing in the mechanism section: inflammation. Statins lower it. Omega-3 lowers it. Exercise lowers it. Sleep lowers it. Fibre lowers it. Losing weight lowers it. Even some of our T1 vaccines lower it indirectly by reducing the infections that cause inflammatory spikes.

This is not coincidence. It is also not a marketing story. It is the closest thing biology has to a unifying theme for "things that go wrong as you age," and it deserves a careful explanation — including where the story breaks down.

What "inflammation" actually means here

In medicine, inflammation covers two very different things:

  1. Acute inflammation. The body's response to injury or infection. Redness, swelling, heat, pain. This is good. Without it you die from a paper cut.
  1. Chronic, low-grade inflammation. A persistent baseline elevation of inflammatory signalling proteins — interleukin-6 (IL-6), tumour necrosis factor (TNF-α), C-reactive protein (CRP) — without an active infection or wound. This is what longevity science cares about. The shorthand is inflammaging.

The first one saves your life. The second one slowly damages tissues over decades. The interventions in our catalogue almost never affect the first. They affect the second.

Why inflammaging matters

Three lines of evidence connect chronic inflammation to mortality:

Observational. People with high baseline CRP have higher rates of heart disease, dementia, cancer, and overall mortality. The Cardiovascular Health Study and the JUPITER trial cohort both showed CRP independently predicting events even after controlling for cholesterol, blood pressure, and smoking.

Mechanistic. Inflammatory cytokines damage endothelial cells (linings of blood vessels), accelerate atherosclerosis, promote insulin resistance, disrupt the blood-brain barrier, and create a cellular environment where damaged cells fail to die properly (senescence). This isn't speculative — these pathways are mapped in detail.

Interventional. The CANTOS trial (Ridker et al, NEJM 2017) tested an antibody (canakinumab) that blocks IL-1β specifically. It reduced cardiovascular events in people who already had a heart attack and high CRP. No effect on cholesterol — pure inflammation reduction, real outcome benefit. This is the cleanest evidence we have that targeting inflammation directly extends life in humans.

CANTOS also showed a side effect that should keep enthusiasm calibrated: fatal infections went up. You can't fully suppress inflammation without weakening defence. The therapeutic window is narrow.

Why so many catalogue items lower inflammation

The interventions with the strongest mortality data tend to do many things at once. Inflammation reduction is usually one of them, even when it isn't the headline mechanism.

Statins were designed to lower LDL. They do, and that lowers events. But statins also lower CRP by ~30%, and the JUPITER trial showed event reduction in people with normal LDL but elevated CRP — suggesting the inflammatory effect contributes independently. The CTT meta-analysis bundles both into a single survival benefit.

Omega-3 at meaningful doses (≥2 g EPA+DHA daily) lowers triglycerides, modestly lowers blood pressure, and reduces a panel of inflammatory markers. The REDUCE-IT trial showed event reduction at high-dose icosapent ethyl that wasn't fully explained by the modest triglyceride change — inflammation is the leading explanation.

Exercise acutely raises inflammation (the work itself stresses tissue), but chronic regular exercise lowers baseline CRP, IL-6, and TNF-α by 20–40%. This is one reason the cardiorespiratory fitness mortality curve is so steep. Mandsager 2018 found a 5x mortality difference between top and bottom fitness quartiles — bigger than any drug.

Sleep. A single night of <6 hours raises next-day CRP. Chronic sleep deprivation maintains a higher inflammatory baseline. Sleep is the cheapest anti-inflammatory in the catalogue and the one most often neglected.

Fibre / fermented foods. Soluble fibre feeds gut bacteria that produce short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate). These directly suppress NF-κB signalling — a master regulator of inflammation. The mechanism runs through the gut, not the bloodstream.

Weight loss (especially visceral fat). Visceral adipose tissue isn't a passive storage depot. It actively secretes IL-6 and TNF-α. Losing 5–10% body weight measurably lowers systemic inflammation. This is why GLP-1 medications appear to have benefits beyond glucose control.

Mediterranean diet. PREDIMED 2013 showed cardiovascular event reduction; later analyses found CRP was lower in the intervention group. The diet's anti-inflammatory profile (olive oil polyphenols, nuts, fish, low ultra-processed) is consistent with that finding.

Where the story breaks

Now the cautions, because this is where pop-science gets it wrong.

"Anti-inflammatory" supplements often don't measurably lower inflammation. Curcumin, resveratrol, NAD+ precursors — the marketing claims anti-inflammatory effects. The human RCT data is thin to absent. CRP doesn't move meaningfully in well-conducted trials. This is partly why we score these items low on certainty.

Lower CRP ≠ longer life automatically. Aspirin lowers CRP and reduces cardiovascular events but increases bleeding risk; the net effect for primary prevention in low-risk people is roughly neutral or negative (ASPREE 2018). The arrow from "inflammation marker down" to "life extended" requires a clinically validated intervention, not just a number changing.

Acute infections need inflammation. This is the CANTOS lesson at consumer scale. Heavy NSAID use, chronic immunosuppression, anything that broadly dampens inflammation has real costs. Vaccines work with inflammation, briefly amplifying it to build immunity. The goal is not "less inflammation." It is "less unnecessary inflammation."

Some inflammation is what kills cancer cells, repairs micro-tears, and clears damaged proteins from the brain. Total suppression isn't the goal. Calibration is.

Genetic variants matter. Some people have constitutively elevated CRP from genetic variants (CRP gene polymorphisms) that don't carry the same risk as inflammation from lifestyle or disease. CRP is a marker, not a destiny.

What this means practically

If you're trying to lower chronic inflammation as a longevity strategy, the evidence-weighted prescription is boring and overlapping:

  1. Move regularly. Especially anything that gets your heart rate up. This does more for inflammation than any supplement.
  2. Sleep ≥7 hours most nights. Single biggest lever for a lot of people.
  3. Eat fibre. Whole grains, legumes, vegetables. Feeds the gut bacteria that produce butyrate.
  4. Lose visceral fat if you have it. Not all fat is equal; the belly fat is the inflammatory engine.
  5. Treat ApoB / cholesterol if elevated. Statins are anti-inflammatory in addition to lipid-lowering.
  6. Get vaccines, especially flu and shingles. Each prevented infection is a prevented inflammatory cascade.
  7. Avoid smoking, limit alcohol, manage stress. All raise baseline inflammation chronically.

If you're considering an "anti-inflammatory supplement," ask whether a human RCT shows it lowering CRP at a meaningful dose — and whether it's been linked to actual mortality or event endpoints. Most won't pass the second test.

The honest summary

Inflammation isn't a unified theory of aging. It is a useful organising framework for understanding why so many disparate interventions converge on similar outcomes. Cholesterol matters. Glucose matters. Blood pressure matters. They all matter partly because they all interact with the inflammatory state.

You don't need to track CRP religiously. You need to do the boring things that lower it as a side effect: move, sleep, eat fibre, manage weight, treat metabolic disease, get vaccines. The catalogue exists to help you choose among the boring things rationally.

When something flashy comes along claiming to "fight inflammation" — ask what RCT showed reduced events, not just a marker dropping. The first is rare. The second is everywhere.
