ApoB — the one CV number worth knowing if you only know one

#methodology#labs#cardiovascular

If you could keep one number for predicting whether you'll have a heart attack in the next 30 years, it would be ApoB.

Not LDL. Not total cholesterol. Not your Framingham risk score. ApoB.

Most people have never heard of it. Most primary-care lipid panels don't include it. The reason it's not standard yet has more to do with medical inertia than with what the evidence says. The data is unambiguous, and the test costs an extra $10-30.

Here's why it matters and what to do about it.

What ApoB measures

Cholesterol travels through your blood inside lipoprotein particles. The dangerous ones — the ones that get stuck in your artery walls and start the atherosclerosis process — are the ApoB-containing lipoproteins. Specifically:

  • LDL (low-density lipoprotein)
  • VLDL (very-low-density lipoprotein)
  • IDL (intermediate-density lipoprotein)
  • Lp(a) — a specific subtype

Each of these particles has exactly one ApoB molecule on its surface. So if you measure ApoB, you're literally counting the number of atherogenic particles in your blood. Every ApoB particle is a potential plaque-builder.

LDL cholesterol, by contrast, measures the cholesterol content of LDL particles. Two people with the same LDL cholesterol can have very different numbers of LDL particles — depending on whether their particles are large and cholesterol-rich (less atherogenic per particle) or small and dense (more atherogenic per particle). The size doesn't matter for the artery wall; the count does.

This is why ApoB outperforms LDL as a predictor.

What the evidence says

Sniderman 2019 (JAMA Cardiology) analysed UK Biobank data on 389,529 participants over a median 8 years of follow-up. ApoB was a stronger predictor of CV events than LDL-C or non-HDL cholesterol. The effect was particularly large in people with discordance — where LDL was normal but ApoB elevated, or vice versa.

The Sniderman 2022 update in JAMA Cardiology pooled multiple cohorts (n = 195,608) and found ApoB the strongest single lipid marker for predicting MI risk. Per-particle effect was consistent regardless of whether someone was on a statin, had diabetes, or had a family history.

Mendelian randomisation studies (Ference 2017) — which use genetic variants as natural randomisation to test causation rather than correlation — found that lifelong lower ApoB causally reduces CHD risk in a dose-dependent way. This isn't just observational association; it's evidence of causal effect.

The European Atherosclerosis Society and Canadian Cardiovascular Society now recommend ApoB as the preferred lipid measurement for risk assessment. American guidelines have been slower to update, but the evidence has been moving in this direction for over a decade.

What's a good ApoB

Reference ranges vary, but for primary prevention (people without established CV disease):

  • <60 mg/dL — Excellent. The level seen in populations with very low CVD rates (rural Chinese, hunter-gatherer populations).
  • 60-80 mg/dL — Optimal. Most preventive cardiologists target this range.
  • 80-100 mg/dL — Borderline. Conventional reference range top, but elevated for prevention.
  • 100-120 mg/dL — Elevated. Increasing risk; lifestyle changes warranted; consider medication if other risk factors present.
  • >120 mg/dL — High. Strong indication for treatment regardless of other risk factors.

For comparison: the average American adult has ApoB around 90-105 mg/dL. The average European is 80-95. Hunter-gatherer populations and people with genetic protection are 35-55. The biology suggests human cardiovascular systems evolved for the lower numbers; the modern average is biologically high even when it sits in the "normal" lab reference range.

If you're at secondary prevention (already had a CV event), the targets tighten further: <65 mg/dL or even <55 mg/dL is what the recent guidelines recommend.

How to get it tested

Most standard lipid panels don't include ApoB by default. Three options:

1. Ask your doctor to add it. Many will if you specifically request it. Modern guidelines support this. Cost: usually under $30 added to a regular lipid panel; insurance often covers it given the risk-stratification rationale.

2. Order it directly. In the US, services like Quest Diagnostics, LabCorp, Marek Health, or Function Health let you order tests directly. ApoB is typically $20-40 standalone.

3. Bundle with other testing. If you're doing comprehensive bloodwork (annual physical with lipids, glucose, etc.), adding ApoB is trivial.

Don't accept "we don't typically test that" — the evidence base is strong, the cost is small, and the information you get is actionable.

What to do with the result

The intervention ladder, in order of evidence and effect size:

1. Diet changes. A diet rich in soluble fibre (legumes, oats, psyllium), low in saturated fat (less red and processed meat, less coconut oil despite the marketing, less butter), and high in unsaturated fats (olive oil, nuts, fatty fish) can lower ApoB by 10-25% over 3 months. The Mediterranean and DASH dietary patterns are the most studied.

2. Weight loss if BMI >25. Each 10 kg of weight loss lowers ApoB by ~5-10 mg/dL on average. Effect is sustained if weight stays off.

3. Aerobic exercise. 150+ minutes/week of moderate aerobic activity reduces ApoB by 5-10 mg/dL. Combined with resistance training, the effect is larger via metabolic mechanisms.

4. Reduce alcohol. Heavy drinking raises ApoB; cutting back can drop it 5-10%.

5. Statins if lifestyle alone doesn't get you to target. Atorvastatin and rosuvastatin produce ApoB reductions of 30-55% depending on dose. The 4S, HPS, and JUPITER trials all showed mortality benefits from statin therapy in elevated-risk populations.

6. Ezetimibe (Zetia, generic). Add to statin or use alone. Reduces ApoB another 15-20% on top of statin effect. IMPROVE-IT trial showed it added clinical benefit.

7. PCSK9 inhibitors (Repatha, Praluent) for high-risk patients. Inject every 2-4 weeks. Reduces ApoB another 50-60% beyond statin+ezetimibe. FOURIER and ODYSSEY trials showed clinical benefit. Expensive, but improving access.

8. Bempedoic acid (Nexletol). Newer. CLEAR Outcomes trial showed CV benefit in statin-intolerant patients. Modest ApoB effect (~25%) but a real option for patients who can't tolerate statins.

The ladder works for almost everyone. Lifestyle moves you 20-30%. Statins move you another 30-55%. Ezetimibe and PCSK9 inhibitors move you the rest of the way if needed.

Common questions

"My LDL is normal but my ApoB is high. What does that mean?" — You have many small dense LDL particles. This is more atherogenic than the same LDL with fewer larger particles. The treatment is the same as for high LDL (lifestyle + medication if needed), but the urgency is higher than your LDL alone would suggest.

"My LDL is high but my ApoB is normal. What does that mean?" — You have fewer, larger LDL particles. Less atherogenic than the LDL alone suggests. Treatment threshold may be higher than LDL-only risk calculators imply.

"What if I'm on a statin already?" — Test ApoB to see how aggressive your lipid-lowering needs to be. Many patients on statins still have elevated ApoB; adding ezetimibe or escalating dose may be warranted. The on-statin ApoB target is even tighter than primary prevention.

"Does diet alone get me there?" — Sometimes. People with genetic familial hypercholesterolemia usually can't get to target without medication. People with mild-moderate elevation often can with serious dietary changes. Try lifestyle for 3-6 months, retest, then escalate if needed.

"How often should I test?" — Every 1-2 years for primary prevention. Every 6-12 months if you're actively trying to lower it (let yourself see the effect of the intervention). Every 6 months if on lipid-lowering medication during dose titration.

The summary

ApoB is the best single CV-risk number you can have. The test costs $20-40. It's actionable — there's a clear intervention ladder for elevated values. The interventions work; the evidence is multi-decade and converging.

If you're over 35, get your ApoB tested. If you're over 50 and haven't, that should be your next blood test. The intervention happens too late if you wait until your first heart attack.

Most preventable major adverse cardiovascular events are preventable in the literal sense: there's a known marker, a known threshold, and known interventions. The marker is ApoB. The patient is whoever's reading this.
