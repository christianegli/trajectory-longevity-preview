Supplements we don't recommend (and why)

Most "longevity supplements" don't make it onto our recommendation list. Some are genuinely weak. Some have reasonable mechanisms but evidence didn't pan out in humans. A few got famous on the strength of one charismatic researcher.

Here's the short list of supplements we score low — and why.

Resveratrol

Effectiveness: 2/10. Certainty: 3/10.

Famous because of David Sinclair's research on yeast cells in 2003. Activates SIRT1, the "longevity gene." Got the cover of every fitness magazine. Got tens of millions in venture funding.

The problem: humans aren't yeast.

The dose used in mouse studies that extended lifespan was equivalent to ~1.5 grams per day in humans — roughly 100 bottles of red wine. At that dose, resveratrol is poorly absorbed (oral bioavailability ~1%) and primarily acts on the gut, not the rest of the body.

Human trials at supplement doses (500 mg–1 g): minimal or no effect on inflammation markers, glucose, blood pressure, or any longevity-relevant outcome. The 2014 NHANES analysis (Semba et al., JAMA Internal Medicine) — looking at 783 elderly Italians for 9 years — found no association between dietary resveratrol intake and cardiovascular disease, cancer, or all-cause mortality.

Mechanism: real. Translation to human longevity: not demonstrated.

If you want polyphenols, eat olives, blueberries, dark chocolate. They have polyphenols too, plus fiber and other things you actually need.

NMN and NR (NAD+ precursors)

Effectiveness: 4/10. Certainty: 4–5/10.

Sinclair again. NAD+ levels decline with age. Boost NAD+ → reverse aging, the pitch goes.

What we know:

  • Yes, oral NR raises blood NAD+. The NAD+ rise is real. (Trammell et al., 2016)
  • Yes, NMN does the same in humans. (Yoshino et al., 2021 — small RCT in postmenopausal women showed insulin sensitivity improvement.)

What we don't know:

  • Whether raising NAD+ in blood translates to raising it in tissues that matter
  • Whether the modest tissue NAD+ increase produces clinically meaningful change
  • Whether the change you get from NR/NMN is meaningfully different from what you get free from exercise (which raises NAD+ via different pathways)

Trial readouts have been disappointing. The 2018 NR trial in healthy older adults (Martens et al., Nature Communications) showed a small reduction in systolic blood pressure (~10 mmHg in the highest hypertensive subgroup) and no other significant outcomes.

NMN has been pulled by the FDA from supplement classification (2022) because pharma companies are pursuing it as a drug — which means commercial supplement-grade NMN may or may not be what's on the label.

Cost: $30–80/month. For something that might do as much as a brisk walk.

If you want NAD+, do high-intensity exercise. It actually raises tissue NAD+, and the rest of what it does is well-established.

High-dose vitamin C

Effectiveness: 2/10. Certainty: 9/10.

Linus Pauling, 1970. Megadose vitamin C cures everything.

Among the most thoroughly studied questions in nutrition. The answer is well-established: vitamin C supplementation (above ~200 mg/day) does not prevent cardiovascular disease, cancer, or extend lifespan in well-nourished populations.

The 2013 Cochrane meta-analysis (29 trials, 11,306 participants): no reduction in all-cause mortality.

What it does:

  • Reduces duration of common cold by ~half a day if taken regularly (modest)
  • Treats outright deficiency (scurvy — almost no one in developed countries)
  • Acts as a mild antioxidant (offset by the body downregulating its own antioxidant enzymes)

What it doesn't do: anything that justifies the $20–60/year that supplement-takers spend.

If your fruit and vegetable intake is decent (5+ servings/day), you have plenty.

Alpha-lipoic acid

Effectiveness: 2/10. Certainty: 5/10.

Antioxidant. Mitochondrial cofactor. Crosses the blood-brain barrier. Everything sounds promising.

Reality:

  • Trials for diabetic neuropathy: modest benefit (some symptom improvement, no disease modification)
  • Trials for weight loss: small effect (~1.5 kg over 4 months) that disappears off-supplement
  • Trials for cardiovascular outcomes: no benefit
  • Trials for dementia prevention: no benefit (Galasko et al., 2012)

The mechanisms all sound reasonable. The clinical signal is weak.

For diabetic neuropathy, ask your doctor — there are better options. For everything else, save your money.

What we recommend instead

If a supplement scored 2–4 on effectiveness in our catalogue, we don't think you should take it routinely. The pattern we look for is:

  1. Mechanism that translates to humans — not just to yeast or mice
  2. Multiple human RCTs with consistent direction — not one charismatic study
  3. Effect size that justifies cost and pill burden — not 5% improvement on a surrogate marker

What clears that bar:

  • Vitamin D3 — if you're deficient. Test first.
  • Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) — at 1–2 g/day for cardiovascular and brain
  • Creatine monohydrate — 5 g/day for muscle and (more recently) brain
  • Fiber — 25–35 g/day, ideally from food
  • Protein — 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day, especially with age
  • Magnesium — if intake is low

That's most of what we recommend, and most of what your money should go to.

The rest of the supplement aisle is built on charismatic mechanisms that didn't translate. Charismatic mechanisms are not the same as evidence.

The pattern

Almost every supplement that became famous on the basis of one researcher's work — Pauling on vitamin C, Sinclair on resveratrol/NAD+ — turned out to be weaker than promised in humans.

Almost every intervention that became famous because thousands of people studied it independently and the effect kept showing up in different populations — exercise, sleep, fiber, statins for the right people — held up.

When you see a supplement marketed with a famous name and a striking mechanism, that's the pattern to be suspicious of. The boring interventions (the ones nobody made $50M from selling a brand) are the ones that work.

We score what's in our catalogue based on human evidence, not mechanism stories. If you want to see how a specific supplement scored, search the catalogue. If we scored it 2–4, we don't think it's worth the money.
