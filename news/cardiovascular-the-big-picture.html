Cardiovascular disease — the big picture, in order

#cardiovascular#methodology#framework

If you optimise one thing about your health, optimise this. Cardiovascular disease (CVD) kills more people than cancer, more than infections, more than dementia, more than accidents. About 18 million deaths per year globally — roughly one in three. And unlike many of the leading causes of death, CVD is largely preventable with interventions whose effectiveness has been validated in randomised trials covering hundreds of thousands of patients across decades.

This post is the framework. Forty-eight interventions in our catalogue touch cardiovascular outcomes — that's overwhelming. The evidence shows clear ordering: some interventions move the needle a lot, some move it a little, some look impressive in marketing but do almost nothing. Here's the picture, ordered by impact.

The single mechanism

Almost all atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease — heart attacks, most strokes, peripheral artery disease — comes from the same process. ApoB-containing lipoprotein particles cross your artery wall, get oxidised, trigger inflammation, attract immune cells, and form a plaque. The plaque grows over decades. At some point, it ruptures, a blood clot forms, an artery occludes, and a major event happens.

The intervention targets are obvious from this mechanism:

  • Reduce ApoB particles in the blood
  • Reduce blood pressure (less force damaging artery walls)
  • Reduce inflammation (less plaque-promoting signal)
  • Avoid acute plaque rupture (lifestyle, smoking, stress)
  • Maintain a metabolically healthy environment (insulin sensitivity, glucose control)

Almost every CVD intervention works through one or more of these levers. Knowing the mechanism lets you evaluate any new claim — does it lower ApoB, BP, inflammation? If not, it's probably noise.

The intervention ladder, ordered by lifetime impact

Tier 1: don't smoke, don't be sedentary, basic dietary pattern

These are the universal foundations. Each accounts for somewhere between 20% and 60% of CVD risk individually. Together, they account for 70-80% of the modifiable population risk.

1. No smoking. Smoking causes 25% of CVD deaths and is the single highest-impact reversible factor. Quitting at any age extends life expectancy by years to decades. The Doll & Peto British Doctors Study followed 35,000 doctors over 50 years; smokers lost 10 years of life expectancy on average vs never-smokers. Quitters in their 40s recovered 9 years; quitters in their 60s recovered 3 years.

2. Mediterranean dietary pattern. PREDIMED 2013 — 7,447 high-CV-risk adults randomised to Mediterranean diet (with EVOO or with mixed nuts) vs low-fat control. Primary endpoint was a composite of MI, stroke, and CV death. Stopped early at 4.8 years for benefit: 30% reduction in events. The dietary pattern matters more than any single component.

3. Aerobic exercise to maintain VO2max. Mandsager 2018 (n=122,007 Cleveland Clinic stress tests): being elite-fit (top 2.3% by age) had HR 0.20 for all-cause mortality vs low-fit. Even moderate fitness (top 75%) had HR 0.51. Larger effect than statins, equivalent to quitting smoking. The mechanism is partly cardiovascular, partly cardiopulmonary, partly metabolic.

4. Resistance training. LIFTMOR + Strasser 2010 meta-analysis: resistance training reduces CV mortality by 15-20% independent of cardio effects. Mechanism includes improved insulin sensitivity, glucose disposal, and visceral fat reduction.

5. Adequate sleep. Cappuccio 2010 meta of n=474,684: U-shaped curve, with sleep <6h or >9h both elevating CV risk vs 7-8h. The mechanism includes inflammation, blood pressure regulation, and metabolic effects.

These five interventions, done consistently, eliminate most modifiable CVD risk for most people. Anything below this list is finishing on top of an existing strong foundation.

Tier 2: lipid-lowering medications when needed

For people with elevated ApoB despite lifestyle changes, statin therapy is the highest-evidence pharmacological intervention in the catalogue.

6. Statins (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin). Meta-analysis of 27 statin trials (CTT Collaboration 2010): each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL cholesterol reduces major vascular events by 22%. The benefit is consistent across baseline LDL, age, sex, diabetes status, and prior CV disease. Side-effect concerns (muscle pain, cognition) have been overstated by media; trial-level analysis (StatinWISE 2021) shows that the muscle pain attributed to statins is largely nocebo when tested in blinded crossover designs.

7. Ezetimibe. Adds another 15-20% LDL reduction on top of statin. IMPROVE-IT trial (Cannon 2015 NEJM, n=18,144): adding ezetimibe to statin reduced primary endpoint (CV death, MI, stroke, etc.) by 6.4% over 7 years.

8. PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, alirocumab). Inject every 2-4 weeks. Reduces LDL another 50-60% beyond statin+ezetimibe. FOURIER trial (Sabatine 2017 NEJM, n=27,564): 15% reduction in primary CV endpoint over 2.2 years. Expensive but increasingly accessible. Reserve for high-risk patients not at goal on max-dose statin + ezetimibe.

9. Bempedoic acid. Newer, useful for statin-intolerant patients. CLEAR Outcomes 2023 (Nissen 2023 NEJM, n=13,970): 13% reduction in MACE in statin-intolerant patients. Smaller effect than statins but a real option for the 5-10% of patients who can't tolerate statins.

Tier 3: blood pressure management

If your BP is consistently above 130/80, the intervention ladder shifts to BP-lowering.

10. Lifestyle BP reduction. DASH diet (Sacks 2001 NEJM, n=412): reduces SBP by 8-14 mmHg in hypertensive patients. Sodium reduction adds 5 mmHg. Weight loss of 10kg adds another 5-10 mmHg. Aerobic exercise adds 5 mmHg. These compound.

11. ACE inhibitors / ARBs. Strong evidence for first-line therapy. ACE inhibitors reduce events independent of their BP effect — endothelial protection. Lisinopril, ramipril (ACE-I); losartan, telmisartan (ARB).

12. Calcium channel blockers and thiazides. Second/third-line. Amlodipine + chlorthalidone are the most-studied generics.

The trial-level evidence shows that getting from "uncontrolled hypertension" (>140/90) to "controlled" (<130/80) prevents about 1 in 5 cardiovascular events. The drugs are cheap, safe at typical doses, and well-validated.

Tier 4: anti-inflammatory and metabolic interventions

The newer frontier in CV medicine. Mechanism: residual inflammation drives the 30-50% of CV events that occur in patients with controlled lipids and BP.

13. SGLT2 inhibitors. Originally diabetes drugs; now established in heart failure and CKD. EMPEROR-Reduced (2020) and DAPA-HF (2019) both showed 25-30% reduction in CV death + heart failure hospitalisation. Used increasingly even in non-diabetics with CV disease. Mechanism includes BP, weight, fluid management, and possibly direct cardiac effects.

14. GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide). Originally diabetes/weight; now CV outcomes drugs. SELECT trial (Lincoff 2023 NEJM, n=17,604, semaglutide in obese non-diabetics): 20% reduction in MACE over 3 years. STEP-HFpEF: improved outcomes in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction. The CV benefit is partly weight-mediated and partly direct.

15. Low-dose aspirin. Well-established for secondary prevention (already had a CV event). For primary prevention: ASCEND/ARRIVE/ASPREE trials in 2018-2019 collectively shifted recommendations against routine primary-prevention use; bleeding risk approximately balances CV benefit in average-risk adults. Now used selectively, when patient has elevated CV risk + low bleeding risk.

Tier 5: targeted nutrition and supplementation

These compound on top of strong foundations. None individually is large; collectively they may add 5-10% additional risk reduction.

16. Omega-3 (EPA-rich). REDUCE-IT (Bhatt 2019 NEJM): high-dose icosapent ethyl reduced primary endpoint by 25% in patients with elevated triglycerides on statins. Lower-dose mixed EPA/DHA: small/null effect. Different formulations matter.

17. Cocoa flavanols. COSMOS trial (Sesso 2022, n=21,442): 27% reduction in CV mortality in adults 60+ over 3.6 years. Specific cocoa flavanol extract; not the same as eating dark chocolate.

18. Olive oil (EVOO). Fundamental component of the Mediterranean dietary pattern. EVOO-specific evidence (Guasch-Ferré 2014, large prospective cohort): higher EVOO consumption associated with lower CV mortality.

19. Soluble fibre (psyllium, oats, legumes). Modest LDL reduction (3-7%) at meaningful doses. Adds to the lipid stack rather than replacing it.

20. Coffee. Moderate consumption (2-4 cups/day) consistently associated with lower CV and all-cause mortality. Mechanism likely includes polyphenols, anti-inflammatory effects, and improved insulin sensitivity.

What does almost nothing

Things heavily marketed as CV-supportive that don't show meaningful benefit in randomised trials:

  • Vitamin E / vitamin C supplementation. Multiple large RCTs negative.
  • Multivitamins. PHS-II (n=14,641, Sesso 2012): no CV benefit.
  • Co-Q10. Modest evidence in heart failure, none in primary prevention.
  • Resveratrol. Cell-line and animal data fascinating; human trials small/null.
  • Garlic supplements. Small BP effect in some trials, null in others. Real garlic in food is fine; pills don't move major endpoints.

These items are in the catalogue (some), but none should crowd out tier 1-3 interventions in your time, attention, or budget.

The compounding effect

Each of the tier 1 interventions (no smoking, exercise, Mediterranean diet, sleep) reduces CV risk by 20-40% individually. They don't add linearly — they compound, but with diminishing returns. Empirically, doing all five is associated with 70-80% lower CVD risk vs doing none. The marginal contribution of each gets smaller as the others are in place.

This is the source of the common observation that two longevity interventions don't double their effect. It's also why optimising the lifestyle stack first is essential before considering anything more exotic.

What this looks like in practice

If you're 35 and asymptomatic:

  • Don't smoke. Don't start.
  • Mediterranean dietary pattern as your default.
  • 3-4 hours/week mixed cardio + 2 sessions/week resistance.
  • 7-8 hours sleep most nights.
  • Get an ApoB test. If elevated, address it (lifestyle first, statin if needed).
  • Get blood pressure measured. If elevated, address it.

If you're 55 with normal labs:

  • All of the above, plus
  • Resistance training is more important now than at 35.
  • Annual ApoB + BP. Consider statin if ApoB >100 even without other risk factors.
  • Consider EPA-rich omega-3 if triglycerides elevated.

If you're 55 with an elevated ApoB or family history:

  • All of the above, plus
  • Aggressive statin therapy. Add ezetimibe if not at goal.
  • PCSK9 inhibitor if very high risk.
  • Consider lower-dose aspirin if bleeding risk allows.
  • Tighten BP target to <125/75.

If you're 65 with established disease (prior MI, stable CAD):

  • All of the above, plus
  • Statin to target ApoB <55 mg/dL.
  • Often statin + ezetimibe + PCSK9i combination.
  • ACE-I or ARB even if BP normal.
  • Consider SGLT2 inhibitor.
  • Cardiac rehab if recent event.

These are general patterns — your actual prescription depends on your specific risk profile, contraindications, and clinician judgment. But the framework is well-established. The treatment isn't the hard part. The hard part is the consistency over decades.

The summary

Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death and is largely preventable. The interventions are well-validated. The ordering matters: lifestyle foundations first, then targeted lipid management, then BP control, then the metabolic-inflammatory frontier. Most CVD events in 2026 occur in people who never received the basic interventions in time. The opportunity to reverse most population-level CV risk exists in the technology we already have — applied earlier, more consistently, more aggressively.

If you read 100 longevity articles and only remember one thing, remember the cardiovascular intervention ladder. It will save more lives than any other single piece of knowledge.
