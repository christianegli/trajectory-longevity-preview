Five things we got wrong in version 1

#methodology#wizard#transparency

The first version of the Trajectory assessment wizard launched and almost immediately produced output that was wrong for at least 5 of the 8 personas we ran through it.

We caught it because we ran a hostile audit. Eight edge personas — designed to find the seams — were sent through the rules. Five of them got recommendations that ranged from useless to actively wrong.

Here's what was broken and what changed. Software should publish its mistakes when it fixes them.

Mistake 1: Apologetic output for already-optimised users

The 35-year-old healthy non-smoker, in great cardio shape, eating a Mediterranean diet, sleeping 8 hours: the wizard said "Here are 12 things to consider — your stack."

This was wrong. That person doesn't need 12 things. The leverage points were already done. The honest answer is "You're already doing the big stuff. The marginal additions are these 2-3 small refinements."

Fix: When the leverage-point pass produces ≤3 items, the result page now reframes — headline becomes "You're already doing the big stuff," with explanatory framing instead of a generic "your stack" treatment. Output adapts to whoever you are, not the standard output shape.

Mistake 2: Depression cohort got generic recommendations

The 50-year-old depressed obese non-T2D persona: the wizard suggested standard quickwins (omega-3, vitamin D, exercise) at standard priority. But for this person, omega-3 isn't a quickwin — it's a leverage point. Specifically, EPA-rich omega-3 has small-to-moderate effects in depression meta-analyses, with the effect concentrated in EPA (not DHA), at higher doses than typical "fish oil" capsules contain.

The original output didn't surface that. It treated depression and 'no depression' as the same input.

Fix: Conditions now bump condition-relevant items to higher priority (55 instead of 35), with condition-specific protocol notes. For depression: omega-3 protocol becomes "2g EPA-rich omega-3 daily (look for >2:1 EPA:DHA ratio) with food. Track mood weekly with PHQ-2." Same for sun exposure (priority bump for depression because circadian dysregulation is part of the picture).

Mistake 3: Sleep-deprived users didn't see bright-light morning intervention

The persona reporting <6 hours of sleep got recommendations about sleep architecture and sleep hygiene — but the bright-light morning exposure intervention (one of the most evidence-supported circadian-realignment tools) wasn't surfaced.

This was a gap in the rules. Sleep architecture interventions and circadian interventions are related but not identical, and the wizard wasn't connecting them.

Fix: Sleep <6h now adds the bright-light morning quickwin at priority 60, with explicit protocol: "10-30 minutes of outdoor light within 60 minutes of waking. If outdoors isn't possible, a 10,000-lux SAD lamp at 30cm for 20-30 minutes. Skip in summer if you're already getting natural morning light."

Mistake 4: Trying-to-conceive persona saw inappropriate items

The 32-year-old TTC persona: the wizard would have surfaced rapamycin, fasting protocols, and several supplements for which pregnancy/early-pregnancy data is absent. Even though the user said they were trying to conceive, the contraindication logic didn't fire because the user wasn't yet pregnant.

This was the worst mistake on the audit. Recommending fasting-mimicking diet to someone who might be pregnant within 4 weeks isn't an academic problem.

Fix: "Trying to conceive" now triggers the same contraindication set as "pregnant" — 20 slugs banned (rapamycin, FMD, high-dose curcumin, dasatinib + quercetin, fisetin, low-dose lithium, several others). They go into the flagged section with explicit "while planning pregnancy" reasoning.

Mistake 5: Cancer survivor cohort got rapamycin in their list

The 55-year-old breast-cancer-survivor persona received rapamycin as a discussable item. Rapamycin is mTOR inhibition; mTOR pathway is implicated in breast cancer biology; the data on rapamycin in cancer survivors is ambivalent at best, and the cancer-survival oncology consensus is to avoid medications that haven't been studied in their specific situation.

The wizard's rule for "cancer history" was checking the existence of the field but not blocking the items most relevant to that history.

Fix: Cancer history now blocks rapamycin and alcohol from the recommendation list (both go to flagged with reason). Lower-stakes items (e.g., omega-3, mediterranean diet, exercise) remain — those are fine in survivorship and arguably beneficial.

What we learned about hostile audits

A few methodological observations:

1. The bugs were obvious only after we looked. Each fix was 2-5 lines of JavaScript. The reason they shipped wasn't that they were hard to identify — it was that we only audit when we explicitly try to break things. The framework that produced the wizard's logic produced sensible output for the 'normal' personas; only the edges broke.

2. Hostile personas need to be designed. The 8 personas weren't picked randomly. Each was constructed to hit a specific failure mode: 'optimised user → does the wizard handle low-leverage cases?', 'cancer survivor → does it block oncology-conflicting items?', 'unknown labs → does it stall or proceed?'. Random sampling of users wouldn't have caught these — the failure modes are too narrow.

3. The fix is rarely in the recommendation engine; it's in the priority logic. Most bugs were "wizard surfaced something that would have been right for a generic person but was wrong for this specific person." The fix is rarely "remove the item" — it's "change when the item gets surfaced and at what priority."

4. Fixes have to be tested across all personas, not just the failing one. When we fixed the depression-omega-3 priority bump, we re-ran all 8 personas to make sure the change didn't break the others. It almost did once: the priority-55 bump caused omega-3 to outrank a more-leverage-y item for one persona. The cap structure caught it; we adjusted.

Why we're publishing this

Most software that recommends interventions is a black box. This wizard runs deterministic JavaScript in your browser. Same inputs → same output, every time. We can audit it. So can you. Source code is in the GitHub repo.

Publishing the failure modes is part of the social contract. If we're going to recommend things to people, we should be on record about how we test and what we've gotten wrong. This is round one. There will be more — we're running another audit pass next week with different personas.

If you've taken the quest and the output felt off, tell us. We use real complaints to find real bugs. The wizard isn't a black box that can't be challenged; it's a deterministic system that can be examined and corrected.

The catalogue is open. The rules are open. The mistakes we've made are now also open.

— Trajectory team
