Stop chasing rapamycin until you're sleeping 7.5 hours

The rapamycin discourse has gotten ahead of the data. The math says: fix sleep, train VO2max, eat Mediterranean — those have a 50–100× larger expected mortality benefit than any current Frontier intervention.

#opinion#rapamycin#sleep#foundation

I'm going to make an unpopular claim: most people reading about rapamycin should not be taking rapamycin. Not because the mechanism isn't compelling (it is), and not because the mouse data isn't striking (it is), but because the math of where to spend your finite attention and risk budget says you should be elsewhere.

The expected-value problem

Let's do something the longevity discourse rarely does: write down expected values.

Rapamycin (Sirolimus)T3E 9C 4 in healthy humans: We have small biomarker trials. We have observational data on transplant recipients (a population so different from healthy users that the read-across is shaky). We have no human RCT with a hard outcome. The honest expected mortality reduction over 10 years for a 50-year-old healthy adult: somewhere between 0% and 5%, with massive uncertainty bars.

Sleep (7-9h, regularity, deep+REM)T1E 8C 8 going from 6 hours to 7.5 hours: We have multiple large prospective cohorts, controlled sleep restriction studies, and mechanistic work showing improvements in glycemic control, immune function, cardiovascular markers, cognitive function, and mortality. The expected mortality reduction over 10 years: 8–15%, well-bounded.

VO2max TrainingT1E 9C 9 going from below-average to above-average fitness: We covered this two days ago. Expected mortality reduction: 15–25%, with confidence intervals tight enough to bet on.

Mediterranean DietT1E 7C 9 adopted seriously: PREDIMED-type effect sizes give roughly 8–12% reduction in cardiovascular events; cohort data suggests similar all-cause mortality benefit.

You can roll your eyes at point estimates, but the order of magnitude is what matters. Rapamycin sits in the 0–5% bucket with massive uncertainty. The four boring interventions sit in the 8–25% bucket with tight uncertainty. Each of them is a 5–25× better expected return than rapamycin, before we account for the cost and risk asymmetry.

"But I'm already doing the basics"

Are you?

When pushed, almost every "I'm already doing the basics" person turns out to be:

  • Sleeping 6.5 hours, not 7.5+, with no fixed wake time
  • Doing some cardio but not actually training VO2max
  • Eating "pretty healthy" but with 30%+ ultra-processed foods by calorie
  • Sitting 9+ hours a day
  • Drinking more alcohol than they admit when asked

Each of those gaps individually has a larger expected longevity benefit than starting rapamycin.

The intuition trap is that the basics feel "done" because you're aware of them, and rapamycin feels "new" because you're chasing it. Awareness isn't execution. The marginal value isn't in adding more interventions on top — it's in actually doing the ones you've already nominally adopted.

The risk asymmetry nobody talks about

The safety case for Rapamycin (Sirolimus)T3E 9C 4 in healthy adults is reasonable but not airtight. Common signals from intermittent dosing trials include:

  • Mouth ulcers (~10%)
  • Lipid elevations (LDL, triglycerides)
  • Glucose dysregulation in some users
  • Theoretical immunosuppression with infection risk
  • Known interactions with statins, cyclosporine, antibiotics, antifungals
  • Unknown long-term effects on humans because we have no long-term human data

The downside of being wrong about rapamycin is "took an immunosuppressant for years and discovered something we don't yet know."

The downside of being wrong about sleeping more is "felt better and had marginally fewer bad days." This is not a hard call.

When does rapamycin actually become reasonable?

Three conditions, all of them:

  1. Foundation Stack is genuinely solid. You sleep 7.5+ hours with high regularity, you have a measured VO2max in the "good" range for your age, your diet quality is "good" not "okay," your strength is benchmarked.
  2. You've optimised the medium-leverage interventions. Mediterranean-style eating, time-restricted-eating, omega-3 status, vitamin D status, fiber intake, the obvious supplements.
  3. You have a physician who actually knows the rapamycin literature and is prepared to monitor lipids, glucose, and infection markers.

If those three boxes aren't checked, the energy is better spent on the basics. They have better evidence, larger expected effect, lower cost, and effectively no downside.

What this is not

This is not "rapamycin is bad." The mouse data is some of the best in the field. The mTOR mechanism is genuinely compelling. There's a plausible world in 5–10 years where the human RCT data lands and we update.

This is "the rapamycin discourse is wildly disproportionate to the evidence, and most people chasing it are mis-allocating their attention."

If you read one piece on this site this month, let it be this one. Boring beats interesting when boring has 5× the evidence and 1/5th the risk.
