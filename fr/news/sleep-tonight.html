Sleep — the highest-leverage intervention you can do tonight

#sleep#lifestyle#foundation

If we ranked the catalogue by mortality-impact-per-dollar-spent, sleep would win. The interventions are free. The medications and supplements you might take for sleep are not the highest-impact items. The highest-impact items are behavioural, and most people skip them.

Here's the actual priority list, ranked by evidence quality and effect size, not by what's popular on Instagram.

What's actually broken (for most people)

The sleep problems we see most often:

  1. Going to bed too late (consistently after midnight, then trying to wake at 7)
  2. Going to bed at irregular times (varying by 60+ minutes night to night)
  3. Insufficient morning bright light (especially indoor workers in winter)
  4. Caffeine after noon (half-life ~5-6 hours, so 4pm coffee is 50% still active at bedtime)
  5. Alcohol within 3 hours of bed (suppresses REM, fragmented architecture)
  6. Screens in bed (less about blue light, more about cognitive arousal)
  7. Bedroom too warm (>20°C / 68°F)

Each of these is fixable without a single supplement.

The hierarchy of sleep interventions, in order

Tier 1: Free, immediate, biggest effect

1. Fix your wake time. Pick a consistent wake time you can hit on weekdays AND weekends. Anchor it. Most sleep dysfunction starts here — varying wake times by >60 minutes is functional jet lag for your circadian clock.

2. Get morning bright light within 60 minutes of waking. 10-30 minutes of outdoor light. If outdoors isn't possible, a 10,000-lux SAD lamp at 30cm for 20-30 minutes. This sets your circadian phase and is the strongest behavioural lever for sleep timing. The evidence base is decades of circadian research.

3. Cut caffeine after noon. Half-life is 5-6 hours. A 1pm coffee is 25-50% still active at 11pm. Even people who say "caffeine doesn't affect me" show measurable sleep architecture changes when tested. Try a 2-week experiment. Most people report better sleep.

4. Stop drinking alcohol within 3 hours of bed. Alcohol disrupts REM sleep heavily. The "puts you to sleep" effect is real but the sleep is shallower and more fragmented. Subjective sleep quality the night after drinking is consistently worse on objective measurement.

5. Cool the bedroom to 18-19°C (64-66°F). Body core temperature drops are part of sleep onset. A warm room blocks this. The most cost-effective sleep intervention you can buy is usually a fan.

These five cost zero dollars. They're the highest-impact things.

Tier 2: Behavioural changes, slightly harder

6. Consistent bedtime ±30 minutes. Less critical than wake time, but a regular bedtime amplifies the effect. After fixing wake time, many people self-regulate bedtime within a few weeks.

7. Wind-down protocol — 30-60 minutes before bed. No screens, dim lights, low cognitive load. Reading on paper, stretching, talking with people who don't trigger anxiety, light tidying. The protocol itself matters less than the consistency.

8. Get out of bed if you can't sleep within ~20 minutes. Lying awake in bed conditions your brain to associate bed with anxious wakefulness. Get up, do something low-stimulation (read, sit somewhere dim), return when sleepy. Counterintuitive but evidence-supported (cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia, CBT-I).

9. No food within 3 hours of bed. Late eating affects circadian glucose response and core temperature. The effect is smaller than the others on this list but real.

Tier 3: Equipment and environment

10. Blackout curtains or sleep mask. The retinal sensitivity of sleeping humans is enough that even small ambient light fragments sleep. Get the room dark enough that you can't see your hand in front of your face.

11. White noise or earplugs. Acoustic disruptions are the most under-recognised sleep saboteur. Trains, neighbours, partners, garbage trucks. White noise machines are $30 and work.

12. Mattress and pillow check. Old mattresses degrade sleep. Replace at 7-10 years. Pillow firmness/loft should match your sleeping position.

Tier 4: Targeted supplements

These come last for a reason. The lifestyle changes above are larger, cheaper, and more durable.

13. Magnesium glycinate, 200-400mg before bed. Modest effect. Helps if you're deficient (most people are mildly so). Glycinate form is best tolerated; oxide and citrate cause GI distress at higher doses.

14. L-theanine, 200mg before bed (if anxious). Reduces sleep-onset anxiety without sedation. Doesn't help sleep architecture much — helps with falling asleep when the issue is racing thoughts.

15. Glycine, 3g before bed (if struggling with sleep depth). Modest evidence for improved subjective sleep quality. Inada 2007 trial showed 3g 60 minutes before bed reduced sleep latency and improved daytime alertness.

16. Tart cherry juice extract. Minor melatonin content. The effect size is small but the side-effect profile is good.

Tier 5: Don't start with these

17. Melatonin (immediate-release). Useful for jet lag and shift work. For chronic insomnia, the effect is small (~7 minutes faster sleep onset, no significant change in total sleep time per meta-analysis). Most people taking it don't need it. Doses sold in stores (5-10mg) are 10-50x what's needed; physiological dose is 0.3-0.5mg.

18. Prescription sleep medications (zolpidem, eszopiclone, etc.) Help short-term; chronic use associated with cognitive impairment and possibly elevated dementia risk in older adults. Use sparingly and short-term, ideally under medical supervision. Not a longevity intervention.

19. Cannabinoids for sleep. THC reduces REM sleep substantially; CBD evidence is weak. Tolerance develops quickly. Not a stable long-term sleep tool.

The actual evidence

Buysse 2014 (Lancet Psychiatry) reviewed sleep and mortality across cohorts: <6 hours and >9 hours both associated with elevated mortality, with the U-shape suggesting 7-8 hours is optimal. The mortality effect is comparable in magnitude to smoking — sleeping 5 hours nightly versus 7 raises mortality risk roughly as much as smoking 5 cigarettes a day.

Tasali 2022 (JAMA Internal Medicine) RCT: extending sleep by 1.2 hours per night for 2 weeks reduced caloric intake by 270 kcal/day in adults sleeping <6.5 hours at baseline. Real metabolic effect from sleeping more.

Cappuccio 2010 meta-analysis: sleep duration <6 hours associated with 12% increase in all-cause mortality vs 7-8 hours.

Riemann 2023 European insomnia guidelines: CBT-I (cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia) is the recommended first-line treatment for chronic insomnia, ahead of all medication classes. It's behavioural; works in 6-8 sessions; effects are durable beyond medication-cessation.

The evidence is unambiguous: behavioural interventions are first-line. Equipment is second. Supplements are third. Medications are last and short-term only.

How to start tonight

If you read this and want to do something tonight:

  • Set tomorrow's wake time. Make it consistent across 7 days.
  • Tomorrow morning: 15 minutes of outdoor light within 60 minutes of waking.
  • Don't drink coffee after noon.
  • Stop drinking alcohol after 8pm.
  • Set your bedroom to 18°C tonight.

That's free. It's higher-leverage than any pill on this site. Try it for 14 days before reaching for melatonin.
