The protein RDA is wrong for adults over 50

The 0.8 g/kg/day RDA was set in 1968 for healthy young adults — and it's wrong for almost everyone over 50. The newer evidence-based target is 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day, ideally spread across meals.

#protein#sarcopenia#frailty#opinion#rda

Few nutritional recommendations have aged worse than the protein RDA.

The 0.8 g/kg/day figure — the one your doctor cites, the one on the back of the box — was derived in 1968 from short-term nitrogen-balance studies in healthy young men. It was designed to prevent overt protein deficiency in a 25-year-old, not to optimise muscle preservation, function, or longevity in an aging adult.

We have 50 years of better data now. None of it supports the RDA for adults over 50.

What "anabolic resistance" actually means

As we age, the muscle protein synthesis response to a given dose of dietary protein gets blunted. A 25-year-old eating 20g of protein triggers a measurable spike in muscle protein synthesis. A 70-year-old eating the same 20g triggers about half the response.

The fix is well-characterised: older adults need more protein per meal, not less, to overcome anabolic resistance. The threshold for a meaningful synthesis signal rises from about 20g per meal in young adults to 30–40g per meal in older adults.

This isn't fringe science. It's the basis of the PROT-AGE consensus statement (2013) and a series of position papers from the International Society of Sports Nutrition. The clinical sarcopenia guidelines from ESPEN and the European Geriatric Medicine Society agree.

What the actual numbers look like

For most healthy adults over 50:

  • Total daily intake: 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day. For a 75 kg adult, that's 90–120 g protein/day.
  • Per-meal distribution: 25–40 g per meal, three meals a day.
  • Quality matters: complete proteins (animal sources, soy, pea+rice combos) signal more strongly than incomplete sources.
  • Timing around training: post-workout protein is helpful but the daily total dwarfs the timing effect.

For older adults with frailty risk or active resistance training, push to 1.6 g/kg/day. For those with chronic kidney disease, dial back and consult nephrology — this is where the RDA's caution actually applies.

How to actually hit the target

Most adults wildly underestimate their protein intake. A spot-check of a typical "high-protein" Western day:

  • Breakfast: 2 eggs + toast = 14g
  • Lunch: chicken Caesar salad = 25g
  • Dinner: salmon + vegetables = 35g
  • Total: ~74g

For a 75 kg adult that's ~1.0 g/kg/day. Below target.

The fix is usually one of these three:

  1. Add a protein-forward breakfast. Greek yogurt (20g) + eggs (12g) + cottage cheese (15g) gets you to 35–45g before 10am.
  2. Add a single 30g whey shake daily. Cheap, fast, evidence-backed.
  3. Anchor every meal with a 30g protein source. Prepping a chicken breast or tofu block in advance solves this.

Why this connects to longevity

Sarcopenia — age-related muscle loss — is one of the strongest predictors of disability, falls, and mortality in older adults. Grip strength alone outpredicts most clinical biomarkers for all-cause mortality.

Protein intake is necessary but not sufficient. You also need the stimulus: Resistance TrainingT1E 7C 9 is the on-switch. Protein is the building material. Without lifting, dietary protein doesn't translate to preserved muscle. Without protein, lifting can't build the muscle it signals for.

The compound move — daily protein + 2× weekly lifting + 5g CreatineT1E 5C 9 — is the most efficient anti-sarcopenia stack we have. None of those interventions are expensive, novel, or risky. The only thing standing between most older adults and substantially better function in their 70s is doing them.

Common pushbacks

"Won't this strain my kidneys?" No, in healthy adults. The "high protein hurts kidneys" claim comes from CKD literature where protein is genuinely problematic. In otherwise healthy adults, multiple long-term trials have failed to show kidney harm at intakes up to 2.0 g/kg/day.

"Doesn't IGF-1 go up?" Yes, and that's a legitimate concern in cancer survivors and people with active malignancy — discuss with your oncologist. For the general aging population, the IGF-1 signal of moderately high protein is dwarfed by the functional benefit.

"Can I just eat plant protein?" Yes, but you'll need ~10–20% more total grams to hit the same MPS signal because plant proteins are usually leucine-poor. Vegan stacks should include a daily soy or pea protein supplement and ideally CreatineT1E 5C 9 (which is naturally low in plant-based diets).

Bottom line

If you're over 50 and following the RDA, you're under-eating protein for your goals. The newer evidence-based target — 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day, with 25–40g per meal — is the foundation under Resistance TrainingT1E 7C 9 and the floor under preserved function in your 70s.

This is what the wizard recommends for older users for a reason. Take it seriously.
