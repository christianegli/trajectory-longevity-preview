Why we have 73 interventions, not 700

#methodology#philosophy#curation

If you Google "longevity supplements," you'll find lists of 200, 500, sometimes 1,000+ compounds. Most of them are theatre. Trajectory has 73 entries. That's not because we're lazy — it's because most things people sell as "longevity interventions" don't have evidence that survives a hostile read.

Here's how we decide what makes the catalogue.

The rule: any serious longevity-relevant evidence

To make the list, an intervention needs at least one of:

  1. Mammalian lifespan extension in a well-designed study (replicated if mouse; convincing primate cohort if not). NMN squeaks in here. So does rapamycin, with much stronger data.
  2. Human mortality or major-event RCT. Statins. GLP-1s. Mediterranean diet (Lyon Heart Study, PREDIMED). This is the gold standard.
  3. Strong human cohort + plausible mechanism for hard endpoints. Sleep duration, smoking cessation, VO2max, resistance training. The mechanism alone isn't enough; we need epidemiology with consistent direction across multiple cohorts.

If something only has in-vitro evidence — yeast, worms, cell culture — it doesn't make the list, even if it's selling well. C. elegans is not a 70-year-old human. The translation rate from worms to mammals is roughly 10%, and from mice to humans is maybe 30-40%. Most "novel longevity compounds" you see in supplement ads have only worm or yeast data.

The exception: things people are taking anyway

A few items get included specifically because people are taking them and need calibration. Resveratrol is the canonical example. It survived no replication attempts in mammalian lifespan studies after the original Sinclair work, but billions of dollars of resveratrol gets sold every year. It deserves a page that says, in plain terms: the original story didn't replicate; here's why people kept taking it; here's the actual data.

Same logic for NAD+ precursors (NR, NMN), spermidine, fisetin, several others. We grade them honestly and explain the gap between what's on the bottle and what's in the literature.

The exclusion: hopium

We exclude things that are mostly speculation or commercial wishful thinking, even if they have a thin paper or two. The bar isn't "any study exists" — it's "the totality of human-relevant evidence is non-trivial."

Some things we've left off (and why):

Stem cell therapies. No good outcomes data outside niche orthopaedic indications. The longevity claims are commercial.

Plasma exchange / "young blood". One small trial in mild Alzheimer's (PLASMA, 2018) showed no benefit. The mouse parabiosis data inspired clinics; clinics are running ahead of the science.

Most "anti-aging peptides" sold online. Almost zero RCT data in humans. The mechanism papers are interesting; the consumer products are mostly research-grade compounds repackaged.

MitoQ. Mechanism is plausible (mitochondrial-targeted antioxidant). But two recent human RCTs (PREVENTABLE, 2023) showed no functional benefit despite oxidative-stress biomarker changes. We'll add it back if a hard-endpoint trial reads out positive.

Most adaptogens at high doses. Ashwagandha makes the list (we have it) because the cortisol/stress data is decent. Most others (rhodiola, ginseng, maca) lack the human RCT base.

The structural rule: tier the catalogue

73 items isn't useful if all 73 look equal. We use three tiers:

Tier 1 (Foundation). Universal basics. Strong evidence, low risk, low cost. Roughly 12 items: don't smoke, exercise, sleep enough, eat reasonably, take vitamin D if deficient, see a dentist. None of these will be on a Joe Rogan podcast next year.

Tier 2 (Targeted). For specific conditions. Real evidence, but only worth taking if it matches your situation. Statins for elevated ApoB. Metformin for T2D. SGLT2 inhibitors for heart failure. Most of the catalogue lives here.

Tier 3 (Frontier). Promising but human evidence pending. Rapamycin (best mammalian lifespan signal we have, no human longevity RCT yet). Senolytics (D+Q, fisetin — small human trials underway). Tier 3 items are the most exciting; they're also the most likely to disappoint.

The wizard uses these tiers to build a sensible stack. Most people end up with 5–8 Tier-1 items (the boring ones) and 1–3 Tier-2 items (the ones that match their conditions). Tier 3 only shows up if someone explicitly asks for "frontier" mode.

What a good intervention looks like

If you're trying to evaluate something we don't have on the list, here's the rubric we use:

  1. Is there at least one human RCT? If no, demand to see solid mammalian data plus a coherent mechanism.
  2. Do the trials measure something that matters? Mortality, hospitalisation, biomarker tied to a hard endpoint. Not "felt 12% less tired."
  3. Is the effect size honest? Relative risk reduction sounds bigger than absolute risk reduction. "44% reduction in MACE" sounds like a lot. From 2% to 1% is a 50% relative reduction; the absolute benefit is 1%. Both numbers matter.
  4. Are the trial sponsors and the conclusion-writers the same people? Industry funding doesn't disqualify a trial; it does mean the spin is asymmetric. Read the Limitations section in the published paper, not the press release.
  5. Has it replicated? Single trials are signals. Replication is evidence.

The supplement industry is built on (1) failing — buying compounds with mechanism papers and selling them as cures. We're allergic to that.

The honest answer

There aren't 700 things that extend healthy human lifespan. There are maybe 50-100 things with real evidence, and the top 12 do most of the work. We added the next 60 because they're worth knowing about (statins for someone with heart disease, GLP-1s for severe obesity, metformin for T2D), even if they're not universal.

If something promising is missing, it's probably because:

  • The data is there but only in worms / mice
  • The data is in humans but the trial was small or sponsor-biased
  • We just haven't gotten to it yet

Email-or-tweet at us if you think something's missing. We add new items roughly weekly.
