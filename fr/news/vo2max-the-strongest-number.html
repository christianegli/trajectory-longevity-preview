VO2max — the strongest single number you can know about your health

#vo2max#exercise#mortality

If you ran every test we have for cardiovascular health and could keep only one, the answer is cardiorespiratory fitness — operationally, your VO2max.

Mandsager and colleagues at the Cleveland Clinic looked at 122,007 patients who'd performed treadmill stress tests and survived to follow-up. They sorted them into fitness categories — low, below-average, above-average, high, elite — and compared mortality across categories. The results changed how the cardiovascular community thinks about risk factors.

Compared to elite cardiorespiratory fitness, low fitness was associated with 5x higher all-cause mortality. By comparison, smoking was associated with 1.41x higher mortality, diabetes 1.4x, end-stage renal disease 3.0x.

Cardiorespiratory fitness was the single strongest mortality predictor. Stronger than smoking. Stronger than diabetes. Stronger than every conventional risk factor in the model.

Either people who train run a different physiology, or training itself confers benefit, or both — but the magnitude of the association is too large to ignore.

What VO2max actually measures

VO2max is the maximum rate at which your body can deliver and use oxygen during intense exercise. It's measured in millilitres of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (mL/kg/min). The number is a global readout of:

  • Heart's pumping capacity (cardiac output)
  • Lung's gas-exchange efficiency (pulmonary diffusion)
  • Blood oxygen carrying (haemoglobin, blood volume)
  • Mitochondrial density in skeletal muscle (oxygen utilisation)
  • Capillary network delivering blood to working muscle

When any of these is impaired — by ageing, sedentary lifestyle, disease, or smoking — VO2max drops. When all of them are high, VO2max is high. So a single number captures something genuinely systemic.

The mortality benefit doesn't require elite fitness — it requires not being in the bottom quartile. Going from "low" to "average" cuts your mortality risk substantially. Going from "above-average" to "elite" still helps, but the marginal returns flatten. The biggest improvement is at the bottom of the distribution.

How to measure it

Best: Direct measurement at a sports-medicine clinic or university lab. Cycling or treadmill test with mask measuring oxygen consumption directly. Cost: $150-300. Accurate to within ~5%.

Good: Sub-maximal test in a clinical setting. Treadmill or bike test where you don't need to go to exhaustion; VO2max is estimated from heart-rate response. Less accurate (~10%) but safer for older adults.

Adequate: Field test at a track. Cooper test (12-minute run, distance covered estimates VO2max) or Rockport walk test (mile walk + heart rate response). Free; ~15% accuracy. Good enough to track changes over time even if absolute number is uncertain.

Acceptable: Smartwatch estimate. Garmin and Apple Watch generate VO2max estimates from heart-rate response during regular exercise. Accuracy varies (~10-15% error) but trend over months is informative. Free if you already have the device.

If you have the budget, get a clinic test — once. Then track changes with the watch.

How to train it

Two protocols, both with RCT support:

Zone 2 base. 3-4 hours/week at intensity where you can hold a conversation but not sing — heart rate roughly 60-70% of max, RPE 4-6 out of 10. Session length 45-90 minutes. Cycling, walking-uphill, slow running, swimming. This builds the mitochondrial and capillary base. Most of your training time should live here.

High-intensity intervals. 1-2 sessions/week. Norwegian 4x4 protocol is the canonical: 4 minutes at 90-95% max HR, 3 minutes recovery, repeat 4 times. Total ~30 minutes session including warm-up. Mandsager's data and the Generation 100 trial both support this addition.

Mandsager's cohort showed mortality benefit at any fitness level above "low" — meaning even people training only Zone 2 saw meaningful gains. The intervals add VO2max ceiling specifically; they're not strictly required for mortality benefit.

How fast do you change it

Untrained adults typically gain 10-15% VO2max over 8-12 weeks of structured training. Older adults gain slightly less but still meaningfully — Generation 100 in Norway showed 70-77 year-olds improved VO2max by 5-8% over 5 years of structured training, while sedentary controls lost ~2%.

The drop from peak with age is roughly 1% per decade if you stay active and 5-10% per decade if you don't. The decline is mostly use it or lose it, not "ageing per se" — meaning the trajectory is largely controllable.

What numbers to aim for

If you're under 50: target the top quartile for your age. If you're 50+: target the top half. If you're 70+ and currently sedentary: target above the bottom quartile within 6-12 months.

These aren't athlete-level numbers. They're the threshold where the mortality curve starts flattening. You don't need elite fitness; you need to leave the bottom of the distribution.

What if you can't train

Several conditions limit training but don't preclude it:

  • Severe COPD: pulmonary rehab programmes show measurable VO2max gains even with lung-function ceilings
  • Severe heart failure: cardiac rehab is associated with mortality benefit beyond medication
  • Severe knee osteoarthritis: swimming or stationary cycling avoids impact
  • Recent CV event: structured supervised programmes are evidence-supported and medication-augmenting

In every case, the answer is some movement, structured. The only people in the literature who don't show fitness gains are those who don't train.

The summary

Of all the numbers your doctor measures, VO2max is the one most predictive of how long you'll live. It's the most modifiable cardiovascular risk factor — more responsive to training than blood pressure or LDL are to medication. The training works at every age. The cost is mostly time.

Get it measured. Then train it. Most people will gain meaningfully in 12 weeks of consistent work.
