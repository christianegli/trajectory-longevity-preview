Bottom Line

Eat mushrooms. Especially shiitake, oyster, king oyster, lion's mane. Supplement form is reasonable but mushroom intake is cheaper and more pleasant.

What this means for you

Ergothioneine is an unusual amino acid found almost exclusively in mushrooms. It has a dedicated transporter that pumps it specifically into mitochondria, where it acts as an antioxidant. The mechanism is fascinating; the human longevity data is modest. Easy answer: eat mushrooms — especially shiitake, oyster, and king oyster — a few times a week.

How to actually do this

  • Eat mushrooms 3+ times per week. Best sources by ergothioneine content: oyster mushrooms > king oyster > shiitake > maitake > white button.
  • 100 g of cooked oyster mushrooms provides ~5–7 mg ergothioneine. White button mushrooms are lower (~1 mg/100g) but still useful.
  • Cooking does not destroy ergothioneine. Sauté, roast, add to soups and stir-fries.
  • Lion's mane mushrooms have less ergothioneine but other interesting compounds — fine to vary.
  • Supplement option: 5–30 mg/day if you don't eat mushrooms. Some people just don't tolerate them.
  • No notable side effects or interactions reported.
Rough cost: Mushrooms $5–$20/week from a grocery store. Supplement $20–$40/month.

Why it works

Most antioxidants get scattered randomly throughout your cells. Ergothioneine is different: your cells have a specific transporter (OCTN1) that recognizes it and concentrates it inside the parts of cells that produce energy and accumulate the most damage — mitochondria. Mushrooms are essentially the only meaningful dietary source. Cohort studies link higher mushroom intake to lower cognitive decline; ergothioneine is one likely active ingredient.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Unique amino acid concentrated in mitochondria via dedicated transporter (OCTN1). Powerful antioxidant; protects mtDNA. Highest dietary source: oyster, shiitake, king oyster mushrooms.

Evidence Summary

Cohort data shows higher serum ergothioneine correlates with lower all-cause and CV mortality. Mechanistic rationale strong (mitochondria-targeted antioxidant with active uptake), but few RCTs. Found in mushrooms, especially exotic varieties.
