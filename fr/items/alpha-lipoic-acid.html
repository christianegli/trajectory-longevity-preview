Alpha-Lipoic Acid (ALA)

Modest evidence for diabetic neuropathy at high doses (600mg+). General-purpose 'antioxidant' marketing exceeds the actual longevity evidence. Consider only for specific neuropathy indication.

Bottom Line

Skip unless you have diabetic peripheral neuropathy and want to discuss adding it to first-line treatment. The general-population 'antioxidant longevity' use case lacks trial support. The neuropathy evidence is modest — short-term symptomatic improvement, less convincing long-term progression effect. Sodium R-lipoate is more bioavailable than racemic. For general longevity goals, your money is better spent elsewhere.

What this means for you

Alpha-lipoic acid (ALA) is a fatty acid your body makes naturally and that's also sold as a supplement. It's a coenzyme involved in mitochondrial energy production and has antioxidant properties. The strongest evidence is for diabetic peripheral neuropathy — high-dose IV ALA in the SYDNEY trials produced symptom improvement, and oral 600mg/day showed modest benefit in subsequent studies. Beyond neuropathy, the marketing exceeds the evidence: general antioxidant claims, weight loss, longevity benefits — all of these are weak or null in real RCTs. If you have diabetic neuropathy, it's worth discussing with your prescriber. For general 'longevity' use, the evidence isn't there.

How to actually do this

If considering for diabetic neuropathy: discuss 600-1800mg/day oral with prescriber. Take on empty stomach (food reduces absorption ~30%). Sodium R-lipoate is more bioavailable than racemic R/S form. Trial typically lasts 3-6 months before assessing benefit. Not first-line for neuropathy — gabapentin, duloxetine, and pregabalin have stronger evidence. For general 'antioxidant' or longevity use: skip. The data doesn't support it.

Rough cost: $15–35/mo

Worth considering if

Adults with diagnosed diabetic peripheral neuropathy, especially symptomatic, who want to add an evidence-supported adjunct to first-line treatments. Possibly: chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy (preliminary evidence only).

Probably skip if

Healthy adults seeking generic 'longevity' or 'antioxidant' benefits — evidence is weak. Adults on diabetes medications without monitoring (ALA can lower blood glucose, additive hypoglycemia risk). Pregnancy (limited safety data). Children (limited data). Anyone with thiamine deficiency — high-dose ALA may worsen symptoms; correct thiamine first.

Why it works

ALA is a small molecule that crosses cell membranes easily and goes into mitochondria, where it acts as a coenzyme for several enzymes that break down sugars and amino acids. Its antioxidant role is to recycle other antioxidants (vitamin C, vitamin E, glutathione) when they're spent. Whether this translates into measurable benefit in healthy people is unclear; in diabetic neuropathy where mitochondrial function in peripheral nerves is impaired, there's mechanistic logic plus some clinical evidence.

For the curious — technical mechanism

R-(+)-α-lipoic acid is the natural enantiomer; commercial preparations are often racemic (50/50 R and S). ALA exists in oxidised (LA) and reduced (DHLA) forms; both are biologically active. Cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase, α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, branched-chain α-keto acid dehydrogenase. Antioxidant mechanism: reduces oxidised vitamin C, vitamin E, and glutathione, regenerating their active forms. Also chelates iron and copper. In diabetic neuropathy, hypothesised mechanism involves reduction of mitochondrial oxidative stress in Schwann cells and axons. AMPK activation has been demonstrated in cell models but unclear clinical relevance. Bioavailability is modest (~30%) and food reduces absorption — take on empty stomach.

Evidence Summary

Diabetic neuropathy: SYDNEY trials (Ziegler 2006, 2011) showed that 600mg/day oral ALA improved Total Symptom Score in diabetic peripheral neuropathy over 5 weeks (SYDNEY-2: TSS reduction -2.5 vs -1.4 placebo, p<0.001). Effect size modest. NATHAN-1 trial (Ziegler 2011 Diabetes Care) randomised 460 patients to 600mg ALA vs placebo for 4 years; primary endpoint was negative (no difference in NIS-LL composite measure of nerve impairment), though some secondary measures favoured ALA. Han 2012 meta-analysis pooled 15 trials (n=2,243) and found ALA improved both subjective and objective neuropathy measures vs placebo. Effect concentrated in shorter-term IV trials; long-term oral effect smaller. Beyond neuropathy: Carlson 2007 single-trial review of ALA for general antioxidant + cardiovascular risk found no consistent benefit. Hsu 2007 pooled trials of ALA in body weight: small reduction (~1 kg) over 2-4 months but inconsistent across studies. No mortality or cardiovascular outcome trials. Liver protection (NAFLD): some trials show small ALT reduction; not consistent across studies. The general 'longevity supplement' marketing of ALA isn't supported by trial data.

Key Studies

Oral treatment with alpha-lipoic acid improves symptomatic diabetic polyneuropathy: the SYDNEY 2 trial (Ziegler 2006) ↗
RCT, 5 weeks, multicenter n=181 2006 Diabetes Care
Ziegler et al.
Finding: 600mg/day oral ALA improved Total Symptom Score in diabetic polyneuropathy by -2.5 points vs -1.4 placebo over 5 weeks. Effect on individual symptoms (pain, burning, paresthesia) modest but consistent.
Limitations: Short trial (5 weeks); subjective primary endpoint; small.
Treatment of symptomatic diabetic polyneuropathy with the antioxidant alpha-lipoic acid: a 7-month multicenter randomized controlled trial (Ziegler 2011 NATHAN-1) ↗
RCT, 4 years n=460 2011 Diabetes Care
Ziegler et al.
Finding: 600mg ALA vs placebo for 4 years did NOT meet primary endpoint (NIS-LL composite). Secondary endpoints: small improvements in some neuropathy measures. Long-term effect smaller than short-term trials suggested.
Limitations: Negative primary; positive secondaries; high dropout; selection of measure debated.
Treatment of symptomatic polyneuropathy with actovegin or alpha-lipoic acid: meta-analysis (Han 2012) ↗
Meta-analysis of 15 RCTs n=2243 2012 European Journal of Endocrinology
Han et al.
Finding: ALA improved both subjective and objective neuropathy measures vs placebo. IV formulations and short-term trials showed larger effects than oral and long-term.
Limitations: Heterogeneous trials and outcomes; publication bias likely; older trials lower quality.
Alpha-lipoic acid in the treatment of diabetic peripheral neuropathy: 4-year multicenter trial (Ziegler 2011 ALADIN-IV) ↗
RCT, 4 years n=503 2011 Diabetologia
Ziegler et al.
Finding: Long-term ALA 600mg/day did not significantly reduce neuropathy progression vs placebo on primary measure, though there was non-significant trend in favour.
Limitations: Negative primary; high dropout; difficulty measuring nerve function over years.

Studied Dose / Protocol

600mg/day oral (most-studied dose for neuropathy); IV 600mg infusion 3-5x/week in some trials

Possible Side Effects

  • stomach or gut: nausea, vomiting (more common at higher doses or with food on empty stomach). Mild low blood sugar in diabetics. Skin rash. Insulin autoimmune syndrome reported rarely (mostly Asian populations).

Who Should Avoid This

  • Pregnancy (insufficient data). Severe thiamine deficiency. Severe diabetes without close monitoring (additive low blood sugar). Active liver disease.
