Resistance Training

Lean mass and strength are independent mortality predictors. Both decline without training.

Bottom Line

Non-negotiable for healthy aging. The strongest interventions for the back third of life: maintain muscle, maintain strength, maintain power. Skip squats and you'll regret it at 80.

What this means for you

Lifting heavy things — squats, deadlifts, presses, rows — twice a week is non-negotiable past 40. Muscle and strength are independent predictors of how long you live. They both decline relentlessly without training, and that decline is what eventually steals your independence in old age.

How to actually do this

  • Train 2–3 times per week, 45–75 min per session. Non-negotiable.
  • Focus on compound lifts: squat (or leg press), deadlift (or hinge), bench/overhead press, pull-ups (or rows). These hit the most muscle for the least time.
  • Progress gradually. Add a small amount of weight or one rep when last week's weight feels manageable. This 'progressive overload' is the entire game.
  • If you're new: hire a coach for 4–8 sessions to learn form. Money well spent — bad form for years is worse than no training.
  • Don't skip legs. Lower-body strength tracks closely with mortality and fall risk.
  • Eat enough protein: ~1.6 g per kg of bodyweight per day (more if older). Train fed; sleep 7–9h.
  • Soreness is fine. Sharp pain is not — back off and reassess.
Rough cost: Gym membership $30–$80/month. Or one good barbell + plates + rack at home: $800–$2000 one-time.

Worth considering if

Everyone. Especially anyone over 40, anyone losing strength or mobility, anyone with osteopenia or family history of frailty.

Probably skip if

Nobody — but anyone with cardiac disease, recent surgery, or active injury should clear with a doctor and ideally work with a physical therapist or qualified coach first.

Helps with

Muscle loss (sarcopenia) Frailty Bone loss / osteoporosis Diabetes / metabolic Heart disease Cognitive decline

Why it works

When you load a muscle hard enough to challenge it, you trigger a repair-and-grow signal. Cells synthesize new protein, motor neurons recruit more fibers, bones lay down more mineral, tendons stiffen. Without that signal, the body assumes you don't need the tissue and quietly takes it back — about 1% of muscle and 3% of strength per year after 40 if you don't train. Compound that for 30 years and you can't get out of a chair. Train and you keep the reserve.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Mechanical loading drives muscle protein synthesis, motor unit recruitment, bone mineralization, and tendon/ligament adaptation. Maintains the metabolic + structural reserves that determine independent function in old age.

Evidence Summary

Resistance training is one of the few interventions with evidence for both mortality reduction and direct preservation of independent function in old age. Saeidifard 2019 meta-analysis: regular resistance training associated with 21% lower all-cause mortality and 17% lower CV mortality. Effect appears partially independent of aerobic exercise. García-Hermoso 2018 meta (n=1.9M): higher muscle strength = 31% lower all-cause mortality even after adjusting for cardiorespiratory fitness — strength has unique mortality signal. Leong 2015 (PURE study): grip strength was a stronger predictor of mortality than systolic BP. LIFTMOR RCT (Watson 2018) demonstrated heavy resistance + impact training increases BMD and function in postmenopausal women with osteoporosis — safely. Practical (NSCA 2019 position statement): 2-3×/week, compound movements, 60-80% 1RM, 2-3 sets of 6-12 reps, progressive overload. Power training (faster movement) specifically improves fall risk.

Key Studies

The association of resistance training with mortality (Saeidifard et al.) ↗
Meta-analysis n=480000 2019 European Journal of Preventive Cardiology
Saeidifard et al.
Finding: Resistance training reduced all-cause mortality 21% and CV mortality 19%, INDEPENDENT of aerobic exercise.
Limitations: Heterogeneous protocols.
Prognostic value of grip strength (Leong et al.) ↗
Cohort n=139691 2015 Lancet
Leong et al.
Finding: Each 5kg decrease in grip strength = 16% increased all-cause mortality. Stronger predictor than systolic BP.
Limitations: Observational.
Muscle strength and all-cause, cardiovascular, and cancer mortality ↗
Meta-analysis (38 cohorts) n=1907580 2018 Arch Phys Med Rehabil
García-Hermoso et al.
Finding: Higher muscle strength associated with 31% lower all-cause mortality and 14% lower cancer mortality, independent of CRF. Effect remained after adjustment for cardiorespiratory fitness — strength has unique signal.
Limitations: Observational; muscle strength tests heterogeneous (grip vs leg vs composite); confounding by health status.
High-Intensity Resistance and Impact Training Improves Bone Mineral Density (LIFTMOR) ↗
RCT n=101 2018 J Bone Miner Res
Watson et al.
Finding: 8 months of high-intensity resistance + impact training in postmenopausal women with low bone mass increased lumbar spine BMD by 2.9%, femoral neck BMD by 0.3%, and improved functional performance. Demonstrated heavy-load training is safe and effective in osteoporosis.
Limitations: Small sample; one center; supervised training (less generalizable to home settings).
Resistance Training for Older Adults: Position Statement (NSCA) ↗
Position statement / evidence synthesis 2019 J Strength Cond Res
Fragala et al.
Finding: Recommends 2-3×/week, multi-joint compound movements, progressive overload (60-80% 1RM, 2-3 sets, 6-12 reps) for older adults. Evidence supports power training (faster concentric phase) for falls prevention specifically.
Limitations: Consensus document; not a single trial.

Studied Dose / Protocol

2-3 sessions/week, compound movements, progressive overload.
