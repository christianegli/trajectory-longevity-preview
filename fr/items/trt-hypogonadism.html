Testosterone Replacement (TRT) for hypogonadism

For men with confirmed hypogonadism (low T + symptoms): improves muscle mass, mood, sexual function. TRAVERSE 2023 NEJM established CV safety. Not for general 'low T' marketing.

Bottom Line

Real medicine for confirmed hypogonadism with symptoms. Not a longevity supplement. The TRAVERSE 2023 trial established cardiovascular safety. Diagnose properly: two morning T levels + consistent symptoms. Don't prescribe to men with normal T 'for optimization' — that's medicalisation, not medicine. If you're considering TRT, work with a clinician who follows the Endocrine Society guideline, not a clinic that prescribes TRT to nearly everyone who walks in. Get fertility plans clarified before starting (or use hCG/clomiphene alternatives if fertility matters).

What this means for you

Testosterone declines with age in men — about 1% per year after 40 — but the rate varies enormously between individuals. The clinical question is when low testosterone is causing symptoms that warrant treatment. TRT done correctly (for men with confirmed biochemical hypogonadism plus symptoms) consistently improves muscle mass, sexual function, mood, and bone density. The historical concern that TRT might increase cardiovascular events was substantially addressed by the TRAVERSE trial (Lincoff 2023 NEJM, n=5,246), which found no increase in major cardiovascular events. The bigger problem is over-prescription: men with normal-range testosterone or borderline-low values without symptoms get prescribed TRT for vague concerns, which doesn't improve outcomes and creates lifetime medication dependence. TRT is real medicine for real hypogonadism, not a longevity supplement.

How to actually do this

Diagnosis FIRST: at least two morning total testosterone levels (8-10am, fasting if possible) below 264 ng/dL (Endocrine Society threshold) or 300 ng/dL (more conservative threshold) AND symptoms consistent with hypogonadism (decreased libido, ED, decreased muscle mass, fatigue, depression, decreased morning erections). Symptoms alone don't warrant TRT; biochemistry alone doesn't warrant TRT. Both together do. Common formulations: testosterone cypionate IM 100-200mg every 1-2 weeks; testosterone gel 25-50mg/day; testosterone enanthate; subcutaneous pellets every 3-6 months. Monitoring: total testosterone, hematocrit (target <54%), PSA, lipids, mood, sexual function — at 3 months, 6 months, then annually. Stop if no symptomatic improvement after 6 months at adequate dose. NOT for: men with high-normal testosterone seeking optimization; men trying to conceive (suppresses spermatogenesis); men with active prostate cancer or untreated severe sleep apnea.

Rough cost: $30–200/mo

Worth considering if

Men with confirmed biochemical hypogonadism (two morning T levels in deficient range) AND symptoms. Particularly relevant for men with surgical/medical hypogonadism (cancer survivors after orchiectomy, congenital causes, pituitary disease). Men with persistent post-pubertal testosterone deficiency.

Probably skip if

Men with normal-range testosterone seeking 'optimization'. Men trying to conceive (suppresses fertility; can take 6+ months to recover after stopping). Men with active prostate cancer (relative contraindication). Untreated severe obstructive sleep apnea (TRT can worsen). Men with hematocrit >54% baseline. Men with breast cancer. Men with severe untreated CV disease. Men with elevated baseline PSA without urology evaluation. Men with primary hypogonadism wanting to preserve fertility (consider hCG/clomiphene alternatives that maintain testicular function).

Why it works

Testosterone is the primary male sex hormone, made mainly in the testes. It supports muscle protein synthesis, libido and erectile function, mood and energy regulation, bone density, and red blood cell production. Some men experience age-related decline that crosses into clinical deficiency (hypogonadism), often combined with symptoms like loss of libido, fatigue, depression, loss of muscle and increased fat. Replacing testosterone in these men brings physiology closer to normal range and resolves many symptoms. Replacing testosterone in men who *aren't* deficient doesn't add benefit and may cause harm.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Testosterone signals via androgen receptor (AR) in tissues with high AR expression: skeletal muscle, bone, brain, prostate, and male reproductive organs. Negative feedback via hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis: exogenous testosterone suppresses GnRH/LH/FSH, leading to testicular shutdown and reversible (usually) infertility. Aromatisation to estradiol is part of testosterone's effect — necessary for bone health, libido, and mood; over-suppression of estradiol via aromatase inhibitors is a common iatrogenic error. Erythropoiesis stimulation via HIF-2α/EPO pathway can produce polycythemia (erythrocytosis) requiring monitoring (hematocrit). Prostate effects are receptor-mediated; the historical fear of TRT promoting prostate cancer has not been borne out in modern evidence (TRAVERSE 2023 PSA monitoring did not show prostate cancer increase). Cardiovascular safety: TRAVERSE showed no increase in MACE; isolated signals for atrial fibrillation and pulmonary embolism warrant attention but absolute risk small.

Evidence Summary

TRAVERSE trial (Lincoff 2023 NEJM): Largest cardiovascular safety trial of TRT, randomised 5,246 men with hypogonadism and CV risk to TRT or placebo for median 22 months. Primary endpoint of MACE (CV death, MI, stroke): HR 0.96 (non-inferior). Established CV safety in the population most worried about. T-Trials (Snyder 2016 NEJM): coordinated set of 7 RCTs in older men with low T evaluated specific outcomes — sexual function (improved), physical function (modest improvement, less than expected), vitality (modest improvement), cognition (no improvement), bone density (improved by DXA), anemia (improved). Effect sizes generally modest; not the dramatic improvement TRT marketing implies. T-Trials hematocrit safety: small absolute increase in erythrocytosis vs placebo. Bhasin 2018 Endocrine Society Guidelines: comprehensive systematic review; recommended treatment of men with consistent symptoms + biochemical evidence; against treatment in men with normal-range T or lacking symptoms. TRiUMPH 2021 (Schoenfeld) meta-analysis of body composition outcomes: TRT increases lean body mass by ~1-3 kg and decreases fat mass by ~1-2 kg vs placebo over 12 months in hypogonadal men. PSA/prostate cancer: TRAVERSE PSA monitoring found small increase in PSA without increase in prostate cancer detection over 22 months (analysis ongoing). The mortality data: cohort studies of men with treated vs untreated hypogonadism (Shores 2012, Ramasamy 2015) show survival benefit, but selection bias is significant; RCT mortality data is short-term and underpowered.

Key Studies

Cardiovascular Safety of Testosterone-Replacement Therapy (TRAVERSE, Lincoff 2023) ↗
Multicenter RCT, non-inferiority n=5246 2023 New England Journal of Medicine
Lincoff et al.
Finding: TRT non-inferior to placebo for major CV events (HR 0.96) in hypogonadal men with CV risk over median 22 months. Established CV safety. Small increase in atrial fibrillation and pulmonary embolism noted.
Limitations: Specific high-CV-risk population; intermediate follow-up; designed for safety not efficacy.
Effects of Testosterone Treatment in Older Men (T-Trials, Snyder 2016) ↗
Coordinated set of 7 RCTs n=790 2016 New England Journal of Medicine
Snyder et al.
Finding: TRT improved sexual function, walking distance (modest), vitality, hemoglobin, bone density. No improvement in cognition. Effect sizes modest overall.
Limitations: Older population (65+); 1-year duration; effects modest, not transformative.
Testosterone therapy in men with hypogonadism: Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline (Bhasin 2018) ↗
Systematic review / clinical practice guideline 2018 Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism
Bhasin et al.
Finding: Recommended treatment for men with both biochemical hypogonadism AND consistent symptoms. Against treatment of men with normal T or lacking symptoms. Outlined monitoring framework.
Limitations: Guideline pre-dates TRAVERSE; updates expected.
Effects of testosterone treatment on body composition: meta-analysis (Bhasin 2001 + subsequent updates) ↗
Original RCT + subsequent meta-analyses 2001 Multiple
Bhasin et al.; meta-updates ongoing
Finding: TRT increases lean body mass 1-3 kg, decreases fat mass 1-2 kg, increases strength modestly in hypogonadal men over 12 months.
Limitations: Effect sizes vary by trial; effects in eugonadal men minimal.

Possible Side Effects

  • Erythrocytosis (hematocrit >54%, requires phlebotomy or dose reduction). Acne. Mild scalp hair thinning in genetically predisposed. Sleep apnea worsening (treat OSA first). Modest LDL increase, HDL decrease. Mild fluid retention. Possible atrial fibrillation and blood clot in the lung signal (TRAVERSE). Suppressed sperm production (reversible but slow).

Who Should Avoid This

  • Active prostate cancer. Severe untreated obstructive sleep apnea. Hematocrit >54% baseline. Breast cancer. Active or recent heart or blood-vessel event (TRAVERSE excluded recent ACS). Untreated too many red blood cells. Pregnancy in partner trying to conceive (T can transfer via skin contact with gels).
