Ginger

Bottom Line

Quietly useful food. Strong evidence for nausea (pregnancy, chemo, motion sickness) and modest evidence for HbA1c and chronic inflammation. Not a longevity headline, but a solid kitchen-pharmacy item — especially for women with morning sickness (where ACOG endorses it) and T2D patients adding to standard care. Not a replacement for ondansetron in cancer therapy, but a meaningful adjunct.

What this means for you

Ginger has the unusual property of being a food with multiple high-quality RCTs showing real clinical effects. The strongest evidence is for nausea — chemotherapy-induced, pregnancy-related, and motion sickness — where it consistently performs as well as some prescription antiemetics. Beyond nausea: meta-analyses show modest reductions in HbA1c (in T2D), CRP (inflammation marker), menstrual pain, and joint pain in osteoarthritis. The food is cheap, low-risk, and has actual clinical utility. Make it part of cooking; consider supplement form for specific conditions.

How to actually do this

1-3g/day of fresh ginger root (a thumb-sized piece adds ~3g) added to cooking, tea, or smoothies. For specific conditions: 500-1000mg standardised ginger extract 2-3x daily for nausea (chemo, pregnancy, motion). For HbA1c/inflammation: 1-2g/day for 8+ weeks. Ginger tea is a reasonable food approach; commercial ginger ales are mostly sugar with negligible ginger.

Rough cost: $5–15/mo

Worth considering if

Pregnant women with morning sickness (extensive safety data; ACOG-supported). Cancer patients undergoing emetogenic chemo. Adults with T2D wanting a low-risk dietary addition. People with osteoarthritis or chronic inflammation looking for adjunct support. Anyone who likes the taste — easy to incorporate.

Probably skip if

Anticoagulant users (theoretical bleeding risk; reports rare but reasonable caution). People with gallstones (ginger stimulates bile secretion). Severe reflux. Pre-surgery (stop 1 week before, like garlic).

Helps with

Diabetes / metabolic Chronic inflammation

Why it works

Ginger contains compounds called gingerols, shogaols, and zingerone. These molecules: (1) block 5-HT3 serotonin receptors in the gut (similar to anti-nausea drugs like ondansetron), (2) have anti-inflammatory effects similar to a mild aspirin, (3) modestly improve insulin signalling, (4) affect platelet function. The clinical effect is real but mostly modest — it's a useful adjunct, not a primary treatment.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Active phenolic compounds: 6-gingerol (fresh), 6-shogaol (dried/cooked, from gingerol dehydration), zingerone, paradol. Antinausea: 5-HT3 antagonism + accelerated gastric emptying + central effects on chemoreceptor trigger zone. Anti-inflammatory: COX-2 inhibition, NF-κB suppression, reduction in PGE2 and leukotriene synthesis. Glucose: increased GLUT4 translocation and modest improvement in insulin signalling via AMPK. Antiplatelet effect via thromboxane synthase inhibition (clinically minor at food doses).

Evidence Summary

Nausea evidence is the strongest. Marx 2013 (Annals of Oncology) systematic review of ginger in chemotherapy-induced nausea found significant reduction in CINV severity across 5 RCTs. Bossi 2017 (Annals of Oncology) showed 1g/day standardised ginger reduced acute nausea in 251 cancer patients receiving emetogenic chemo. Pregnancy: Vutyavanich 2001 (Obstetrics & Gynecology) showed ginger 1g/day for 4 days reduced nausea and vomiting in 70 women with morning sickness. ACOG endorses ginger as first-line non-pharmacological treatment for nausea/vomiting of pregnancy. Glucose: Daily 2024 (Phytotherapy Research) meta-analysed 11 RCTs (n=786) of ginger in T2D and pre-diabetes — found HbA1c reduction of 0.46% over 8-12 weeks, fasting glucose reduction of 16 mg/dL. Pourmasoumi 2018 (Phytotherapy Research) meta-analysis of 12 inflammation trials (n=590) found ginger reduced CRP by 1.39 mg/L and TNF-α by 1.16 pg/mL. Bartels 2015 (Osteoarthritis Cartilage) Cochrane-style review of 5 RCTs in knee/hip OA showed modest pain reduction (effect size ~0.30) — smaller than NSAIDs but with much better tolerability. Daily 2015 (Pain Medicine) meta-analysis of 7 dysmenorrhea RCTs found ginger as effective as NSAIDs for menstrual pain.

Key Studies

Ginger for nausea and vomiting in chemotherapy: systematic review (Marx 2013) ↗
Systematic review of 5 RCTs 2013 Annals of Oncology
Marx et al.
Finding: Ginger reduced chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting severity across multiple trials. Effect comparable to some 5-HT3 antagonists at lower side-effect cost.
Limitations: Heterogeneous dosing; small individual trial sizes; some open-label.
Effects of ginger on glycemic indices in type 2 diabetic patients: meta-analysis (Daily 2024) ↗
Meta-analysis of 11 RCTs n=786 2024 Phytotherapy Research
Daily et al.
Finding: Ginger 1-2g/day for 8-12 weeks reduced HbA1c by 0.46% and fasting glucose by 16 mg/dL in T2D and pre-diabetes patients.
Limitations: Mostly Iranian/Asian cohorts; heterogeneous duration; surrogate endpoint.
Ginger in chemotherapy-induced nausea (Bossi 2017) ↗
RCT, double-blind n=251 2017 Annals of Oncology
Bossi et al.
Finding: 1g/day standardised ginger reduced acute nausea (Day 1) in cancer patients on emetogenic chemo. Effect on delayed nausea less clear.
Limitations: Standardised commercial product; one cancer population.
Effects of ginger supplementation on inflammatory markers: meta-analysis (Pourmasoumi 2018) ↗
Meta-analysis of 12 RCTs n=590 2018 Phytotherapy Research
Pourmasoumi et al.
Finding: Ginger reduced CRP by 1.39 mg/L and TNF-α by 1.16 pg/mL across 12 RCTs.
Limitations: Surrogate endpoints; heterogeneous populations.

Studied Dose / Protocol

1-2g/day fresh ginger or extract (most RCTs); 0.5-1g standardised extract for nausea (multiple trials)

Possible Side Effects

  • Heartburn, mild stomach or gut upset at higher doses. Theoretical bleeding risk (clinical reports rare). Lower blood pressure modestly (relevant if you're already on antihypertensives — usually beneficial).

Who Should Avoid This

  • Active anticoagulation (caution, not absolute). Gallstones. Pre-surgery (stop 1 week before). Severe severe acid reflux.
