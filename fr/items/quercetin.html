Bottom Line

Without dasatinib, quercetin alone is barely interesting. The food-source value is real (eat apples, onions). Pill form: skip.

What this means for you

Quercetin is the famous flavonoid in apples, onions, and capers. As a senolytic, it only works combined with a chemo drug called dasatinib (D+Q protocol used in research). Solo quercetin pills are unimpressive. The food-source benefit is real — eat apples, onions, kale. The pill: skip.

How to actually do this

  • Eat the foods: apples (with skin), red onions, capers, kale, dark berries. Daily.
  • If you're set on supplementing: 500–1000 mg/day with food. Don't expect much from quercetin alone.
  • The D+Q senolytic protocol is research-only; not a do-it-yourself thing. Dasatinib is a chemo drug with serious side effects.
  • If you want a senolytic bet that works as a single agent: fisetin has more evidence (separate entry).
  • Watch for drug interactions: quercetin inhibits CYP3A4 — collisions with statins, BP meds, antidepressants are possible.
  • Skip if on anticoagulants or cyclosporine.
Rough cost: $10–$25/month for capsules. Onions and apples are cheaper and better.

Worth considering if

Pretty much nobody as a supplement. Everyone for the food sources.

Probably skip if

On anticoagulants, cyclosporine, or multiple prescriptions without checking interactions.

Why it works

Senolytics are drugs that selectively kill 'zombie' senescent cells — old damaged cells that won't die and leak inflammation. The most studied senolytic protocol is Dasatinib + Quercetin (D+Q). Critically: in the experiments, neither alone works well; the combination does. Without dasatinib, quercetin is just another flavonoid with mild antioxidant effects. The apple-and-onion food matrix has its own benefits, but those don't transfer cleanly to capsules.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Flavonoid with antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and weak senolytic effects (potent only when combined with dasatinib). Found in apples, onions, capers, kale.

Evidence Summary

Alone, quercetin has modest effects on BP, allergic symptoms, and inflammation. The 'D+Q' senolytic protocol (dasatinib 100mg + quercetin 1000mg) has small open-label data in IPF, diabetic kidney disease, and Alzheimer's. As monotherapy, no significant longevity signal. Bioavailability poor; phytosome formulations help.
