Higher Protein Intake (1.2-1.6 g/kg)

Protein needs rise with age, training, weight loss, and menopause. 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day is the practical longevity range for most adults over 50.

Bottom Line

Most longevity nutrition advice underplays protein. Aim for enough protein to preserve muscle while keeping the rest of the diet high-fiber and cardiometabolically sane. For many adults over 50, that means 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day, split across meals, paired with resistance training.

What this means for you

Protein is not a bodybuilding side quest. It is the raw material for muscle, bone, immune proteins, enzymes, and recovery. With age, muscle becomes less sensitive to small protein doses, so the old RDA of 0.8 g/kg/day often under-shoots for preserving strength and independence. The practical target changes by age, sex, training, weight loss, and kidney function.

How to actually do this

  • Under 50 and sedentary: 0.8-1.2 g/kg/day is usually enough if body composition and appetite are stable.
  • Training or trying to gain muscle: 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day is the evidence-based sports nutrition range.
  • Over 50: use 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day as the practical target, especially if strength, appetite, or weight is changing.
  • During weight loss: lean toward 1.6 g/kg/day, sometimes higher if already lean and resistance training, to preserve lean mass.
  • Per meal: aim for 25-40 g protein, or about 0.4 g/kg/meal, across 3-4 meals. Breakfast is the meal most people under-dose.
  • Women: protein matters more after menopause because estrogen loss accelerates changes in muscle, bone, and body composition. Pair protein with heavy resistance training, not dieting alone.
  • Men: absolute grams are often higher because body mass is higher, but the per-kg target is similar. Do not use protein as an excuse for low fiber or high saturated fat.
  • Plant-based diets: totally workable, but plan density. Soy foods, legumes, seitan, tofu/tempeh, pea/rice blends, and higher total grams help overcome lower leucine density.
  • Kidney caveat: normal kidneys do not need protein restriction, but eGFR <30 or medical kidney disease changes the equation. Get clinician guidance.
Rough cost: Mostly food costs. Whey protein supplement: $20-40/month for 30g/day. Plant protein blends similar.

Worth considering if

Adults over 50; postmenopausal women; anyone doing resistance training; people losing weight; vegetarians/vegans with low protein density; adults with low appetite, frailty risk, or recent illness/injury.

Probably skip if

Advanced chronic kidney disease not on dialysis, inherited protein-handling disorders, or clinician-prescribed protein restriction. People already eating enough protein do not need powders just to chase a number.

Why it works

Your muscles maintain themselves by constantly breaking down and rebuilding protein. As you age, your muscles become 'resistant' to the rebuilding signal — you need more protein per meal to trigger the same response. If you don't get enough, your body slowly loses muscle (sarcopenia), which is the main driver of frailty and falls in old age. Strength training amplifies the rebuild signal; protein gives the building blocks.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Anabolic resistance in aging muscle requires higher leucine concentrations (>2.5g leucine per meal) to maximally activate mTORC1-S6K1 signaling and muscle protein synthesis. Per-meal threshold ~0.4 g/kg of high-quality (high-leucine) protein. Distribution matters: 4 evenly spaced meals at 0.4 g/kg outperform skewed (low breakfast, high dinner) distributions. Whey protein has highest leucine density (~10%) and fastest digestion kinetics.

Evidence Summary

PROT-AGE recommends at least 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day for healthy adults over 65, and 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day for many older adults with acute or chronic disease. ISSN recommends 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day for exercising individuals, with higher ranges during energy restriction to preserve lean mass. The mechanism is anabolic resistance: older muscle needs a stronger amino-acid/leucine signal per meal. Sex differences are mostly contextual rather than a different protein biology: postmenopausal women face accelerated bone/muscle/body-composition shifts, while men often need more absolute grams because of larger body size. The practical prescription is per-kg dosing plus per-meal distribution, not one universal gram number.

Key Studies

Evidence-based recommendations for optimal dietary protein intake in older people (PROT-AGE Study Group, Bauer 2013) ↗
Consensus position paper (review of multiple RCTs/cohorts) 2013 J Am Med Dir Assoc
Bauer J, Biolo G, Cederholm T, Cesari M, Cruz-Jentoft AJ, Morley JE, Phillips S, Sieber C, Stehle P, Teta D, Visvanathan R, Volpi E, Boirie Y
Finding: Healthy older adults need ≥1.0-1.2 g/kg/day protein; older adults who are exercising or have acute/chronic disease need 1.2-1.5 g/kg. RDA of 0.8 g/kg is inadequate. Per-meal target ~25-30 g high-quality protein.
Limitations: Consensus document, not a single trial. Reflects expert synthesis.
Effects of leucine-enriched whey protein supplementation on physical function in pre-sarcopenic and sarcopenic older adults: a randomized controlled trial (PROVIDE) ↗
RCT (13 weeks, multicenter) n=380 2015 J Am Med Dir Assoc
Bauer JM, Verlaan S, Bautmans I, Brandt K, Donini LM, Maggio M, McMurdo ME, Mets T, Seal C, Wijers SL, Ceda GP, De Vito G, Donders G, Drey M, Greig C, Holmbäck U, Narici M, McPhee J, Poggiogalle E, Power D, Scafoglieri A, Schultz R, Sieber CC, Cederholm T
Finding: Leucine-enriched whey protein (20g protein, 3g leucine, twice daily) increased appendicular lean mass and chair-stand test performance in sarcopenic older adults vs isocaloric placebo over 13 weeks.
Limitations: Industry-funded. Specific formulation. No mortality endpoint.
Dietary protein and exercise have additive effects on body composition during weight loss in adult women ↗
RCT n=48 2005 Journal of Nutrition
Layman et al.
Finding: Higher protein (1.6 g/kg) + exercise during weight loss preserved more lean mass than standard protein (0.8 g/kg) + exercise. Foundational evidence that 'older RDAs' under-prescribe for older / weight-losing adults.
Limitations: Small all-female sample; short duration (10 weeks).
Higher Protein Intake Is Associated With Higher Lean Mass and Quadriceps Muscle Strength in Adult Men and Women ↗
Prospective cohort (Framingham Offspring) n=2986 2017 J Nutr
Mangano et al.
Finding: Adults in the highest tertile of protein intake (greater than 1.2 g/kg) had higher lean mass index and grip strength than those in the lowest tertile (<0.8 g/kg). Effect persisted across age groups; strongest in 60+.
Limitations: Observational; protein intake by FFQ; reverse causality possible.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Healthy older adults: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day minimum; active/weight-loss/illness contexts commonly 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day; exercising adults 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day. Per meal: 25-40 g or ~0.4 g/kg/meal.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Lean body mass
  • Grip strength
  • Chair-stand performance
  • Sarcopenia incidence

Possible Side Effects

  • Mild stomach or gut upset with rapid increases
  • Increased thirst (urea excretion)
  • Higher per-day calorie load if not displacing other macros

Who Should Avoid This

  • Advanced kidney disease (severely reduced kidney function) — discuss with nephrology
  • Inherited protein-handling disorders

Opinion 2026-04-15

The protein RDA is wrong for adults over 50

The 0.8 g/kg/day RDA was set in 1968 for healthy young adults — and it's wrong for almost everyone over 50. The newer evidence-based target is 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day, ideally spread across meals.
