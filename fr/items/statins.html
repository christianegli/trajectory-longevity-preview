Bottom Line

If you have elevated ApoB or established atherosclerosis, statins are the single best-validated longevity drug we have. Not a 'natural' alternative — they're a real win. If you're low risk, NNT is large; discuss with your physician.

What this means for you

If your ApoB or LDL cholesterol is elevated, especially if you already have any sign of heart-artery plaque, statins are the single best-proven longevity drug we have. They reduce heart attack and death rates substantially. The 'muscle ache' fear is real but small and often goes away or can be managed by switching brands. Don't avoid them on vibes — get tested and decide with your doctor.

How to actually do this

  • Get an ApoB blood test (better than standard LDL) and a coronary calcium score (CAC) if you're 40+. These are the best risk-stratifying tests we have.
  • If your ApoB is high or your CAC is non-zero, talk to your doctor about a statin. Atorvastatin and rosuvastatin are the most commonly used.
  • Start at a moderate dose. Recheck lipids and liver enzymes at 6–12 weeks.
  • If you get muscle aches: try switching to a different statin (rosuvastatin and pravastatin tend to be better tolerated). Often works.
  • Combine with lifestyle: diet, exercise, sleep — they multiply the benefit.
  • Don't stop without talking to your doctor. Stopping statins after years on them carries real cardiac risk.
  • Avoid grapefruit juice with simvastatin (interaction).
Rough cost: $5–$30/month generic. Among the cheapest interventions on this list.

Worth considering if

Anyone with elevated ApoB, established atherosclerosis, prior heart attack, diabetes with cardiovascular risk factors, or strong family history of early heart disease.

Probably skip if

People with active liver disease, people who are pregnant or breastfeeding, and low-risk patients with normal ApoB and zero CAC unless a clinician thinks the tradeoff makes sense.

Helps with

Heart disease

Why it works

Your liver makes most of your cholesterol; food contributes less than people think. Statins block one specific step in the liver's cholesterol-making process, which lowers blood LDL and forces the liver to pull more LDL out of circulation. Beyond just the number, statins also calm inflammation in artery walls and stabilize existing plaque so it's less likely to rupture and cause a heart attack.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Inhibits HMG-CoA reductase → reduces hepatic cholesterol synthesis → upregulates LDL receptors → lowers serum LDL. Pleiotropic effects: anti-inflammatory, plaque stabilization, endothelial function.

Evidence Summary

30+ years of consistent randomized evidence. Pivotal trials: 4S (1994) — first to prove statins save lives in CHD survivors (30% mortality reduction). Heart Protection Study (2002, n=20,536) — extended benefit to diabetes and broader high-risk groups, 13% all-cause mortality reduction independent of starting LDL. ASCOT-LLA (2003) — first clear primary-prevention RCT in hypertensives. JUPITER (2008) — proved benefit in low-LDL/high-CRP primary prevention. CTT meta-analysis (2010, n>170,000) — each 1 mmol/L LDL reduction = ~22% MACE reduction; benefit scales with baseline risk. Side effects: muscle symptoms (mostly nocebo per SAMSON 2020 N-of-1 trial), small T2DM incidence increase (offset by CV benefit), rare liver/CK elevation.

Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration
Finding: 22% reduction in major vascular events per 1 mmol/L LDL reduction.
Limitations: Effect varies by baseline risk; primary prevention NNT higher.
Rosuvastatin to Prevent Vascular Events in Men and Women with Elevated CRP (JUPITER) ↗
RCT n=17802 2008 NEJM
Ridker et al.
Finding: 44% reduction in MACE in low-LDL/high-CRP primary prevention; trial stopped early.
Limitations: Industry-funded; effect size has been debated.
Randomised trial of cholesterol lowering in 4444 patients with coronary heart disease (4S) ↗
RCT (secondary prevention) n=4444 1994 Lancet
Scandinavian Simvastatin Survival Study Group
Finding: 30% reduction in all-cause mortality, 42% reduction in coronary mortality over 5.4 years in patients with prior MI/angina. The trial that proved statins save lives.
Limitations: High-risk secondary prevention only — cannot extrapolate effect size to primary prevention.
MRC/BHF Heart Protection Study of cholesterol lowering with simvastatin in 20,536 high-risk individuals ↗
RCT n=20536 2002 Lancet
Heart Protection Study Collaborative Group
Finding: 13% reduction in all-cause mortality and 18% reduction in vascular death over 5 years. Benefit independent of starting LDL level — extends evidence to broader high-risk population including diabetes.
Limitations: Selected high-CV-risk population; absolute benefits smaller in lower-risk groups.
Prevention of coronary and stroke events with atorvastatin in hypertensive patients (ASCOT-LLA) ↗
RCT (primary prevention with hypertension) n=10305 2003 Lancet
Sever et al.
Finding: 36% reduction in non-fatal MI and fatal CHD over 3.3 years; trial stopped early for benefit. First clear primary-prevention RCT in hypertensives with average cholesterol.
Limitations: Stopped early; absolute risk reduction smaller than relative; pharma-funded.
Self-reported muscle symptoms during statin therapy (SAMSON N-of-1 trial) ↗
N-of-1 RCT (statin vs placebo vs no pill) n=60 2020 NEJM
Wood et al.
Finding: 90% of self-reported muscle symptoms occurred on placebo months too — confirming most statin muscle complaints are nocebo, not true myopathy.
Limitations: Small sample; participants were already statin-intolerant by self-report.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Atorvastatin 20-80mg, rosuvastatin 5-40mg, simvastatin 20-40mg/day.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • All-cause mortality
  • Cardiovascular mortality
  • MACE
  • Stroke

Possible Side Effects

  • Muscle aches or weakness can happen. For many people they improve by changing dose or switching to a different statin.
  • A small increase in diabetes risk, mainly in people already near diabetes. For higher-risk heart patients, the heart-protection benefit usually outweighs this.
  • Liver blood-test changes are uncommon. Your doctor may check liver enzymes after starting or changing the dose.
  • Very rare serious muscle injury. Stop and seek urgent care for severe muscle pain or weakness, especially with dark urine.

Who Should Avoid This

  • Do not use with active liver disease unless a clinician specifically says it is appropriate.
  • Do not use during pregnancy or breastfeeding.
