Bottom Line

Reasonable to take 500-1000mg/day if stacking with NAD+ precursors, but not magical alone.

What this means for you

TMG (trimethylglycine, also called betaine) is a methyl-donor compound often paired with NMN or NR. The logic: NAD+ precursors burn through your body's methyl groups, and TMG replaces them. Reasonable to take if you're stacking NAD+ precursors; not magical alone. Naturally found in beets and spinach.

How to actually do this

  • If you take NMN or NR: pair with 500–1000 mg TMG/day. Take same time as the NAD+ precursor.
  • If you don't take NMN/NR: probably skip TMG unless your homocysteine is elevated.
  • Powder form is cheap and tasteless; capsules are convenient.
  • Beets and spinach are natural sources — useful background but not enough to replace a deliberate methyl-donor supplement when stacking NAD+ precursors.
  • Watch your LDL cholesterol — TMG at high doses (>3 g/day) has been shown to raise LDL slightly.
  • If you have high LDL, use lower doses (500 mg) or skip.
  • Mild fishy body odor is a rare side effect; back off if it happens.
Rough cost: $8–$20/month.

Worth considering if

People stacking NMN or NR. People with elevated homocysteine on routine bloodwork.

Probably skip if

People not on NAD+ precursors and with normal homocysteine. People with elevated LDL (use lowest effective dose).

Helps with

Heart disease Diabetes / metabolic

Why it works

Your body uses 'methyl groups' as tags to switch genes on or off and to convert various molecules. NMN and NR — when broken down — consume methyl groups. If you're taking high doses of NAD+ precursors daily, you may slowly deplete your methyl pool, which can raise homocysteine (a cardiovascular risk marker). TMG donates methyl groups directly, refilling the pool. If you're not stacking NAD+ precursors, the case for TMG is weaker.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Provides methyl groups to support methionine cycle. NAD+ precursors (NMN, NR) consume methyl groups via NNMT-mediated nicotinamide methylation; TMG buffers this. Also lowers homocysteine.

Evidence Summary

Reliably lowers homocysteine in most populations. Lipids may shift unfavorably (LDL up) at higher doses. Endpoint trials on hard outcomes are limited; the homocysteine-CV link itself is now considered weak (per HOPE-2, VISP).

Key Studies

Finding: 6g/day betaine reduced fasting homocysteine 18% but raised LDL and total cholesterol.
Limitations: Small N; surrogate endpoints; LDL signal concerning.

Studied Dose / Protocol

500-3000mg/day commonly. 6g used in some trials (high).
