Sleep (7-9h, regularity, deep+REM)

Bottom Line

If you only sleep 5-6h regularly, fixing that is more impactful than any supplement on this list. Regularity matters as much as duration. Get screened for sleep apnea if you snore or wake unrested.

What this means for you

If you're sleeping 5–6 hours regularly and accepting it, fixing that is the single biggest health upgrade you can make — bigger than any pill on this site. 7–9 hours, consistent timing, and a quiet dark cool room. If you snore or wake unrested, get screened for sleep apnea — that's the most under-diagnosed killer in this whole space.

How to actually do this

  • Pick a bedtime and stick to it. Variation under 30 minutes night-to-night. Regularity rivals duration in the data.
  • Aim for 7–9 hours in bed. If you can't function on 7, your sleep quality is the problem, not your need.
  • Cool, dark, quiet room. 18–20°C / 65–68°F. Blackout curtains or eye mask. Fan/white noise if needed.
  • No screens 30–60 min before bed. Or use them with brightness down + blue-light filter.
  • No caffeine after lunch. No alcohol within 3 hours of bed (it wrecks REM sleep even if it 'helps' you fall asleep).
  • Get morning sunlight within an hour of waking — anchors your circadian rhythm.
  • If you snore loudly, wake up gasping, or feel unrested despite 8h: get a sleep study. Sleep apnea is the silent killer here.
Rough cost: $0 to start. Sleep study $200–$1500 (often insurance-covered). Decent mattress + blackout curtains $500–$2000 one-time.

Worth considering if

Everyone. If you're chronically under 6h or have any unrested-wake symptoms, this is your highest-leverage intervention.

Probably skip if

Nobody. People with chronic insomnia or shift work need targeted help (CBT-I beats sleeping pills long-term).

Why it works

While you sleep, your brain runs a janitorial system that flushes out metabolic waste it can't clear when you're awake — including the sticky proteins linked to dementia. Your body recalibrates hormones, repairs muscle, files memories, and resets your immune system. Skip enough sleep and all of these go wrong simultaneously. It's not a luxury; it's when most of the maintenance happens.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Sleep drives glymphatic clearance (brain waste removal), memory consolidation, immune function, hormonal regulation, metabolic recovery. Both insufficient and excessive sleep are associated with worse outcomes; regularity (timing consistency) is independently predictive.

Evidence Summary

Sleep duration and quality are among the most validated longevity factors. Cappuccio 2010 meta-analysis (n>1.3M): U-shaped mortality curve with optimal at 7-8h; <6h or >9h associated with 12-30% higher mortality. Windred 2024 (n=60K UK Biobank): sleep regularity (consistent timing) is a stronger mortality predictor than duration itself. Mechanisms: Tasali 2008 (PNAS) showed 3 nights of slow-wave sleep suppression reduced insulin sensitivity by 25% — comparable to gaining 9kg. Shokri-Kojori 2018 (PNAS): one night of sleep deprivation increased brain amyloid-β by 5%, supporting the glymphatic clearance hypothesis for Alzheimer's risk. Treatment: CBT-I (Irwin 2017 meta) is first-line for insomnia, ahead of pharmacotherapy, with moderate-to-large effects sustained at 6-month follow-up.

Key Studies

Sleep duration and all-cause mortality: a systematic review and meta-analysis (Cappuccio et al.) ↗
Meta-analysis n=1382999 2010 Sleep
Cappuccio et al.
Finding: Short sleep (<7h) RR 1.12 for all-cause mortality; long sleep (>8-9h) RR 1.30.
Limitations: Long sleep may be marker of underlying disease.
Sleep regularity is a stronger predictor of mortality than sleep duration (Windred et al.) ↗
Cohort (UK Biobank) n=60977 2024 Sleep
Windred et al.
Finding: Sleep regularity index outperformed sleep duration as predictor of all-cause and cancer mortality.
Limitations: Observational.
Effects of Slow Wave Sleep Deprivation on Insulin Resistance ↗
RCT (mechanism) n=9 2008 PNAS
Tasali et al.
Finding: Three nights of selective slow-wave sleep suppression (without changing total sleep time) reduced insulin sensitivity by 25% — comparable to gaining 9 kg of body weight. Mechanism evidence that deep sleep specifically matters for metabolic health.
Limitations: Tiny sample (n=9); short-term; lab setting.
Effect of one night's sleep deprivation on amyloid-β42 in human cerebrospinal fluid ↗
Crossover (mechanism) n=20 2018 PNAS
Shokri-Kojori et al.
Finding: One night of sleep deprivation increased brain amyloid-β by 5% in healthy adults — direct evidence that the glymphatic clearance hypothesis matters for Alzheimer risk.
Limitations: Small sample; one night not chronic; PET imaging proxy.
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia in Older Adults: A meta-analysis ↗
Meta-analysis of RCTs n=1881 2017 Sleep
Irwin et al.
Finding: CBT-I produced moderate-to-large improvements in sleep onset latency, wake after sleep onset, and sleep efficiency in older adults; effects sustained at 6-month follow-up. CBT-I now first-line per AASM, ahead of pharmacotherapy.
Limitations: Heterogeneous CBT-I delivery; older adults studied — broader generalisability less established.

7-9 hours/night, consistent timing (bedtime varies <30 min night-to-night), dark cool room, no screens 30-60 min before.
