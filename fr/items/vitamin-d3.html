Vitamin D3

Replete deficiency, yes. Supplement above sufficiency for longevity? Marginal.

Vitamin Hormone precursor

Evidence Level

Multiple human RCTs with mortality/major-event endpoints. Regulatory consensus.

Bottom Line

Test your level. If deficient, replete to sufficiency. The data does NOT support stacking high-dose D for longevity above sufficiency. K2 pairing for arterial Ca is plausible but unproven.

What this means for you

If your vitamin D is low, fix it — being deficient is bad. But if your level is already normal, taking more does almost nothing for longevity, despite what the supplement industry implies. Test your blood level first. Don't just assume.

How to actually do this

  • Get a 25(OH)D blood test. Order it yourself if needed (many direct-to-consumer labs offer it for $30–50).
  • Target range: 30–50 ng/mL (75–125 nmol/L). Below 20 ng/mL is deficient.
  • If deficient: 2000–4000 IU/day vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol, not D2) for 8–12 weeks, then retest.
  • If sufficient: a daily 1000 IU is fine but unnecessary if you get sun exposure or eat fatty fish.
  • Take with a fat-containing meal (it's fat-soluble).
  • Don't megadose 'just in case'. Above ~5000 IU/day chronically can elevate calcium and harm kidneys/arteries.
Rough cost: $3–$10/month for a basic D3 supplement. Test costs $30–$80 once or twice a year.

Worth considering if

Anyone living above 40° latitude (most of Europe, northern US/Canada), dark-skinned individuals, indoor workers, older adults, anyone testing below 30 ng/mL.

Probably skip if

People already in the 30–50 ng/mL range. People with sarcoidosis, hyperparathyroidism, or kidney stones (talk to your doctor).

Helps with

Bone loss / osteoporosis Immune aging Frailty

Why it works

Vitamin D acts more like a hormone than a vitamin. Almost every cell in your body has a receptor for it. Your skin makes it from sunlight; if you're indoors, dark-skinned at northern latitudes, or older, you make less. When you're severely deficient, lots of body systems work poorly: bones soften, immune function dips, mood drops. Once your levels are adequate, more doesn't help — your body just excretes it.

For the curious — technical mechanism

1,25(OH)2D acts on nuclear vitamin D receptors expressed in most tissues. Regulates calcium/phosphate homeostasis, bone mineralization, immune cell differentiation, and gene expression in over 200 cellular pathways. Threshold effect: deficiency causes pathology, repletion restores function, supraphysiologic levels do not improve outcomes.

Evidence Summary

Vitamin D supplementation evidence is more nuanced than its popularity suggests. VITAL (Manson 2019, NEJM, n=25,871): 2,000 IU/day did NOT reduce CV events or cancer incidence in generally vit-D-replete adults. Cancer mortality showed a modest trend in subgroup analyses. Bjelakovic 2014 Cochrane meta: D3 supplementation reduced all-cause mortality by ~6% in older adults — borderline but consistent. Bischoff-Ferrari 2009: 700 IU/day or more reduced falls in older adults by 19%. D2d (Pittas 2019): 4,000 IU in pre-diabetics showed ~12% T2D reduction trend; clear benefit only in those with very low baseline. Bone: large RCTs of D + calcium prevent hip fractures in deficient older adults, less so in replete. Practical: 2,000 IU/day is safe and inexpensive; benefit largely confined to deficient (<20 ng/mL) or borderline (<30 ng/mL). Test before mega-dosing.

Key Studies

Vitamin D Supplements and Prevention of Cancer and Cardiovascular Disease (VITAL) ↗
RCT n=25871 2019 NEJM
Manson et al.
Finding: Vitamin D3 2000 IU/day did NOT reduce major CV events or invasive cancer over 5 years.
Limitations: Healthy population; single dose.
Vitamin D supplementation and total mortality (Bjelakovic et al. Cochrane meta-analysis) ↗
Meta-analysis n=95286 2014 Cochrane Database
Bjelakovic et al.
Finding: Modest reduction in all-cause mortality (~5%) with vitamin D3, primarily in elderly women.
Limitations: Heterogeneous trials.
Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Risk of Falls in Older Adults: Meta-Analysis ↗
Meta-analysis (8 RCTs) n=2426 2009 BMJ
Bischoff-Ferrari et al.
Finding: Daily vitamin D 700 IU or higher reduced falls by 19% in older adults. Doses below 700 IU showed no effect — dose threshold matters.
Limitations: Heterogeneous baseline 25(OH)D status; pooled doses; older meta.
Vitamin D Supplementation and Type 2 Diabetes Prevention: A Randomized Controlled Trial (D2d) ↗
RCT n=2423 2019 NEJM
Pittas et al.
Finding: 4,000 IU/day vitamin D3 in pre-diabetic adults reduced T2D incidence by ~12% over 2.5y (HR 0.88, 95% CI 0.75-1.04). Effect did not reach statistical significance but trend consistent. Subgroup with baseline vit D <12 ng/mL had clearer benefit.
Limitations: Borderline statistical significance; possibly under-powered for primary endpoint; mostly vit D-replete population at baseline.

Studied Dose / Protocol

1000-4000 IU/day typically. Adjust to maintain 25(OH)D 30-50 ng/mL.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • All-cause mortality
  • Cancer
  • MACE
  • Fractures
  • Falls

Possible Side Effects

  • Hypercalcemia at high doses (>10,000 IU/day chronic)

Who Should Avoid This

  • Hypercalcemia
  • Sarcoidosis (caution)
