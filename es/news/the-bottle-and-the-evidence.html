The bottle and the evidence

#methodology#supplements#industry

A pattern we keep finding: the marketing on a supplement bottle and the evidence behind that supplement are related but not the same thing. The bottle says "clinically studied." The study often examined a different compound, dose, formulation, population, or duration than what's in the bottle.

This post documents five specific patterns we've run into while building this catalogue.

Pattern 1: extract vs whole food

The cleanest version of this is green tea. Drinking green tea has decent population-level evidence: Japanese cohorts of 40,000+ adults, 11-year follow-up, lower cardiovascular and dementia mortality at 3-5 cups/day. Drinking the tea. The marketing pitch from supplement companies: "EGCG is the active ingredient! Skip the tea, take the capsule!"

Two problems with that. First, isolated EGCG capsules at >600-800mg/day have caused liver injury serious enough to land in the FDA's adverse event database. The brewed tea doesn't do this — bioavailability is lower and the matrix matters. Second, the cohort data is for tea drinkers, not capsule takers. Extrapolating from one to the other treats the active compound as the whole story when it's clearly not.

This shows up everywhere. Curcumin has decent anti-inflammatory data, but most of the curcumin in turmeric isn't bioavailable; the studies use specific formulations (Meriva, Theracurmin, BCM-95) that are not interchangeable with ground turmeric. Resveratrol trials use specific microcrystalline preparations that achieve plasma concentrations the supplement bottle can't replicate. Sulforaphane trials usually use myrosinase-activated extracts; capsules without active myrosinase yield much lower plasma concentrations.

The pattern: when the study uses a research-grade formulation and the bottle uses a generic one, the bottle is selling the idea of the study, not its result.

Pattern 2: the dose isn't the dose

We see this with omega-3. Most consumer fish-oil capsules contain 300mg total of EPA+DHA. The cardiovascular RCTs that show benefit — REDUCE-IT (icosapent ethyl), VITAL (EPA), ASCEND (1g/day prescribed) — use 1-4g of the active fatty acids per day. To match REDUCE-IT, you'd need 13 of those 300mg capsules per day. You don't.

The bottle says "supports heart health!" with a citation to a trial that used a different molecule at 13× the dose. Not technically false — fish oil does, in theory, support cardiovascular function. But the evidence behind that claim is for a different intervention than what's in your hand.

The fix: read the back of the bottle for EPA + DHA milligrams, not "fish oil milligrams" (1000mg of fish oil might contain 200-300mg of actual EPA+DHA). Aim for 1g+ EPA+DHA per day if you're trying to match the trial doses.

Pattern 3: surrogate endpoint creep

A trial measures CRP (an inflammation marker) and finds the supplement reduces it 15%. The headline becomes "reduces inflammation." Then the marketing says "supports a healthy inflammatory response." Then the implication becomes "good for chronic disease." None of those steps individually look bad. The chain takes you from a CRP measurement to "good for chronic disease" without anyone ever testing whether the supplement reduces chronic disease.

CRP isn't a perfect proxy for cardiovascular events. C-reactive protein dropping doesn't necessarily prevent heart attacks. Sometimes the chain holds (statins reduce CRP and prevent heart attacks). Often it doesn't (vitamin E reduces oxidative stress markers and doesn't prevent heart attacks; antioxidant trials in cancer prevention have failed at hard endpoints despite consistent biomarker improvement).

When you read a supplement claim, ask: was this tested on the thing you actually care about, or just a marker someone hopes is connected to that thing?

Pattern 4: cherry-picked species

Most "anti-aging compounds" you see in marketing have data from worms (C. elegans) or yeast. The translation rate to mammals is poor — maybe 10% of compounds that extend worm lifespan also extend mouse lifespan, and most of those have only modest effects in mice. Translation from mice to humans is roughly 30-40%.

So when a brand cites a compound that "extends lifespan in C. elegans by 30%," the realistic expectation for humans is: probably nothing detectable, possibly something modest, almost certainly not 30%.

We try to be explicit about this. Items in our catalogue with only worm or yeast data (resveratrol's original Sinclair work) are flagged as Tier 3 with the verdict making clear that mammalian and human data is what matters. Items with mouse-only data (NMN, NR, spermidine) are also Tier 3. Items get to Tier 1 only when there's solid human RCT or large prospective cohort data.

Pattern 5: trials of healthy young people, marketing to anxious old people

This is subtle and pervasive. Many supplement trials recruit healthy young adults — easier to enrol, faster outcomes, cheaper. The trial finds the compound improves some marker (memory test, sleep duration, cortisol response) over 4-8 weeks in 20-40 year olds. The marketing then targets 60+ adults worried about dementia or sleep or stress.

The implicit claim is that the effect generalises. Sometimes it does. Often it doesn't — older adults have different baseline physiology, different drug interactions, different response curves. The right test of "does this help cognitive decline?" is to study people with cognitive concerns, not college students.

When you see a supplement marketed for age-related concerns, check the trial population. If the trial was 25-year-old psychology undergrads, the relevance to a 70-year-old with mild cognitive impairment is uncertain.

What to do with this

We're not anti-supplement. Some of the catalogue items are supplements (creatine, omega-3, vitamin D, magnesium, several others). The point isn't to never take anything; it's to read the evidence behind a claim, not the claim itself.

When you're considering something, work the chain backwards:

  1. What specific compound, dose, formulation, and duration did the trials use? Match that, or know you're not.
  2. What endpoint did the trial measure? Hard outcomes (mortality, hospitalisation) trump biomarkers (CRP, LDL) trump self-report (mood, sleep quality).
  3. Who did the trials enrol? If you're not in that population, expect smaller (or zero) effect.
  4. Was it replicated? Single trials are signals; multiple consistent trials are evidence.
  5. Who funded it? Industry-funded isn't disqualifying, but it shifts the burden of proof — read the limitations section, not the press release.

If a brand can't tell you these things on the back of the bottle or on their website, the claim is fashion, not evidence.

Trajectory exists because we got tired of brands pointing at studies they didn't actually replicate. Every catalogue item links to the actual studies, with their actual sample sizes, their actual durations, and their actual limitations. Read the studies. Don't trust the bottle.
