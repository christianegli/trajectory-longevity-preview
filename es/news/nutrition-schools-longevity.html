Nutrition schools of thought: what to take, what to leave

#nutrition#evidence#frameworks

Longevity nutrition is noisy because every camp is partly right.

NutritionFacts is right that whole plant foods, legumes, fiber, and lower saturated-fat patterns are underused. Bryan Johnson is right that measurement and consistency matter. Huberman is right that nutrition sits inside sleep, training, light, and behavior. Valter Longo is right that timing, protein dose, and periodic fasting deserve serious study. Peter Attia is right that muscle, ApoB, insulin resistance, and protein adequacy matter. David Sinclair is right that aging biology is exciting.

The mistake is turning any one of those lenses into a religion.

Our job is simpler: extract the useful, human-evidence-backed pieces and ignore the parts that outrun outcomes data.

The boring consensus

If you force the camps to overlap, the practical center is not mysterious:

  • Eat mostly minimally processed foods.
  • Make plants, legumes, nuts, berries, whole grains, olive oil, and fiber-rich foods normal.
  • Get enough protein, especially after 50 or during weight loss.
  • Keep saturated fat and ultra-processed calories low enough that ApoB, blood pressure, glucose, and waist circumference move the right way.
  • Avoid smoking and keep alcohol low or zero.
  • Use supplements only when they solve a real gap.

The American Heart Association's dietary guidance lands close to this center: Mediterranean-style, DASH-style, pescetarian, and vegetarian patterns align best with cardiovascular guidance; very-low-carbohydrate and ketogenic patterns align least well when saturated fat, fiber, and food quality are considered.

NutritionFacts: strong on plants, weaker when absolutist

NutritionFacts and Michael Greger are strongest when they point readers toward beans, lentils, whole grains, berries, greens, nuts, and other whole plant foods. The legume signal is especially useful because it is cheap, scalable, and consistent with cardiometabolic biology.

Where we become cautious: animal-food avoidance is not the same claim as "more legumes and fiber are good." The first is ideological unless tied to an individual's lipids, preferences, ethics, kidney function, iron status, B12 status, and adherence. A whole-food plant-based diet can be excellent. It can also be poorly executed if protein, B12, calcium, iodine, omega-3, and total energy are ignored.

What to take: legumes, fiber, berries, greens, whole grains, nuts, low saturated fat, food-first framing.

What to leave: the assumption that vegan is automatically optimal for every longevity phenotype.

Blueprint: strong on consistency, weaker when productized

Bryan Johnson's Blueprint is useful because it makes nutrition measurable. Same meals, known ingredients, tracked biomarkers, low alcohol, sleep-protective routine, high consistency. Most people would improve if they copied the boring parts: structured meals, enough protein, vegetables, berries, nuts, cocoa, olive oil, and less late-night eating.

Where we become cautious: Blueprint now sells nutrition products and stacks. That creates a different evidentiary question. "This diet pattern helped one quantified person optimize many biomarkers" is not the same as "this branded powder/bar/stack improves clinical outcomes for you."

What to take: consistency, measurement, protein adequacy, plants, low alcohol, earlier eating, repeatable defaults.

What to leave: assuming the product stack is the intervention.

Sinclair: strong on aging mechanisms, weak on consumer nutrition translation

David Sinclair helped popularize aging biology and pathways like sirtuins, NAD, and resveratrol. That matters for the field.

But nutrition decisions need human outcomes, not pathway elegance. Resveratrol, NMN, and related "longevity supplement" claims are not backed by the same kind of human endpoint evidence as exercise, Mediterranean diet, fiber, protein adequacy, ApoB lowering, or blood pressure control.

What to take: aging biology is real, NAD metabolism is interesting, mechanisms can generate hypotheses.

What to leave: taking resveratrol/NMN as default nutrition because a pathway sounds plausible.

Huberman: strong on behavior stack, mixed on supplement gravity

Huberman's nutrition/supplement framing is often strongest when it starts with behavior: sleep, light, exercise, caffeine timing, alcohol reduction, and basic diet quality. That is the right ordering.

Where we become cautious: long supplement lists can make second-order tools feel first-order. For nutrition, the big question is not "what molecule could help?" It is "what intervention changes ApoB, blood pressure, glucose, muscle, body composition, symptoms, or hard outcomes enough to matter?"

What to take: caffeine timing, alcohol caution, behavior-first sequencing, performance nutrition when matched to a goal.

What to leave: supplement complexity before food, sleep, and training are handled.

Longo: strong on fasting science, needs personalization

Valter Longo's fasting-mimicking diet and "longevity diet" framework are among the more serious fasting/nutrition bodies of work. Periodic fasting can improve weight and cardiometabolic markers in some people.

Where we become cautious: fasting is not free. It can be a poor fit for frailty, pregnancy, eating-disorder history, underweight people, high training loads, some medications, and older adults who already struggle to maintain muscle. In longevity nutrition, preserving muscle may matter more than periodically proving you can tolerate restriction.

What to take: periodic energy restriction may be useful in selected metabolic-risk phenotypes.

What to leave: fasting as a universal anti-aging requirement.

Attia: strong on muscle and ApoB, less diet-tribal

Peter Attia's nutrition lens is useful because it is less attached to a named diet and more attached to outputs: ApoB, insulin resistance, lean mass, visceral fat, and adherence. His emphasis on protein and resistance training corrects a weakness in many plant-forward longevity conversations.

Where we become cautious: high-protein advice can be misread as "eat anything as long as protein is high." For longevity, protein adequacy should coexist with fiber, cardiometabolic food quality, and ApoB control.

What to take: protein adequacy, muscle preservation, ApoB as a scoreboard, adherence over diet identity.

What to leave: using protein as an excuse to ignore fiber and lipids.

Trajectory's nutrition hierarchy

Here is the order we would use for most adults:

  1. Pattern: Mediterranean/DASH/pescetarian/vegetarian-ish, mostly minimally processed.
  2. Protein: 1.2-1.6 g/kg/day if older, training, or losing weight.
  3. Fiber: 25-40 g/day from legumes, oats, berries, vegetables, nuts, seeds, or psyllium.
  4. Lipids: measure ApoB/LDL response; reduce saturated fat if needed; use medication when indicated.
  5. Timing: avoid late heavy eating if it hurts sleep or glucose; do not let fasting reduce protein or training quality.
  6. Supplements: creatine, vitamin D if deficient, B12 if vegan/low, omega-3 if low fish intake or specific indication, psyllium if fiber/ApoB need help.
  7. Frontier: NAD boosters, resveratrol, spermidine, fasting-mimicking cycles, and polyphenol extracts are optional hypotheses unless personal data shows a clear reason.

That hierarchy is less exciting than a named protocol. It is also harder to monetize. That is usually a good sign.
