What I'd actually take if I were 50

#methodology#personal-stack#worked-example

A common question we get: "You have 85 things in the catalogue. What would you actually do?"

The honest answer is it depends entirely on who you are. But "it depends" isn't useful, and the wizard-driven personalisation is one approach but not the only one. Sometimes seeing somebody else's reasoning is more illuminating than answering ten questions about yourself.

Here's a worked example: a hypothetical 50-year-old man, broadly healthy, with the following profile.

  • 50 years old, male
  • Non-smoker (never)
  • BMI 24, blood pressure 122/78
  • LDL 140 mg/dL (above target but not catastrophic), ApoB unknown but probably similar
  • HbA1c 5.5% (normal)
  • Currently exercises 2-3x/week (mostly running, no resistance training)
  • Sleeps ~6.5 hours on weeknights, 8 on weekends (irregular)
  • Diet: average — some Mediterranean elements but inconsistent, drinks 4-7 alcoholic drinks/week
  • No regular medications
  • Family history: father had MI at 64, mother alive and well
  • Income: comfortable but not unlimited; willing to spend ~$100/month

This isn't anybody specific. It's a representative middle-aged-man profile that lives in this site's most common reader demographic.

Here's how I'd reason about it.

Step 1: Foundation — non-negotiable

Before any pill or supplement, the foundations:

1. Get LDL/ApoB tested properly. With a family history of CAD at 64 and current LDL 140, the question of whether to start a statin is real. Get a full lipid panel including ApoB, plus a coronary calcium score (CT scan, ~$200, 5 minutes). If calcium score is 0, the lifestyle path may suffice. If >100, statin discussion is on the table.

2. Fix sleep. 6.5 hours weeknights with 8 hours weekend recovery is borderline. Tasali 2022 showed extending sleep reduced caloric intake 270 kcal/day in adults sleeping <6.5h. The wake-time-first protocol applies — pick a 7am wake-time and hold it across 7 days. Bright light morning, no caffeine after noon, alcohol away from bedtime. Free.

3. Add resistance training. Two times a week, 45 minutes each. Squat-pattern, hinge-pattern, push, pull, carry. Skip the bodybuilding split; do compound lifts and progress. The longevity literature is clear that grip strength + lean mass at 50 predicts mortality at 70+ better than most blood markers do. Cost: gym membership ~$30-50/month or basic home setup.

4. Add Zone 2. Currently running 2-3x/week is fine but the heart-rate target probably isn't disciplined. 3 sessions of 45-60 minutes at 60-70% max HR per week. Aerobic base improves more from longer easy sessions than from harder shorter ones. No additional cost.

5. Cut alcohol. 4-7 drinks/week sounds modest; it isn't. The post-2020 evidence (especially Mendelian randomisation) increasingly suggests no safe lower threshold for alcohol on multiple endpoints (cancer, cognition, sleep). The earlier "1 drink/day is heart-protective" finding was confounded. I'd target 0-2 drinks/week. Not zero unless you want to; just much less than current.

6. Diet — push toward Mediterranean / DASH. Daily legumes (huge evidence base — see the legumes item), olive oil as primary fat, ≥2 servings fish/week (or omega-3 supplement), nuts daily (small handful), whole grains over refined, more vegetables. Reduce sugar-sweetened drinks and ultra-processed foods. No supplement does what this diet does; trying to substitute supplements for diet is the most common longevity-spend mistake.

7. Vaccines. Annual flu vaccine. Stay current on the rest per primary-care guidance. The IAMI trial alone is enough to make this a foundation. At 50 it's still early for shingles vaccine (eligibility starts age 50 in most places — check); pneumococcal usually starts later.

These seven things, done consistently, would do more for this person's longevity than any combination of supplements would.

Cost so far: ~$30-80/month for the gym; coronary calcium score is one-time $200. Everything else is free.

Step 2: Selective targeted — based on actual numbers

Now I'd evaluate targeted interventions based on this person's specific profile.

Vitamin D3 + K2. I'd add daily 2,000 IU D3 + 100 mcg MK-7 K2, especially if a 25-OH-D test came back below 30 ng/mL (which is most people, particularly outside summer at higher latitudes). Modest effect; cheap; well-tolerated. Cost: ~$10-15/month.

Omega-3. If fish intake is <2 servings/week, 2g/day EPA+DHA omega-3. The cardiovascular evidence is mixed but the brain/mood/inflammation evidence is consistent. If fish intake is regularly ≥2 servings/week, skip the supplement. Cost: ~$15-20/month.

Magnesium glycinate, 200-400mg evening. If sleep is borderline. Modest effect; cheap; helps sleep onset and quality without sedation. Cost: ~$8/month.

Creatine, 5g/day. Especially valuable now that resistance training is part of the plan. Cognitive evidence is more recent and modest but real. Cost: ~$5-10/month.

Statin — based on calcium score. If calcium score >100 OR ApoB >120: start atorvastatin or rosuvastatin at low dose. The 4S/HPS evidence is unambiguous; the side-effect rate is lower than reputation suggests. If calcium score = 0 and ApoB <100: no statin yet, repeat calcium score in 5 years.

That's $40-50/month at most for daily targeted supplements, plus possibly a statin (cheap with insurance, $4-15/month generic).

Step 3: What I'd skip

This is the harder editorial position. Most popular longevity items don't make my hypothetical 50-year-old's list:

  • Rapamycin. Frontier; evidence in humans for longevity is absent; immunosuppression risk is real. Not at 50 with this profile. Maybe at 65 if data improves.
  • Metformin in a non-diabetic. TAME hasn't reported. Without prediabetes, the evidence-to-action gap is too large.
  • NMN/NR. Modest NAD+ rises; mortality data absent. Maybe in 5 years; not now.
  • Resveratrol. The original story didn't replicate well; data has been disappointing for a decade.
  • Senolytics (D+Q, fisetin). Frontier; one human RCT in IPF; not generalisable.
  • Curcumin/turmeric. Some inflammation and joint evidence; bioavailability concerns; reasonable if joint pain develops.
  • High-dose vitamin C. No mortality benefit; some kidney stone risk.
  • NAC, glycine, GlyNAC. Interesting hypothesis; thin RCT data; expensive for sustained use.
  • Most "biological age" tests. Marketing exceeding evidence; not actionable.

This isn't because these are wrong — most have something interesting going on. They just don't pass the "what's the strongest case for spending money on this in this person's situation?" test.

Step 4: Re-evaluate annually

The list above isn't static. At 60, the calculus changes — pneumococcal vaccine becomes relevant, the case for HRT/TRT (if symptomatic) becomes more interesting, fall-prevention training matters more, statins probably get added if not already. At 70, frailty interventions move up; muscle mass becomes the dominant concern.

Re-test labs every 1-2 years. If LDL/ApoB drops on lifestyle alone, no need for medication. If HbA1c rises, the metformin/GLP-1 conversation comes earlier than expected. If hs-CRP elevates, look for source rather than starting an anti-inflammatory.

Total monthly stack for this person

  • Gym: $30-50
  • Calcium score: $200 (one-time, year 1 only)
  • D3+K2: $10-15
  • Omega-3: $15-20 (skip if eating fish 2+ times/week)
  • Magnesium glycinate: $8
  • Creatine: $5-10

Plus a statin if indicated: $4-15.

Total recurring monthly: roughly $75-115. Well within the budget.

This is less than what most longevity-content consumers spend now and would produce more measurable benefit. The stack is short. The supplements are minor compared to the foundation work. The most expensive line item is the gym membership, which is the single highest-impact spend on the list.

What this isn't

This isn't medical advice. It's a worked example of the reasoning. Your own profile is different — different family history, different baseline labs, different goals, different constraints. The wizard does this reasoning more systematically and personally; this is the longform version of the same thinking.

The point isn't to copy this stack. It's to show the shape of the reasoning: foundation first, lab-driven additions next, frontier-skipping by default, annual re-evaluation. The pattern travels even when the specifics don't.
