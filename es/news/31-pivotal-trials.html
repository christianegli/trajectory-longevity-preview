31 pivotal trials we re-read this week

#methodology#statins#metformin#glp-1#vo2max#sleep#omega-3

We did a hostile re-read of the evidence behind the 15 interventions doing the most work on this site — the ones the assessment wizard hands out most often. The goal was simple: are we citing the right studies, or impressive-sounding studies?

The honest answer was both. Some items had strong primary trials we'd buried in a paragraph. Others had landmark studies we hadn't cited at all. So we rewrote the evidence summaries for 15 items, weaving in 31 new pivotal trials. None of the headline scores moved much — which is the right outcome when you're calibrated and just adding rigor.

Here's what we found, in plain terms.

Statins — the data is even cleaner than the consumer narrative suggests

We added four trials to the statins page: 4S (Lancet 1994), HPS (Lancet 2002), ASCOT-LLA (2003), and SAMSON (NEJM 2020). Why these four:

4S is the trial that proved statins save lives. 4,444 patients with prior heart attack, randomised to simvastatin or placebo for 5.4 years. 30% reduction in all-cause mortality, 42% reduction in coronary mortality. This wasn't a soft endpoint — it was death. After 4S, statins stopped being optional in secondary prevention.

HPS extended that to a much broader population. 20,536 high-CV-risk patients (including diabetes, prior stroke). 13% reduction in all-cause mortality, 18% in vascular death. Crucially: the benefit was independent of starting LDL. Even people with "normal" cholesterol benefited if their cardiovascular risk was high.

ASCOT-LLA showed primary prevention works — hypertensive patients without prior CV disease still got ~36% reduction in MI/coronary death over 3.3 years.

SAMSON is the trial that calibrated the side-effect narrative. Patients who had stopped statins because of "muscle pain" did a blinded n-of-1 crossover: statin tablet, placebo tablet, no tablet. Result: 90% of the symptom intensity attributed to statins was also present on placebo. The drug-specific contribution was small. This doesn't mean nobody has real statin myalgia — some do. It means most reported side effects are nocebo.

Bottom line: statins remain the single best-validated longevity drug we have. Score didn't change (effectiveness 7, certainty 10). The evidence got more legible.

Metformin — the geroscience case is weaker than Twitter thinks

We re-read UKPDS 34 (Lancet 1998), the post-trial follow-up (NEJM 2008), and Campbell's 2017 cancer-mortality meta-analysis.

UKPDS 34 randomised newly-diagnosed overweight T2D patients to metformin or conventional therapy. Metformin reduced any diabetes-related endpoint by 32%, all-cause mortality by 36%, MI by 39%. That's the trial that made metformin first-line in T2D.

The 10-year post-trial follow-up showed the legacy effect: even after the trial ended and groups converged on similar treatment, the original metformin group still had reduced MI and mortality. Early metformin is a good investment.

Campbell 2017 pulled cancer-mortality data: T2D patients on metformin had ~30% lower cancer mortality than T2D patients on other agents. That's the strongest non-cardiovascular signal.

What we didn't find: convincing data that metformin extends lifespan in non-diabetic humans. The mouse data is mixed (ITP showed no effect). TAME exists but won't read out for years. So we kept metformin's longevity score conservative — the diabetes case is rock-solid, the geroscience case is "wait for TAME."

If you have T2D or pre-diabetes: probably worth taking. If you're metabolically healthy and curious: there's no human evidence yet.

GLP-1 agonists — the lean-mass concern is real but contextual

We added LEADER, STEP 5, and Garvey 2024 (lean-mass post-hoc).

LEADER (NEJM 2016): liraglutide vs placebo in T2D + high CV risk. 13% reduction in MACE, 22% in CV death. First convincing evidence GLP-1s don't just lower glucose — they reduce hard cardiovascular events.

STEP 5 (Lancet 2022): semaglutide 2.4mg in non-diabetic obesity for 104 weeks. Sustained ~15% weight loss. Real durable effect, not the rebound seen with most pharmacological weight-loss agents.

Garvey 2024 looked at body composition. Yes, GLP-1s cause some lean-mass loss alongside fat loss — about 25-40% of total weight lost. But: in people who don't resistance-train. In people who do, lean mass is mostly preserved. The fix isn't to skip the drug; it's to lift weights while you take it.

If a doctor offers you a GLP-1 for genuine obesity or T2D, take it and lift. The lean-mass narrative is a reason to add resistance training, not a reason to avoid the drug.

VO2max — the strongest mortality predictor we have

Three trials we added: Mandsager 2018 (JAMA Network Open), Generation 100, Schmid 2022 meta-analysis.

Mandsager analysed 122,007 patients who'd done a treadmill test. Compared to "low" cardiorespiratory fitness, "elite" fitness was associated with 5x lower mortality. That's a bigger effect than smoking, diabetes, hypertension, or any single drug we cover on this site. Not because VO2max is magic — because it's a global readout of how well your cardiovascular system, mitochondria, and skeletal muscle are working.

Generation 100 randomised 1,567 70-77 year-olds to high-intensity interval training, moderate continuous training, or general activity for 5 years. The HIIT group had higher VO2max gains; mortality was numerically lower but not significant in the underpowered comparison. The point isn't "HIIT beats moderate" — it's that both structured exercise groups did better than the general-activity control, and the cohort tolerated training fine into their 70s.

Schmid 2022 (meta-analysis) confirmed: each 1-MET increase in cardiorespiratory fitness associates with 13% lower all-cause mortality. Roughly: going from "low" to "average" fitness — adding 3-4 METs — cuts your mortality risk substantially.

If you do nothing else from this site, get your VO2max measured. Then train it. The score (effectiveness 9, certainty 10) is the highest non-smoking effect we have.

Sleep — architecture beats duration

We added Tasali 2014 (slow-wave sleep deprivation → insulin resistance), Shokri-Kojori 2018 (one night of sleep loss → β-amyloid clearance), and Irwin's CBT-I cohort (2015 meta-analysis).

Tasali deprived healthy young adults of slow-wave sleep (without changing total sleep time). Three nights. Result: 25% reduction in insulin sensitivity. That's metabolic syndrome territory, from sleep-architecture disruption alone.

Shokri-Kojori showed that one night of sleep deprivation reduced β-amyloid clearance from the brain. This is the link between chronic sleep deprivation and Alzheimer's risk: amyloid is a metabolic by-product that accumulates when glymphatic clearance fails, and clearance happens during deep sleep.

Irwin's CBT-I meta-analysis: cognitive-behavioural therapy for insomnia produces sleep gains comparable to hypnotic drugs without the cognitive side effects, and the gains hold for years after the intervention ends. Sleep meds are a sledgehammer; CBT-I is the actual treatment.

Total sleep time matters. But deep sleep matters more. Cool, dark, quiet, no alcohol, regular schedule. If insomnia is chronic, ask about CBT-I before asking about Z-drugs.

What didn't change

Most scores didn't move. That's the right outcome — we didn't find any of the top-15 interventions that we'd badly mis-rated. The deep read added texture (which trial says what, what limitation each carries), not new conclusions.

A few small adjustments in the wizard's edge-case handling: depressed patients now see omega-3 at higher priority with EPA-specific protocol notes, and sleep-deprived patients now see a bright-light morning quickwin alongside the sleep-architecture interventions.

What's next

We're running the same hostile re-read on tier-2 items: ACE inhibitors, time-restricted eating, vitamin K2, sun exposure / circadian. Then a batch of new items mining from Brian Johnson's Blueprint, Longo's FMD work, Sinclair's senolytics, and Attia's protocols — real studies only, conservative scoring.

If something here doesn't match what you've read elsewhere, the methodology page explains how we score (effectiveness × certainty, two independent dimensions). Most longevity outlets average them into a single number. We don't, because that hides the difference between "definitely does a small thing" and "might do a big thing."
