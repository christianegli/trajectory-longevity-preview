Why VO2max is the vital sign nobody measures (and how to measure yours)

Cardiorespiratory fitness predicts all-cause mortality more strongly than smoking, hypertension, and diabetes — and unlike those, your doctor probably hasn't measured it.

#vo2max#cardio#mechanism#mortality

If your doctor has ever measured your VO2max — congratulations, you have an unusually attentive doctor.

For most people, the most powerful single predictor of how long they're going to live is a number nobody in their healthcare team has ever asked about, let alone written down.

The data, briefly

The Cleveland Clinic cohort of 122,000 adults followed for a median of 8 years found something striking: the gap in all-cause mortality between the lowest and highest fitness quintiles was larger than the gap between non-smokers and smokers.

Larger than diabetes vs no diabetes. Larger than chronic kidney disease vs no chronic kidney disease. Larger than every traditional cardiovascular risk factor we routinely screen for.

This isn't a single outlier study. The HUNT3 cohort in Norway, the Aerobics Center Longitudinal Study in Texas, and a half-dozen others all show the same shape:

  • A 20% improvement in cardiorespiratory fitness is associated with roughly a 15% reduction in all-cause mortality.
  • The benefit is dose-responsive across the full range — going from "low" to "below average" matters as much as going from "average" to "elite."
  • The benefit holds across age, sex, BMI, and comorbidities.

Why this is true mechanistically

VO2max — your maximal oxygen uptake — is an integrated measurement. It captures:

  1. Cardiac output: how much blood your heart can move per beat, times beats per minute.
  2. Vascular health: how efficiently that blood reaches the working tissues.
  3. Mitochondrial density and function: how much oxygen those tissues can actually consume and turn into ATP.
  4. Recovery capacity: how quickly the system returns to baseline after a stressor.

Almost every aging mechanism degrades one of these. Most degrade several. A high VO2max is the single best functional readout of "this person's biology is working."

The corollary is that improving VO2max isn't a vanity exercise — it's a direct intervention on multiple aging mechanisms at once. Which is why the VO2max TrainingT1E 9C 9 entry on this site sits at Effectiveness 9 / Certainty 9, the highest combination on the lifestyle side.

How to actually measure yours

You don't need a clinical CPET. You probably don't even need a smartwatch.

Cooper test (free, ~15 minutes including warm-up): Run as far as you can in 12 minutes on a flat surface. Plug your distance into a Cooper test calculator. Accuracy: within roughly 10% of a lab measurement for most adults.

Garmin / Apple Watch / Whoop estimate: Most modern wearables now estimate VO2max from heart rate response during runs and walks. They're noisy but trend reliably over months. Track the 30-day average rather than any single day.

Lab CPET: Gold standard. Costs $150–500 in the US depending on facility. Worth it once if you're over 50 and serious about training.

If you're "below average" for your age, that's where the highest mortality leverage is. Going from below-average to average buys you more years of healthy life than any supplement on this site.

How to actually train it

The protocol with the strongest evidence is dirt simple:

  • 3× per week of Zone 2 CardioT1E 8C 9 — 30–60 minutes at conversational pace. Builds the aerobic base.
  • 1× per week of intervals — the classic 4×4 Norwegian protocol: 4 minutes hard (8/10 effort), 3 minutes easy, repeat four times. Builds the ceiling.
  • 2× per week of Resistance TrainingT1E 7C 9 — protects the muscle that supports the cardiovascular system as you age.

That's the entire program. Most people see measurable VO2max improvements in 6–8 weeks.

Bottom line

If you only track one number for the next decade, it's not your weight, your LDL, or your steps. It's your VO2max.

It's the highest-signal vital sign you have access to. And nobody is measuring it for you.
