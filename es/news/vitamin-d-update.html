Vitamin D after VITAL: where the evidence actually lands

VITAL didn't kill vitamin D — it killed the idea that supplementing vitamin D in vitamin-D-replete adults would prevent cancer or cardiovascular events. The fracture prevention case in deficient older adults is still real.

#vitamin-d#vitamin-d3#fracture#falls#supplements

Vitamin D went through a hype cycle, then a backlash, and now sits in a more honest place. Let's catch up on what the evidence actually supports in 2026.

The story so far

Through the 2010s, observational data linked low vitamin D to roughly every chronic disease worth naming: cardiovascular events, multiple cancers, autoimmune disease, dementia, all-cause mortality. The intuition was simple — supplement, get the benefit, fewer diseases.

VITAL (2018), the largest vitamin D RCT ever run, tested that intuition directly. 25,871 adults, 5+ years, 2,000 IU/day vs placebo, all the major endpoints.

The headline result: No reduction in cancer incidence. No reduction in cardiovascular events. No reduction in all-cause mortality.

The supplement industry pretended this didn't happen. Skeptics declared vitamin D dead. Both got it wrong.

What VITAL actually showed (and didn't)

What VITAL did show:

  • No primary prevention benefit for cancer or cardiovascular disease in a generally vitamin-D-replete US population (median baseline 25(OH)D ≈ 31 ng/mL).
  • Modest signal for cancer mortality in those who developed cancer during follow-up, suggesting an effect on progression rather than incidence.
  • Improved bone density in the calcium-supplemented subgroup — but no reduction in fractures in the main analysis.

What VITAL didn't show:

  • It didn't address vitamin-D-deficient populations. The trial enrolled people in the normal range; benefit in deficient populations is a different question.
  • It didn't test fall prevention rigorously — that's where the older-adult literature still stands.
  • It didn't run long enough or in old enough participants to settle dementia or fracture questions in 80+ year-olds.

Where Vitamin D3T1E 4C 8 still has a real case

After VITAL, the honest priority order is:

  1. Confirmed deficiency (<20 ng/mL). Repletion to 30–50 ng/mL is well-supported. Cheap, effective, low-risk.
  2. Older adults with fall or fracture risk. Multiple meta-analyses still support fracture reduction at 800–1000 IU/day, especially when combined with calcium and Vitamin K2 (MK-7, MK-4)T2E 4C 4. The signal is most robust in deficient or institutionalized older adults.
  3. Limited sun exposure populations. Northern latitudes in winter, indoor workers, dark-skinned populations in low-UV regions. Even VITAL's null finding doesn't argue against repletion in groups likely to be deficient.
  4. Pregnancy. Generally recommended; deficiency is associated with several bad outcomes and the supplementation case is robust.

Where it doesn't have a real case

  • Healthy, vitamin-D-replete adults expecting cardiovascular or cancer prevention. VITAL settled this. Save your money.
  • High-dose annual mega-bolus dosing. Some trials suggest harm. Don't do this.
  • Levels above 60 ng/mL. No evidence of additional benefit; possible harm at the very high end (hypercalcemia, kidney stones).

The practical update to our wizard recommendations

The wizard's Vitamin D3T1E 4C 8 suggestion stays roughly the same: 2,000 IU/day with a fatty meal, retest at 8 weeks, target 30–50 ng/mL. We've updated the rationale:

  • For users with no risk factors and unknown vitamin D status, we now frame this as a cheap insurance policy with a small upside, not a longevity intervention.
  • For users >65, with limited sun exposure, or with osteoporosis, the priority is bumped — fracture prevention is the strongest remaining case.
  • For users already on calcium for bone health, we push the Vitamin K2 (MK-7, MK-4)T2E 4C 4 cofactor harder.

Bottom line

Don't take vitamin D expecting a 20% reduction in cardiovascular mortality. Do take it if you're deficient, older, or in a low-sun environment. Don't let post-VITAL nuance be misread as "vitamin D is useless" — repletion of deficiency remains one of the cleanest examples of a cheap intervention with a measurable effect on the right population.

The lesson VITAL actually taught us isn't about vitamin D specifically. It's about how often "low X is associated with bad outcomes" fails to translate to "supplementing X prevents bad outcomes." Worth remembering for the next single-nutrient hype cycle.
