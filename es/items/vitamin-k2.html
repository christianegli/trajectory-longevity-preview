Vitamin K2 (MK-7, MK-4)

Theoretical role in vascular calcification reduction. Strong cohort data, weak RCT data.

Bottom Line

Cheap hedge with plausible mechanism, weak RCT support. Pair with D3 if you supplement. Eat natto if you can stand it; else 100-200μg MK-7 supplement.

What this means for you

Vitamin K2 (different from the K1 in spinach) helps direct calcium to your bones instead of letting it deposit in your arteries — at least that's the theory. Cohort data looks good, but the gold-standard randomized trials are weak. Cheap hedge if you're already supplementing vitamin D. Don't take it if you're on a blood thinner like warfarin — it interferes with the medication.

How to actually do this

  • Best: eat the food sources — natto (if you can stand it), hard aged cheese, egg yolks, grass-fed butter.
  • If supplementing: 100–200 μg/day of MK-7 form (longer half-life, daily dosing works) or MK-4 (shorter half-life, multiple daily doses).
  • Pair with vitamin D3. Many supplements combine them; that's fine.
  • Take with a meal containing fat (it's fat-soluble).
  • DO NOT take K2 if you're on warfarin (Coumadin). It interferes directly with how the drug works.
  • Newer blood thinners (apixaban, rivaroxaban) don't have this interaction — but tell your doctor anyway.
Rough cost: $8–$20/month.

Worth considering if

People supplementing vitamin D3 for extra hedge. People with low bone density. People with a coronary calcium score above zero who want a low-cost layer.

Probably skip if

Anyone on warfarin. People who already eat natto/aged cheese regularly.

Helps with

Heart disease Bone loss / osteoporosis

Why it works

Your body has two proteins that need vitamin K2 to do their job: one in your bones (helps deposit calcium where it belongs) and one in your arteries (actively prevents calcium from depositing where it doesn't). Without enough K2, you can have the strange combination of weak bones AND calcified arteries — calcium going to the wrong places. Eating fermented foods like natto, hard aged cheese, and egg yolks gives you K2; most modern diets don't have much.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Activates matrix Gla protein (MGP), an inhibitor of vascular calcification, and osteocalcin (bone). Differs from K1 (clotting). Found in fermented foods (natto, hard cheese).

Evidence Summary

Rotterdam Study showed highest tertile of K2 intake had ~50% lower CHD mortality. RCTs are smaller and less impressive: Knapen et al. (2015) showed 3 years of MK-7 reduced arterial stiffness in postmenopausal women. No mortality RCTs. Pairing with vitamin D is theoretically synergistic for directing calcium to bone vs vessel.

Key Studies

Dietary intake of menaquinone is associated with a reduced risk of coronary heart disease (Rotterdam Study) ↗
Cohort n=4807 2004 Journal of Nutrition
Geleijnse et al.
Finding: Highest tertile of dietary K2 (vs lowest) associated with 41% lower CHD incidence and 57% lower CHD mortality over 10 years.
Limitations: Observational; food-based; possibly confounded.
MK-7 supplementation improves arterial stiffness in healthy postmenopausal women (Knapen et al.) ↗
RCT n=244 2015 Thrombosis and Haemostasis
Knapen et al.
Finding: 3 years of MK-7 180μg/day reduced arterial stiffness markers vs placebo.
Limitations: Surrogate endpoint; one study.

Studied Dose / Protocol

100-200μg/day MK-7 or MK-4.
