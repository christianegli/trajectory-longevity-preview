Rapamycin (Sirolimus)

The most consistent mammalian-lifespan-extending drug we have. Human longevity data still pending.

Bottom Line

Strongest mammalian lifespan signal of any drug, period. Human longevity benefit is plausible but not yet proven. If you can find a longevity-literate physician, intermittent low-dose protocols are increasingly common off-label. Self-medication is unwise — interactions and dosing matter.

What this means for you

Rapamycin is the only drug proven to extend lifespan across nearly every animal tested, including old mice. In humans, we know it tunes down the cellular signal that drives growth and ramps up your body's recycling system. Whether it actually adds years to human lives is still an open question — the trials haven't read out yet.

How to actually do this

  • Don't self-medicate. Find a longevity-literate physician (functional med, anti-aging clinic, or a primary doc willing to prescribe off-label).
  • Typical off-label longevity protocol: 5–8 mg once weekly, NOT daily. Daily transplant doses cause immunosuppression.
  • Take it on an empty stomach, same day each week. Most protocols cycle it or take breaks every few months.
  • Get bloodwork before starting and every 3–6 months: lipids, fasting glucose, HbA1c, CBC. Watch for mouth ulcers (most common side effect).
  • Pause it 2 weeks before any surgery and skip dosing if you're fighting an infection.
  • If you're trying to conceive, on chemo, or have an active infection — don't take it.
Rough cost: $30–$150/month depending on pharmacy and dose. Telehealth longevity clinics charge $200–500 in addition for consult + bloodwork.

Worth considering if

Adults over 50 with no contraindications who want to act on the strongest mammalian longevity signal we have, accept that human proof is still pending, and have access to a doctor willing to prescribe and monitor.

Probably skip if

Anyone with active infections, planning surgery, pregnant or trying to conceive, severely immunocompromised, with poorly controlled diabetes, or unwilling to do regular bloodwork. Also: don't take it just because someone on a podcast does.

Why it works

Think of mTOR as your cell's growth thermostat. When food is plentiful, it tells cells to grow and divide. When food is scarce, it switches the cell into maintenance mode — fixing proteins, recycling damaged parts, cleaning house. Rapamycin tricks cells into thinking food is scarce even when it isn't, so they spend more time in cleanup mode. That cleanup is what seems to slow aging.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Inhibits mTORC1, mimicking the cellular state of caloric restriction. Reduces protein synthesis, enhances autophagy, dampens chronic inflammation. mTORC1 is a central regulator of growth-vs-maintenance.

Evidence Summary

Extends lifespan in every model organism tested (yeast, worms, flies, mice). The NIA Interventions Testing Program (ITP) showed rapamycin extends median mouse lifespan ~10-25% even when started in old age — the only drug to do so robustly across all 3 ITP sites. Human longevity RCTs have not yet read out. Small human studies show immune rejuvenation in elderly. Major safety concern: immunosuppression (used clinically post-transplant), but intermittent dosing (5-10 mg weekly) appears better tolerated. PEARL trial (2024 readout) showed safety and lean-mass preservation at moderate doses; no mortality endpoint yet.

Key Studies

Rapamycin fed late in life extends lifespan in genetically heterogeneous mice (ITP) ↗
RCT (mice) n=1900 2009 Nature
Harrison, Strong, Sharp, Nelson, Astle, Flurkey et al.
Finding: Late-life rapamycin extended median lifespan by 9% (males) and 14% (females). First demonstration of pharmacological lifespan extension started after middle age in mammals.
Limitations: Mouse model. Dose translation to humans non-trivial.
mTOR inhibition improves immune function in the elderly ↗
RCT (humans) n=218 2014 Science Translational Medicine
Mannick et al.
Finding: Low-dose RAD001 (everolimus) for 6 weeks improved influenza vaccine response by ~20% in elderly. First human RCT showing mTOR inhibition has functional anti-aging effect.
Limitations: Surrogate endpoint (vaccine response), not lifespan.
PEARL: Participatory Evaluation of Aging with Rapamycin for Longevity ↗
RCT (humans) n=114 2024 Aging (Albany NY)
Kaeberlein lab / AgelessRx
Finding: 5-10 mg weekly rapamycin for 48 weeks: well-tolerated, lean mass preserved in women. No mortality endpoint.
Limitations: Surrogate endpoints; small N; selected population.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Off-label longevity dosing typically 5-8 mg once weekly, intermittent. Not the daily transplant dose (which causes immunosuppression).

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Vaccine response (elderly)
  • Lean mass
  • Periodontal health
  • Glucose tolerance

Possible Side Effects

  • Mouth ulcers (most common at higher dose)
  • Lipid elevation
  • Glucose intolerance with chronic dosing
  • Wound healing impairment
  • weakened immune system (dose-dependent)

Who Should Avoid This

  • Active infection
  • Pre-surgery (within 2 weeks)
  • Pregnancy
  • Significant weakened immune system
  • Severe hyperlipidemia
