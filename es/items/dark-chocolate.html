Dark Chocolate (cocoa flavanols)

COSMOS trial (2022, n=21,442) is the first large RCT of cocoa flavanols on hard CV endpoints. 27% reduction in CV death.

Bottom Line

One of the better-evidenced food interventions. The COSMOS trial (21,442 people, 27% reduction in CV death) is hard to wave away. Eat 20-30g of 85%+ dark chocolate daily, or use cocoa powder if you don't want the sugar. Don't switch to milk chocolate or 'dark' at 50%; the flavanol load doesn't transfer.

What this means for you

Dark chocolate isn't just a guilty pleasure — high-cocoa varieties contain flavanols, plant compounds with measurable effects on blood vessels. The COSMOS trial in 2022 was the first large randomised study (over 21,000 adults) testing whether daily cocoa-flavanol supplements reduce hard cardiovascular outcomes. The headline result: a 27% reduction in cardiovascular death, despite the primary composite endpoint missing significance. Dark chocolate at the dose used in trials (≥85% cocoa, modest portion) is a reasonable food-source approach. Just watch the calories — chocolate is calorie-dense even at 85% cocoa.

How to actually do this

20-30g (one or two squares) of ≥85% cocoa dark chocolate daily, OR 1 tablespoon (5g) of unsweetened cocoa powder added to oatmeal, smoothies, or coffee. Choose non-alkalised ('natural') cocoa when possible — alkalisation destroys most flavanols. Avoid milk chocolate (too little cocoa, too much sugar) and 'dark' chocolate at 50-70% cocoa (still mostly sugar). The COSMOS trial used a 500mg flavanol supplement; equivalent intake from food is roughly 30g of 85% chocolate or 1 Tbsp cocoa powder.

Rough cost: $10–25/mo

Worth considering if

Adults with elevated BP or family history of CV disease. People who already eat sweet desserts (replace one with dark chocolate). Anyone wanting a low-effort food addition with real evidence.

Probably skip if

Diabetes (watch portions; even 85% has some sugar). Migraines triggered by chocolate. Caffeine sensitivity at high evening intake (cocoa contains modest caffeine + theobromine). Reflux. Anyone who can't stop at one square.

Helps with

Heart disease

Why it works

Cocoa contains flavanols, especially epicatechin. These compounds increase nitric oxide in your blood vessels, which dilates them slightly and lowers blood pressure modestly. They also seem to improve the lining of arteries (endothelial function) and reduce platelet stickiness. The effect is real but small per square of chocolate — you need consistent daily intake to see the benefits.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Cocoa flavanols (notably (−)-epicatechin and procyanidin oligomers) are absorbed in the small intestine and conjugated; circulating epicatechin reaches low-µM concentrations within 1-2 hours of intake. Epicatechin activates eNOS via Akt phosphorylation, increasing NO bioavailability. Effects: ~3 mmHg reduction in SBP, improved flow-mediated dilation, modest reduction in platelet aggregation. Procyanidin oligomers are partly metabolised by gut bacteria to phenolic acids with separate cardioprotective effects. Bioavailability varies by processing (alkalisation/'Dutch processing' destroys flavanols; non-alkalised cocoa retains them).

Evidence Summary

COSMOS (Sesso 2022, AJCN) is the headline trial: 21,442 older adults randomised to 500mg cocoa flavanols + 80mg (−)-epicatechin daily vs placebo for a median 3.6 years. The primary composite CV endpoint (MI, stroke, CV death, revascularisation, etc.) didn't reach significance overall (HR 0.90, 95% CI 0.78-1.02), but the secondary endpoint of CV death was significantly reduced (HR 0.73). Lifestyle adjustment for adherence-corrected analyses showed stronger primary endpoint reduction. Ried 2017 Cochrane meta-analysis of 35 short-term RCTs (n=1,804) confirmed: cocoa intake reduces SBP 1.8 mmHg, DBP 1.8 mmHg over 4-18 weeks, with stronger effects in hypertensive subjects. Hooper 2012 (AJCN) showed flow-mediated dilation improved 1.34% absolute increase from chocolate consumption, an effect size larger than most blood-pressure medications produce. Buitrago-Lopez 2011 (BMJ) meta-analysed observational chocolate cohorts: highest vs lowest consumption associated with 37% reduction in CV disease and 29% reduction in stroke — though confounding is hard to rule out in observational data. The combination of consistent short-term BP/endothelial RCTs, plausible mechanism, and now a 21,000-person hard-endpoint trial makes cocoa flavanols one of the better-evidenced food interventions.

Key Studies

COSMOS: Effect of cocoa flavanol supplementation for the prevention of cardiovascular disease events ↗
RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled n=21442 2022 American Journal of Clinical Nutrition
Sesso et al.
Finding: 500mg cocoa flavanols + 80mg (−)-epicatechin daily reduced CV death by 27% (HR 0.73) over 3.6 years in adults age 60+. Primary composite endpoint missed significance (HR 0.90, p=0.11) but trended in the right direction.
Limitations: Composite primary endpoint missed by ~0.04; lower-than-expected event rate; supplement form, not chocolate.
Cocoa as treatment for hypertension and pre-hypertension (Cochrane Review 2017, Ried) ↗
Meta-analysis of 35 RCTs n=1804 2017 Cochrane Database
Ried et al.
Finding: Cocoa intake reduced SBP by 1.76 mmHg and DBP by 1.76 mmHg vs control over 4-18 weeks. Effect larger in hypertensive subjects.
Limitations: Modest effect size; mostly short trials; surrogate endpoint.
Chocolate consumption and cardiometabolic disorders: systematic review and meta-analysis (Buitrago-Lopez 2011) ↗
Meta-analysis of 7 cohort studies n=114009 2011 BMJ
Buitrago-Lopez et al.
Finding: Highest vs lowest chocolate consumption associated with 37% lower CV disease (RR 0.63) and 29% lower stroke (RR 0.71).
Limitations: Observational; cannot rule out reverse causation (sicker people eat less); highest-intake category was modest (1-2 servings/wk).
Flavonoid-rich cocoa consumption affects multiple cardiovascular risk factors in a meta-analysis of short-term studies (Hooper 2012) ↗
Meta-analysis of 24 RCTs n=1297 2012 American Journal of Clinical Nutrition
Hooper et al.
Finding: Cocoa flavanol consumption increased flow-mediated dilation by 1.34% absolute, reduced LDL/HDL ratio, and reduced fasting insulin.
Limitations: Heterogeneous interventions; short trials (median 4 weeks); endothelial function is surrogate.

Studied Dose / Protocol

500mg cocoa flavanols + 80mg epicatechin/day (COSMOS); equivalent to ~30g of 85% dark chocolate or 1 Tbsp non-alkalised cocoa powder

Possible Side Effects

  • Calories matter at consistent intake. Caffeine/theobromine in cocoa can disturb sleep at evening intake (~25mg caffeine/30g chocolate). Can trigger reflux or migraines in susceptible people.

Who Should Avoid This

  • Migraine triggered by chocolate. Severe sleep onset issues with evening intake. Caution in dogs (toxic) — keep out of reach.
