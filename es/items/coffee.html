Coffee (Caffeinated)

Daily coffee (3-5 cups) is consistently associated with lower all-cause mortality across dozens of cohort studies. Causality probable but not proven.

Bottom Line

If you already drink 2-4 cups of filtered coffee a day, you're doing one of the best-evidenced longevity practices. If you don't drink it, the evidence isn't strong enough to start. Avoid unfiltered (espresso, French press) at high volumes — raises LDL. Stop after noon if it disturbs sleep.

What this means for you

Daily coffee (3-5 cups) is one of the most consistent food/beverage patterns associated with lower mortality in observational studies — about 17% lower all-cause death rate, with strong protective signals for type 2 diabetes, Parkinson's, liver disease, and several cancers. The evidence isn't from randomized trials but the signal is unusually consistent across populations and ages. Use filtered (drip) coffee — espresso/French press at high volumes raises cholesterol slightly.

How to actually do this

Drink 2-4 cups of filtered coffee in the morning hours. Avoid coffee after ~2 PM if you're not a known fast metabolizer (caffeine half-life ~5h, longer in slow metabolizers). Don't add 4 sugars and cream — that wipes out the metabolic benefit. Black, with milk, or with a small amount of sugar is fine. Decaf still has chlorogenic acid but loses the caffeine-related benefits.

Rough cost: Free if you make at home from beans (~$10-30/month). $100+/month if you buy daily takeaway.

Why it works

Coffee is a complex mix, not just caffeine. The polyphenols (chlorogenic acid) are antioxidants, caffeine improves insulin sensitivity slightly, and the whole package reduces low-grade inflammation. The reasons it protects against Parkinson's and liver disease aren't fully understood — but the patterns hold across many countries and decades, which is unusual for nutrition research.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Multiple bioactives: caffeine (adenosine A1/A2A receptor antagonist; promotes wakefulness, mild thermogenesis, increased lipolysis), chlorogenic acid (polyphenol antioxidant, modulates glucose metabolism), trigonelline, melanoidins. Cafestol and kahweol diterpenes (in unfiltered coffee) inhibit hepatic LDL receptor, raising LDL ~10% at high intake. CYP1A2 polymorphisms determine caffeine clearance — slow metabolizers (rs762551 CC genotype) experience greater pressor effect and may not benefit cardiovascularly.

Evidence Summary

Poole 2017 BMJ umbrella review (201 meta-analyses): 3-5 cups/day associated with 17% lower all-cause mortality (largest reduction at this dose), lower CVD mortality, and reduced T2D, liver disease, Parkinson's, and several cancers. UK Biobank analysis (n=171,616) confirmed J-shaped curve with optimal benefit at 2-3 cups/day. Key caveats: (1) all observational, (2) effect attenuated in slow caffeine metabolizers (CYP1A2*1F variant), (3) unfiltered coffee (espresso, French press) raises LDL via cafestol — drip/filter coffee preferred for lipids.

Key Studies

Coffee consumption and health: umbrella review of meta-analyses of multiple health outcomes ↗
Umbrella review (201 meta-analyses) 2017 BMJ
Poole R, Kennedy OJ, Roderick P, Fallowfield JA, Hayes PC, Parkes J
Finding: 3-4 cups/day: 17% lower all-cause mortality, 19% lower CV mortality, 30% lower T2D incidence, 31% lower Parkinson's incidence, lower liver cirrhosis and HCC. J-shaped curve with optimal at 3-4 cups.
Limitations: All observational. Healthy-user bias possible. Different coffee preparations bundled together.
Long-term coffee consumption, caffeine metabolism genetics, and risk of cardiovascular disease: a prospective analysis of up to 347,077 individuals ↗
Prospective cohort (UK Biobank + Mendelian randomization) n=347077 2019 Am J Clin Nutr
Zhou A, Hyppönen E
Finding: Coffee consumption inversely associated with CVD risk in U-shape. Mendelian randomization using CYP1A2 and AHR variants supported a likely causal relationship for moderate intake (3-4 cups/day).
Limitations: Mendelian randomization assumptions. UK-only cohort.

Studied Dose / Protocol

3-5 cups (8 oz / 240 mL) of brewed coffee daily, ideally filtered (drip), spread through morning hours.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • All-cause mortality (cohort)
  • CV mortality
  • T2D incidence
  • Parkinson's incidence
  • HCC and cirrhosis

Possible Side Effects

  • Insomnia (caffeine half-life ~5 hours, longer in slow metabolizers)
  • Anxiety/jitteriness (especially in CYP1A2 slow metabolizers)
  • Acid reflux
  • Mild dependence (withdrawal headache common)
  • Modest acute blood pressure rise (~3-5 mmHg)

Who Should Avoid This

  • Severe anxiety or panic disorder
  • Uncontrolled hypertension
  • Pregnancy (limit to 200mg caffeine/day per ACOG)
  • Slow CYP1A2 metabolizers with hypertension or CHD
