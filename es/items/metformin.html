Metformin

Diabetes drug with intriguing longevity signal in observational data. TAME trial in progress.

Bottom Line

Type 2 diabetes: yes, first-line, the data is overwhelming. Non-diabetics for longevity: not yet — the only RCT data shows it BLUNTS exercise gains. If you exercise hard for fitness, metformin is a poor fit. Wait for TAME. If you're pre-diabetic and exercise is hard to maintain, the diabetes-prevention case is stronger.

What this means for you

Metformin is a cheap, decades-old diabetes drug that diabetics on it seem to live longer than even non-diabetics — which is strange and interesting. Whether healthy people benefit is genuinely unclear. The big trial (TAME) is still running. Important catch: it blunts the muscle and fitness gains you get from hard exercise, so if you train seriously, this is a real tradeoff.

How to actually do this

  • If you have type 2 diabetes or pre-diabetes — this is probably already on your doctor's table. Talk to them.
  • If you're healthy and want it for longevity: ask about it explicitly. Most primary docs won't prescribe off-label; longevity-focused clinics will.
  • Start at 500 mg extended-release with dinner to minimize GI upset. Many people work up to 1000–1500 mg/day over weeks.
  • If you exercise hard for performance/muscle: take it on rest days only, or skip it on heavy training days. Or skip the drug entirely.
  • Get a B12 level checked yearly — long-term metformin can quietly drop B12.
  • Drink water. Stop if you're sick with vomiting/diarrhea (rare risk of lactic acidosis when dehydrated).
Rough cost: $5–$20/month generic. Among the cheapest things on this list.

Worth considering if

People with pre-diabetes, fatty liver, PCOS, or strong family history of type 2 diabetes who don't train at high intensity for performance gains.

Probably skip if

Serious athletes or anyone whose primary goal is maximizing strength/hypertrophy from training. Also: kidney impairment, on contrast dye, or chronic alcohol use.

Helps with

Diabetes / metabolic Cancer Heart disease Chronic inflammation

Why it works

Metformin tells your liver to dump less sugar into your blood, and makes your muscles and cells more sensitive to insulin. It also gently stresses cells in a way that triggers their cleanup and repair machinery — similar to what fasting or exercise does, but milder. The exercise-blunting effect comes from the same pathway: the cellular stress signal that normally tells your muscles to adapt and grow stronger gets dampened.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Activates AMPK, inhibits mitochondrial complex I, suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis. Improves insulin sensitivity. Pleiotropic effects on inflammation, gut microbiome, and mTOR. The AMPK activation also blunts mTOR-driven anabolic adaptation to resistance training.

Evidence Summary

In type 2 diabetes: foundational RCT evidence. UKPDS 34 (1998, n=753) — metformin reduced all-cause mortality 36% in newly-diagnosed overweight T2D over 10.7 years vs conventional treatment. 10-year UKPDS post-trial follow-up (2008) showed legacy effect: 27% mortality reduction persisted after glucose control converged. Bannister 2014 cohort (n=78,241) suggested diabetics on metformin had longer survival than matched non-diabetic controls — striking but observational. In non-diabetics: ITP mouse study showed no lifespan extension alone. MASTERS RCT (2019) found metformin BLUNTED cardiorespiratory fitness gains from exercise training in healthy older adults. TAME trial (Barzilai) aims to test whether metformin delays multimorbidity in non-diabetics — not read out yet. Cancer-prevention meta-analyses suggest modest reductions in colorectal/liver/pancreatic cancer in T2D, all observational. Bottom line: gold standard for T2D; the longevity-in-healthy-people pitch remains a hypothesis.

Key Studies

Can metformin extend lifespan? Effects on chronologically aged mice (ITP) ↗
RCT (mice) n=600 2016 Aging Cell
Strong et al.
Finding: Metformin alone did NOT extend median lifespan in male mice. Combined with rapamycin showed modest benefit.
Limitations: Mouse, male only, single dose.
Metformin use and mortality among patients with type 2 diabetes (UKPDS 34) ↗
Cohort n=78241 2014 Diabetes Obes Metab
Bannister et al.
Finding: Diabetics on metformin had longer survival than matched diabetics on sulfonylureas AND than matched non-diabetic controls (HR ~0.85).
Limitations: Observational; selection bias possible.
Metformin inhibits mitochondrial adaptations to aerobic exercise training (MASTERS) ↗
RCT (humans) n=53 2019 Aging Cell
Konopka et al.
Finding: Metformin blunted improvements in cardiorespiratory fitness from exercise training in non-diabetic older adults.
Limitations: Small N; surrogate endpoints.
Effect of intensive blood-glucose control with metformin on complications in overweight patients with type 2 diabetes (UKPDS 34) ↗
RCT n=753 1998 Lancet
UK Prospective Diabetes Study (UKPDS) Group
Finding: Metformin (vs conventional treatment) reduced any diabetes-related endpoint by 32%, all-cause mortality by 36%, and MI by 39% in newly-diagnosed overweight T2D patients over 10.7 years. Foundational RCT establishing metformin as first-line.
Limitations: Modest sample for primary metformin arm; T2D-specific population, not generalizable to non-diabetics.
10-Year follow-up of intensive glucose control in type 2 diabetes (UKPDS post-trial) ↗
RCT extension follow-up n=4209 2008 NEJM
Holman et al.
Finding: Legacy effect — even after the RCT ended and glucose control converged between groups, the metformin group maintained reduced MI (33%) and mortality (27%) at 10-year follow-up.
Limitations: Post-trial observational; potential for unmeasured confounders during follow-up.
Metformin and cancer: an existing drug for cancer prevention? (meta-analysis) ↗
Meta-analysis n=65540 2019 Annals of Translational Medicine
Yu et al.
Finding: Metformin associated with reduced cancer incidence and mortality in T2D, especially colorectal, liver, and pancreatic. Effect size modest but consistent across studies.
Limitations: All observational — confounding by indication is plausible (sicker patients on alternatives).

Studied Dose / Protocol

500-2000 mg/day extended-release with food. Diabetic dose; longevity dose unclear.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • All-cause mortality (T2DM cohorts)
  • Insulin sensitivity
  • Cancer incidence
  • Cardiovascular events

Possible Side Effects

  • stomach or gut distress
  • B12 deficiency (long-term)
  • Lactic acidosis (rare)

Who Should Avoid This

  • severely reduced kidney function
  • Acute illness
  • contrast dye for a scan (hold 48h)
  • Heavy alcohol use
