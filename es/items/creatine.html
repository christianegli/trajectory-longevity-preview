Bottom Line

Cheapest, most-validated supplement on this list. If you do any resistance training, take it. The longevity case is via lean mass + strength preservation, both of which are independent mortality predictors.

What this means for you

Creatine is the most-studied supplement in existence. It works: it makes you a little stronger when you train, helps you preserve muscle as you age, and there's growing evidence it helps the brain too — especially when you're sleep-deprived or vegetarian. It's cheap, safe, and the kidney-damage scare is a myth in healthy people.

How to actually do this

  • Take 3–5 grams of creatine monohydrate per day. Skip the loading phase — it just causes bloating.
  • Brand doesn't matter much. Look for 'Creapure' or any third-party tested product. Powder is cheapest.
  • Mix it into water, juice, coffee, or your protein shake. Timing doesn't matter — daily consistency does.
  • Drink an extra glass of water with it. Expect 1–2 lbs of water-weight gain in the first weeks (this is muscle hydration, not fat).
  • Combine with resistance training (2–3x/week) to get the lean-mass benefit. Creatine without lifting still helps cognition but the muscle benefit is the bigger longevity story.
  • Take it forever. The benefit reverses if you stop.
Rough cost: $10–$25/month. One of the best longevity ROI on this entire site.

Worth considering if

Anyone over 40 who wants to preserve muscle and bone. Vegetarians and vegans (you get less from food). Anyone training for strength or hypertrophy.

Probably skip if

People with severe kidney disease should ask their doctor first (precautionary, evidence is reassuring in healthy kidneys).

Helps with

Muscle loss (sarcopenia) Cognitive decline Frailty Bone loss / osteoporosis

Why it works

Your muscles and brain run on a tiny battery called ATP. When you do something hard — sprint, lift, think under pressure — those batteries drain fast. Creatine is the rechargeable backup pack: it lets you regenerate ATP a few seconds faster. More usable energy means a few more reps, slightly heavier lifts, slightly sharper thinking under stress. Compounded over years of training, that's a meaningful difference in muscle and bone preservation.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Phosphocreatine system buffers ATP regeneration in high-energy tissues (muscle, brain). Supplementation increases muscle and brain creatine pool, improving high-intensity work capacity and possibly cognition under metabolic stress.

Evidence Summary

Creatine monohydrate is the most studied sports supplement, with ~30 years of safety data and robust efficacy for strength + lean mass. ISSN 2017 position stand (Kreider et al.): supports 5g/day for cellular saturation, no loading phase needed. Safety established up to 5 years (Kreider 2003) in healthy adults. Forbes 2021 systematic review: creatine + resistance training in older adults = ~1.4 kg more lean mass + 5-10% more strength than RT alone. Cognitive: Roschel 2021 review found small but consistent improvements in working memory and intelligence test performance in older adults (less benefit in young healthy). Mechanism: increases phosphocreatine pool -> faster ATP regeneration -> more high-quality reps; in brain, may buffer ATP under stress (sleep deprivation, cognitive load). Practical: 5g monohydrate daily, any time, with or without food. Skip the loading phase. Plateau effect ~3-4 weeks. Expect 1-2 kg water-weight gain in muscle (intracellular water — that's the mechanism). CKD patients: discuss with nephrologist; healthy kidney function is not a contraindication.

Key Studies

International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand: creatine supplementation (Kreider et al.) ↗
Position statement / meta-analysis 2017 JISSN
Kreider et al.
Finding: Most effective ergogenic for high-intensity exercise; safe long-term; benefits across age groups; potential cognitive and clinical benefits.
Limitations: Position statement; pooled data.
Effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function (Roschel et al.) ↗
Review/meta-analysis 2021 Nutrients
Roschel et al.
Finding: Modest improvement in working memory and short-term memory; effect strongest in vegetarians and during sleep deprivation.
Limitations: Heterogeneous studies; modest effect.
Creatine supplementation in older adults: A systematic review ↗
Systematic review 2021 Nutrients
Forbes et al.
Finding: Creatine + resistance training in older adults consistently improved lean mass (~1.4 kg over baseline) and strength (5-10%) more than RT alone. Effect is robust across multiple RCTs.
Limitations: Meta-analysis; trials varied in duration and dose; quality moderate.
Long-term safety and efficacy of creatine monohydrate supplementation in athletes ↗
Long-term safety review 2003 Mol Cell Biochem
Kreider et al.
Finding: Up to 5 years of creatine supplementation showed no adverse effects on kidney, liver, muscle, or hematologic markers in healthy subjects. Established the long-term safety profile.
Limitations: Industry-funded; healthy athletes only; not generalizable to CKD patients.

Studied Dose / Protocol

3-5g/day (no loading needed for most).
