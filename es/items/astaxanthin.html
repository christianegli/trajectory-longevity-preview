Astaxanthin

Powerful carotenoid from microalgae. Modest evidence for skin elasticity, eye fatigue, and lipid oxidation. Marketed beyond what the data supports.

Bottom Line

Real but small effects on skin and eye fatigue. Not a longevity drug — marketing is way ahead of evidence. If you want the antioxidant effect, eat salmon (the natural source) or a serving of red-pigmented algae. Skip the expensive supplements unless skin/eye fatigue is your specific issue.

What this means for you

A red pigment from microalgae (it's what makes salmon and shrimp pink). Small studies show 4-12mg/day improves skin elasticity slightly and reduces eye fatigue from screens. The marketing claims (anti-aging, performance, etc.) are way ahead of the actual evidence — this is a quality-of-life supplement, not a life-extension drug.

How to actually do this

If you want to try: 4-12 mg/day of astaxanthin from microalgae source (Haematococcus pluvialis). Take with a meal containing fat for absorption (salmon, eggs, olive oil). Effect on skin and eye fatigue takes 8-12 weeks to appear.

Rough cost: $15-30/month for 4-12mg algae-derived astaxanthin.

Worth considering if

["Visible photo-aging on skin and you've already done sunscreen + retinoids", 'Significant screen-related eye fatigue', "You don't eat salmon/shrimp/wild-caught fish (natural sources)"]

Probably skip if

['You want longevity benefit — no human mortality data', 'Budget-constrained — sunscreen, retinoid, and dietary salmon cost less and do more', 'You eat 2+ servings wild salmon/week — already getting it']

Why it works

Astaxanthin is a very strong antioxidant that gets into nearly every tissue in your body, including the brain and the back of your eye. It neutralizes the type of damage that comes from sun exposure and aging. The challenge: most antioxidant supplements look impressive in test tubes but produce only small effects in actual humans.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Xanthophyll carotenoid spanning the lipid bilayer, with hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions. Quenches singlet oxygen and reactive oxygen species in both lipid and aqueous phases. Crosses blood-brain and blood-retinal barriers. Modulates Nrf2 antioxidant response pathway. May modestly inhibit cytokine release in macrophages.

Evidence Summary

Multiple small RCTs (n=20-100 each) show 4-12 mg/day improves skin elasticity, reduces age spots and fine wrinkles vs placebo (Tominaga 2012, Yoon 2014). Small RCTs also show improved eye accommodation and reduced eye fatigue from screens. Meta-analysis suggests modest LDL oxidation reduction. No mortality or hard-endpoint data. Generally well-tolerated with very few side effects.

Key Studies

Cosmetic benefits of astaxanthin on humans subjects ↗
RCT (8-week) n=65 2012 Acta Biochim Pol
Tominaga K, Hongo N, Karato M, Yamashita E
Finding: 6mg/day astaxanthin oral + 2ml topical for 8 weeks improved skin moisture, elasticity, wrinkle depth, and age-spot size vs placebo in middle-aged Japanese women.
Limitations: Short duration. Subjective + photographic endpoints. Industry-funded (Fuji Chemical).
Effects of astaxanthin supplementation on lipid peroxidation ↗
RCT (12-week) n=35 2009 Anti-Aging Med
Iwabayashi M, Fujioka N, Nomoto K, Miyazaki R, Takahashi H, Hibino S, Takahashi Y, Nishikawa K, Nishida M, Yonei Y
Finding: 12mg/day astaxanthin reduced markers of oxidative stress (8-OHdG, malondialdehyde) in healthy subjects with elevated baseline oxidative stress.
Limitations: Small sample. Surrogate biomarkers, not clinical outcomes.

Studied Dose / Protocol

4-12 mg/day, taken with a fat-containing meal for absorption.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Skin elasticity/wrinkles
  • Eye accommodation

Possible Side Effects

  • Mild orange-red skin tint at very high doses (rare)
  • Possibly lowers blood pressure slightly

Who Should Avoid This

  • None at studied doses
