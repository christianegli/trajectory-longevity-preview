Blueberries and Anthocyanin-Rich Berries

Blueberries and other dark berries are a top dietary source of anthocyanins. Modest RCT evidence for blood pressure, vascular function, and cognition.

Bottom Line

Add 1 cup/day of blueberries (or mixed dark berries) to your routine. Small but real cardiovascular and possible cognitive benefit. Frozen are nutritionally equivalent and cheaper. Skip the 'anthocyanin supplement' capsules — anthocyanins from real berries come with fiber and other polyphenols that supplements miss.

What this means for you

Blueberries and other dark berries are loaded with anthocyanins (the deep purple/blue pigments). A 6-month trial showed that one cup of blueberries a day modestly improved blood vessel function and lowered blood pressure by about 5/2 mmHg. Long-term cohort studies link high berry consumption to ~32% lower heart attack risk in younger women. Skip the supplements — eat the actual berries.

How to actually do this

Eat 1 cup (150g) of blueberries or other dark berries (blackberries, raspberries, blackcurrants, tart cherries) most days. Frozen is fine — same anthocyanins, cheaper, available year-round. Add to oatmeal, yogurt, or eat as a snack. Mixed berries deliver complementary anthocyanin profiles. Don't bother with 'anthocyanin extract' capsules — the food matrix matters.

Rough cost: $15-30/month for 1 cup/day of frozen mixed berries (Costco bulk best value). Fresh in season cheaper.

Why it works

The deep purple pigments in berries are antioxidants that help your blood vessels make more nitric oxide (the same molecule Viagra works through, just gentler) which relaxes arteries and improves blood flow. The pigments also seem to enter the brain in small amounts and may directly support memory cells. The benefits are real but modest — these are food-level effects, not drug-level.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Anthocyanins (cyanidin, delphinidin, malvidin, peonidin glycosides) are poorly absorbed (~1-2% bioavailability) but their gut microbiota metabolites (especially protocatechuic acid) reach circulation and have biological activity. Effects: endothelial NO support, ACE inhibition (mild), reduced platelet aggregation, anti-inflammatory cytokine modulation, and cross blood-brain barrier where they accumulate in hippocampus. Pterostilbene and resveratrol-related stilbenoids in berries add complementary polyphenol exposure.

Evidence Summary

Curtis 2019 BLU-FLOW RCT (n=115, 6 months): 1 cup/day blueberries reduced 24h ambulatory BP by ~5/2 mmHg, improved flow-mediated dilation. Krikorian 2010 (n=9 elderly with mild cognitive impairment): blueberry juice improved memory tests over 12 weeks. Cassidy 2013 NHS cohort (n=93,600): higher anthocyanin intake associated with 32% lower MI risk in young/middle-aged women. The strongest evidence is for vascular function and modest BP reduction. Cognitive evidence still preliminary but suggestive.

Key Studies

Blueberries improve biomarkers of cardiometabolic function in participants with metabolic syndrome ↗
RCT (6 months) n=115 2019 Am J Clin Nutr
Curtis PJ, van der Velpen V, Berends L, Jennings A, Feelisch M, Umpleby AM, Evans M, Fernandez BO, Meiss MS, Minnion M, Potter J, Minihane AM, Kay CD, Rimm EB, Cassidy A
Finding: 1 cup/day blueberries (150g freeze-dried equivalent) for 6 months improved flow-mediated dilation (+1.45 percentage points) and 24h ambulatory systolic BP by ~5 mmHg vs placebo in subjects with metabolic syndrome.
Limitations: Subjects had metabolic syndrome — effect may be smaller in healthy subjects. Industry-funded (US Highbush Blueberry Council).
High anthocyanin intake is associated with a reduced risk of myocardial infarction in young and middle-aged women ↗
Prospective cohort (NHS II) n=93600 2013 Circulation
Cassidy A, Mukamal KJ, Liu L, Franz M, Eliassen AH, Rimm EB
Finding: Highest quintile of anthocyanin intake (≥3 servings berries/week) had 32% lower MI risk vs lowest quintile in young/middle-aged women, after adjustment.
Limitations: Observational. Healthy-user bias possible. Female-only cohort.

Studied Dose / Protocol

1 cup (150g) blueberries/day OR equivalent anthocyanin-rich berries (blackberries, raspberries, blackcurrants, tart cherries). Frozen and fresh both effective.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Flow-mediated dilation
  • 24h ambulatory BP
  • Cognitive function (memory)
  • MI incidence (cohort)

Possible Side Effects

  • Mild stomach or gut upset at very high intake
  • Tooth staining (rinse after)

Who Should Avoid This

  • None at dietary doses
  • Anthocyanin supplements at high doses may interact with blood thinners
