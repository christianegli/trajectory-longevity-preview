Bottom Line

If you smoke, quitting is the highest-impact thing you can do for longevity, by an enormous margin. There is no supplement or drug on this site that comes close. Use NRT, varenicline, support; whatever works.

What this means for you

If you smoke, quitting is the single highest-impact thing you can possibly do for your health and longevity. Nothing else on this site comes close — not exercise, not diet, not any drug. Quitting at any age extends life; quitting before 40 erases nearly all the excess mortality risk. Use whatever works: nicotine replacement, varenicline, bupropion, hypnosis, support groups. Just quit.

How to actually do this

  • Pick a quit date in the next 2 weeks. Tell people. Make it real.
  • Use medication. Quit success doubles with help. Best options: varenicline (Chantix) or combined nicotine patch + lozenge/gum.
  • Bupropion (Wellbutrin) is an alternative if varenicline isn't tolerated.
  • Most people relapse 3–7 times before successful quit. That's normal. Keep trying.
  • Avoid your old triggers (specific places, drinks, social settings) for the first 1–3 months.
  • If you vape: that's better than smoking but not zero risk. Plan to quit vaping too once cigarette-free for 6+ months.
  • Free help: smokefree.gov, your country's quitline, local nicotine support groups.
Rough cost: Quit-aid medications: $50–$200/month for 3–6 months. Replaces cigarettes which cost $200–$500/month — net massive savings.

Every single person who smokes. Full stop.

Why it works

Tobacco smoke damages your body in dozens of ways at once: poisons the cells lining your lungs, drives mutations that cause cancer, narrows and stiffens blood vessels, makes blood clot more readily, and accelerates aging across virtually every organ. Quitting starts reversing some of this within hours (carbon monoxide clears) and continues for years. By 10–15 years smoke-free, lung cancer risk has dropped by half; cardiovascular risk normalizes faster than that.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Tobacco smoke contains carcinogens, oxidants, and vasoactive compounds. Drives lung cancer, COPD, atherosclerosis, multiple cancers, accelerated aging across nearly every organ system.

Evidence Summary

Smoking is the single most reversible mortality risk factor known. Doll 2004 (50-year follow-up of British male doctors): lifelong smokers lost an average of 10 years of life compared to never-smokers. Jha 2013 (NEJM, n=200K US adults): quitting before age 40 avoids ~90% of excess smoking-related mortality; benefit accrues at any quit age. Million Women Study (Pirie 2013, Lancet, n=1.3M): triple all-cause mortality for lifelong smokers; women who quit before 40 avoided >90% of excess mortality. Mechanisms span CV disease, ~30 cancers, COPD, immune dysfunction. Treatment evidence (separate items): combination NRT + behavioral support roughly doubles quit success vs willpower alone; varenicline is most effective single agent.

21st-Century Hazards of Smoking and Benefits of Cessation in the United States ↗
Pooled cohort analysis n=200000 2013 NEJM
Jha et al.
Finding: Current smokers in the US lose at least 10 years of life expectancy vs never-smokers. Quitting before age 40 reduces excess smoking-related mortality by ~90%; quitting at any age reduces risk substantially.
Limitations: Self-reported smoking status; observational.
Smoking and Lung Cancer in the Million Women Study ↗
Prospective cohort n=1300000 2013 Lancet
Pirie et al.
Finding: Lifelong smokers had triple the all-cause mortality of never-smokers; women who quit before age 40 avoided more than 90% of the excess mortality. Confirmed the dose-effect of quitting age.
Limitations: Female-only cohort; UK population only.
