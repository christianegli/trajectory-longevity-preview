# Trajectory.quest — Episode 001
## The dirt on Easter Island that might add 15 years to your life

**Hosts:** Andrew & Ava
**Date:** 2026-05-15
**Topic:** Rapamycin
**Source:** /Users/atlas/org/longevity-site/content/briefs/2026-05-14-rapamycin.md

---

[Ava]: Okay, so a bunch of scientists fly to Easter Island. They scrape some dirt off one of those giant stone heads. And what they find in that dirt might add fifteen good years to your life.

[Andrew]: Wait. Dirt? Off a statue? That's the start?

[Ava]: That's the start. 1972. They take it back to the lab, they look inside the dirt, and they find a bacteria. Then they look inside the bacteria, and they find one specific molecule. And they name the molecule after the local name for the island. Rapa Nui.

[Andrew]: Rapamycin.

[Ava]: Rapamycin. Yeah.

[Andrew]: Okay. So here's what I want to know. Of all the molecules people have studied for living longer — and there are a lot of them — why is this one a big deal?

[Ava]: Because it's the only one that has reliably done the thing. Every species they've fed it to, it makes them live longer. Yeast. Worms. Flies. Mice.

[Andrew]: Hold on. Every single one?

[Ava]: Every single one they've tested.

[Andrew]: That's actually kind of wild. Because most of these compounds, you read the headline, you go look at the data, and it's like, well, it worked in this one weird strain of fruit fly under these specific conditions. This one just works.

[Ava]: It just works. And in old mice — mice that are already kind of past their prime — it adds about twenty-five percent to the time they have left.

[Andrew]: Twenty-five percent. So if you do the math on that for a person?

[Ava]: Roughly fifteen extra good years.

[Andrew]: Fifteen extra good years.

[Ava]: That's the human equivalent. If it works in humans the way it works in mice. Which is the giant if. We'll come back to that.

[Andrew]: Okay. So before we get to whether this works in people — what is it actually doing? Like, what's the trick?

[Ava]: The trick is that it makes your cells think there's no food.

[Andrew]: Wait, what?

[Ava]: Yeah. So your body has two basic modes it can be in. Mode one is — there's plenty of food, let's grow, let's build new stuff, let's make more cells. Mode two is — uh oh, food is scarce, let's stop growing and start cleaning up.

[Andrew]: Cleaning up what?

[Ava]: All the junk that piles up inside your cells over time. Broken proteins. Damaged little energy factories called mitochondria. Bits of DNA that got messed up. Your cells actually have a built-in cleaning crew that recycles all that stuff.

[Andrew]: And that cleaning crew slows down as you get older.

[Ava]: Way down. And a lot of what we think of as aging — feeling slower, healing slower, the body just kind of breaking down — a lot of that is the cleaning crew falling behind on the trash.

[Andrew]: So rapamycin basically lies to your cells. Tells them, hey, no food, get back to work cleaning.

[Ava]: Pretty much. It flips the switch from build mode into clean mode, even when you've eaten plenty.

[Andrew]: That's a really clean story. Almost too clean. What's the catch?

[Ava]: The catch is — every result I just told you is in animals.

[Andrew]: Right.

[Ava]: The twenty-five percent number. The lifespan thing. That's mice. Yeast. Flies. We don't yet have the equivalent number for humans. Because to get that number you'd have to give a bunch of healthy people rapamycin and then wait, like, fifty years.

[Andrew]: Which nobody's done.

[Ava]: Which nobody's done. And honestly, nobody's going to in a hurry.

[Andrew]: Okay, but is this a brand-new molecule? Like, has anyone actually taken it?

[Ava]: This is the part that surprised me. Rapamycin is already an approved drug. It's been used for decades. The medical name is sirolimus.

[Andrew]: Wait, really? For what?

[Ava]: They give it to people who get an organ transplant. To stop the body from rejecting the new organ.

[Andrew]: So we know what it does in humans at high doses for years.

[Ava]: We know a lot. The safety picture at transplant doses is pretty well mapped out. Side effects exist, they're known.

[Andrew]: But the dose for not-rejecting-a-kidney and the dose for maybe-living-longer — those are not the same dose.

[Ava]: Probably very different. The longevity idea is much smaller doses, taken much less often. Maybe once a week. And we don't actually know the right number yet. That's one of the open questions.

[Andrew]: So we know the molecule is real. We know it works in animals. We know it's safe enough that doctors give it to transplant patients. We just don't know — does the animal magic carry over to people, and if it does, how much do you take, and how often.

[Ava]: That is exactly the situation.

[Andrew]: Has anyone actually run a trial in regular healthy people?

[Ava]: A small one. It's called PEARL. They gave low-dose rapamycin to healthy adults and tracked them. They saw some modest improvements in body composition. They didn't see a major safety problem. But it was small. And they weren't measuring lifespan, because — again — that takes fifty years.

[Andrew]: So it's a hint, not a verdict.

[Ava]: It's a hint. There are bigger trials running now.

[Andrew]: Okay so let me try to summarize this for somebody who's listening on a run or doing the dishes. The story is — fifty years ago, dirt on Easter Island. Inside the dirt, a molecule. The molecule extends life in every animal we've tested. It works by tricking cells into clean-up mode. It's already an approved medicine, but at much higher doses than the longevity people would use. In healthy people we have one small trial that looks okay but doesn't prove anything yet.

[Ava]: That's the whole picture.

[Andrew]: So what do you actually do with that? If you're not a scientist, you're not in a trial — what's the takeaway?

[Ava]: Honestly? Don't go buy this online and start taking it. That's a real temptation, and it's a bad idea.

[Andrew]: Why a bad idea?

[Ava]: Because the dose matters a lot, the timing matters a lot, and what you're taking it with matters a lot. And nobody knows yet what the right answers are for healthy people. You're basically running an experiment of one on yourself with a powerful immune-system drug. That's not a great hobby.

[Andrew]: Right. So the smart move is — pay attention.

[Ava]: Pay attention. Watch the trials. If your doctor brings it up, have the conversation. If you can find a real research protocol to join, that's the cleanest path. And know that this is the single most promising thing on the longevity shelf right now. Promising. Not proven.

[Andrew]: Promising, not proven. That's a good way to say it.

[Ava]: That's the honest version.

[Andrew]: Okay, so — Easter Island, dirt, twenty-five percent more life in mice, cleaning crew, possibly fifteen extra years for humans, do not buy this online.

[Ava]: That is the episode.
