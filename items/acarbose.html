Acarbose

Bottom Line

Quietly intriguing. Strong mouse data, plausible human benefit via postprandial glucose smoothing, low cost. Underused. GI tolerability is the practical barrier.

What this means for you

Acarbose is a cheap diabetes drug that slows how fast your gut absorbs starches and sugars from a meal — flattening the blood-sugar spike afterward. In mice it consistently extends lifespan. In humans the longevity case is plausible but not proven. The catch: it ferments undigested carbs in your gut, which causes a lot of gas. That side effect is why most people who could benefit don't take it.

How to actually do this

  • Talk to your doctor; this is prescription. Easier to get if you have pre-diabetes or insulin resistance.
  • Start tiny: 25 mg with the first bite of one meal a day. Increase slowly.
  • Target dose: 50–100 mg with each carb-containing meal.
  • Take it with food, NOT before. Skip it if the meal is very low-carb (no benefit).
  • Expect significant gas/bloating in the first weeks. It usually improves; if it doesn't, the drug isn't for you.
  • Avoid if you have IBD, severe kidney disease, or any history of bowel obstruction.
  • If you have hypoglycemia while on insulin or sulfonylureas, treat with glucose tablets, NOT sucrose (acarbose blocks sucrose absorption).
Rough cost: $15–$40/month generic.

Worth considering if

People with pre-diabetes, post-meal blood sugar spikes, or anyone who eats a high-carb diet and wants to smooth the metabolic impact without changing food.

Probably skip if

Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, UC). Severe kidney impairment. History of bowel obstruction. Anyone who can't tolerate gas/bloating.

Helps with

Diabetes / metabolic Heart disease Cancer

Why it works

After you eat a carb-heavy meal, an enzyme in your gut wall breaks starch into sugar so it can absorb into your blood. Acarbose blocks that enzyme. Result: less sugar absorbed quickly, smaller spike, gentler insulin response. The undigested carbs continue down to your colon where bacteria ferment them — producing gas (the side effect) and short-chain fatty acids that may themselves be beneficial for your gut lining and metabolism.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Inhibits intestinal alpha-glucosidases → slows carbohydrate absorption → blunts postprandial glucose. Net: lower glucose excursions, mild weight benefit, increased SCFA production from undigested carbohydrates.

Evidence Summary

ITP showed acarbose extends median male mouse lifespan 22% (largest single-agent effect in ITP for males). Female effect smaller. STOP-NIDDM trial showed acarbose reduced T2DM incidence and possibly cardiovascular events in pre-diabetics. Widely used in Asia for diabetes; underused in Western practice. GI side effects (gas, bloating) limit adoption.

Key Studies

Acarbose, 17-α-estradiol, and nordihydroguaiaretic acid extend mouse lifespan (ITP, Harrison 2014) ↗
RCT (mice) n=1500 2014 Aging Cell
Harrison et al.
Finding: Acarbose extended median male mouse lifespan by 22%, female by 5%.
Limitations: Mouse; sex-specific; mechanism debated.
Acarbose for prevention of type 2 diabetes mellitus (STOP-NIDDM) ↗
RCT n=1429 2002 Lancet
Chiasson et al.
Finding: 25% reduction in T2DM incidence over 3.3 years in IGT patients. Cardiovascular event reduction also seen.
Limitations: Single trial for CV endpoint; not replicated for mortality.

Studied Dose / Protocol

50-100mg with each meal, titrated up.

Possible Side Effects

  • Bloating
  • Diarrhea
  • Mild liver enzyme rise (rare)

Who Should Avoid This

  • Severe kidney problems
  • stomach or gut obstruction
