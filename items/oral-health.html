Bottom Line

Floss. Get cleanings. Treat gum disease aggressively. Underrated longevity lever masquerading as a hygiene chore.

What this means for you

Gum disease is one of the most underrated drivers of long-term health damage. Chronically inflamed gums seed bacteria into your bloodstream that show up in heart-artery plaques and Alzheimer's brains. Floss every day, get cleanings twice a year, and treat gum disease aggressively if you have it. This is a longevity lever masquerading as a hygiene chore.

How to actually do this

  • Brush twice a day for 2 full minutes. Electric toothbrush (Oral-B or Sonicare) is meaningfully better than manual.
  • Floss every night. Not 'when food is stuck' — every night. Water flossers (Waterpik) work for those who hate string floss.
  • Professional cleaning every 6 months. Every 3–4 months if you have any history of gum disease.
  • If your dentist mentions 'pockets' or 'periodontitis': take it seriously. Get a deep cleaning (scaling and root planing). May need a periodontist.
  • Notice bleeding gums when brushing or flossing? That's not normal. It means inflammation. Floss MORE, not less — should improve in 1–2 weeks. If it doesn't, see a dentist.
  • Tongue scraping in the morning reduces bacterial load.
  • Avoid antibacterial mouthwash daily — it disrupts the helpful oral microbiome too. Use only short-term if directed.
Rough cost: Electric toothbrush $30–$200 one-time. Floss $20/year. Cleanings $80–$200 each, twice a year. Treating gum disease can run $1000–$3000+ but stops the damage.

Worth considering if

Everyone. Especially anyone with cardiovascular risk factors, family history of dementia, or diabetes (bidirectional relationship).

Probably skip if

Nobody.

Why it works

Your mouth has its own ecosystem of bacteria. When you don't disrupt it daily (with brushing and flossing), certain bad actors — especially Porphyromonas gingivalis — start to dominate. They cause your gums to inflame, bleed slightly, and become a leaky border. From there, the bacteria and the inflammatory chemicals leak into your bloodstream, where they contribute to artery plaque and have even been found inside Alzheimer's-affected brain tissue. Treating gum disease isn't just about teeth — it's about systemic inflammation.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Chronic gum inflammation seeds bacteria (Porphyromonas gingivalis, others) systemically, drives chronic low-grade inflammation. P. gingivalis specifically detected in atherosclerotic plaques and Alzheimer's brain tissue.

Evidence Summary

Multiple cohort studies show periodontitis associated with 25-50% increased CV mortality, increased Alzheimer's, increased pancreatic and oral cancers. RCTs of periodontal treatment show improvements in endothelial function and HbA1c. Direct mortality RCTs lacking, but mechanistic + cohort evidence consistent.
