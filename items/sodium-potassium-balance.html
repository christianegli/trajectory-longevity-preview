Sodium / Potassium Balance

Lower sodium and higher food-based potassium reliably lower blood pressure. The effect is boring, measurable, and highly actionable.

Effectiveness
6/10
Size of effect IF real

9/10

Evidence Level
A
Multiple human RCTs with mortality/major-event endpoints. Regulatory consensus.

Bottom Line

If blood pressure is high, sodium reduction plus potassium-rich foods is one of the highest-certainty nutrition moves. Use food first; be careful with potassium chloride salt substitutes if kidney function or medications make potassium risky.

What this means for you

For blood pressure, sodium and potassium are the nutrition levers most people can actually measure. Less restaurant/packaged food lowers sodium; more beans, potatoes, greens, yogurt, fruit, and nuts raises potassium. The goal is not electrolyte biohacking. It is moving blood pressure down.

How to actually do this

  • Estimate sodium for one normal week. Restaurant meals and packaged foods are usually the source.
  • Aim for under 2,300 mg/day; under 1,500 mg/day if hypertension and tolerable.
  • Add potassium from food: beans, lentils, potatoes, leafy greens, yogurt, bananas, citrus, nuts.
  • Consider potassium-chloride salt substitute only if kidney function is normal and medications allow it.
  • Use home BP readings, not vibes, to assess effect.
Rough cost: $0-10/month. Salt substitutes are cheap; produce/legumes may change grocery mix.

Worth considering if

Adults with elevated blood pressure, high processed-food intake, or low fruit/vegetable/legume intake.

Probably skip if

People with CKD, hyperkalemia, potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, or heart failure should ask a clinician before potassium salts.

Helps with

Heart disease Diabetes / metabolic

Evidence Summary

DASH-Sodium, salt-substitution trials, and blood-pressure meta-analyses converge: sodium reduction lowers BP, potassium-rich patterns lower BP, and replacing sodium chloride with potassium chloride reduces stroke/cardiovascular events in high-risk populations. The safety caveat is potassium handling in CKD and certain medications.

Key Studies

No primary studies cataloged yet.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Sodium reduction to roughly 1,500-2,300 mg/day; potassium increase through food or partial salt substitution.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Blood pressure
  • Stroke
  • Cardiovascular events

Possible Side Effects

  • Food tastes flat for 2-3 weeks while palate adapts
  • Potassium salts can cause dangerous high potassium in susceptible people

Who Should Avoid This

  • chronic kidney disease with high potassium risk
  • Potassium-sparing diuretics
  • Unmonitored ACE inhibitor or ARB use with potassium salt substitutes
