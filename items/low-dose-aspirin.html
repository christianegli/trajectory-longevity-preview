Low-Dose Aspirin

Useful for secondary prevention. Net benefit in primary prevention is now disputed.

Antiplatelet OTC
Effectiveness
4/10
Size of effect IF real

Evidence Level
A
Multiple human RCTs with mortality/major-event endpoints. Regulatory consensus.

Bottom Line

If you've had an event, take it as your cardiologist directs. If you haven't and you're otherwise low-risk, the modern evidence does not support taking it just for longevity.

What this means for you

If you've already had a heart attack or stroke, low-dose aspirin reduces the chance of another one and is usually worth the bleeding risk. If you haven't — and most people haven't — the modern evidence no longer supports taking it 'just in case.' The bleeding risk often eats the cardiovascular benefit. Don't start daily aspirin without a real reason.

How to actually do this

  • If your cardiologist already has you on it after a heart attack, stent, or stroke: keep taking it. Don't stop without checking with them.
  • If you don't have established heart disease: don't start it 'for prevention' on your own. The 2018–2022 trials (ARRIVE, ASPREE, ASCEND) shifted the consensus.
  • If you're considering it: discuss with your doctor based on personal cardiovascular risk score AND bleeding risk score. There are calculators they can use.
  • If on it: take 75–100 mg enteric-coated daily, with food. Avoid alcohol and NSAIDs (extra GI bleeding).
  • Stop 5–7 days before any surgery (per surgeon's instructions).
  • Don't give to children or teens with viral illness — Reye's syndrome risk.
Rough cost: $2–$8/month.

Worth considering if

People with established cardiovascular disease (post-MI, post-stroke, stents, established peripheral artery disease) under physician guidance.

Probably skip if

Most healthy adults without prior cardiovascular events. Active GI ulcer or recent bleeding. Severe liver disease. Children with viral illness.

Helps with

Heart disease

Why it works

Aspirin permanently disables one of the chemicals platelets use to clump together and form clots. Less clot formation means fewer heart attacks and clot-type strokes — but also more bleeding from the stomach and brain. For someone with established heart disease, the trade is favorable: fewer events outweigh the bleeding. For someone healthy, the math has flipped in recent large trials. The bleeding risk grows with age, but so does the cardiovascular risk — so it's an individual calculation.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 → reduces thromboxane A2 → reduces platelet aggregation. Anti-inflammatory and possibly chemopreventive effects.

Evidence Summary

ASPREE (2018) tested low-dose aspirin in healthy adults ≥70. Found no mortality benefit, slight bleeding increase, surprising increase in cancer mortality on aspirin. ARRIVE and ASCEND showed similar marginal-or-negative net effects in moderate-risk populations. For SECONDARY prevention (post-MI, post-stroke), aspirin remains standard of care with clear net benefit. USPSTF 2022 walked back primary prevention recommendations.

Key Studies

Effect of Aspirin on All-Cause Mortality in Healthy Elderly (ASPREE) ↗
RCT n=19114 2018 NEJM
McNeil et al.
Finding: No reduction in CVD events; HR for all-cause mortality 1.14 (slightly worse on aspirin); increased major hemorrhage.
Limitations: Healthy elderly cohort already low-risk.
Aspirin in patients with previous MI or stroke (ATT meta-analysis) ↗
Meta-analysis n=17000 2009 Lancet
Antithrombotic Trialists' Collaboration
Finding: Secondary prevention: ~20% reduction in serious vascular events. Primary prevention net benefit uncertain.
Limitations: Pooled trials; heterogeneous populations.

Studied Dose / Protocol

75-100 mg/day enteric-coated for cardiovascular indications.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • All-cause mortality
  • MACE
  • Cancer incidence/mortality
  • Major hemorrhage

Possible Side Effects

  • stomach or gut bleeding
  • Hemorrhagic stroke (small absolute increase)
  • Easy bruising

Who Should Avoid This

  • Active bleeding
  • Recent surgery
  • Severe liver disease
  • Children with viral infections (Reye's)
