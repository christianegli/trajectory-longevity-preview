Cocoa Flavanols

Plant compound from cocoa with the strongest RCT evidence of any 'food' supplement for heart-disease death reduction.

Bottom Line

Worth considering for cardiovascular protection, especially if you have elevated BP or family history. The evidence is real but modest per-person. Get the supplement form (CocoaVia, ~$30/mo) — eating dark chocolate doesn't deliver enough flavanols and adds sugar.

What this means for you

A plant compound found in cocoa that helps your blood vessels work better. The biggest study ever done on a supplement (21,000 people over 4 years) showed people taking it had 27% fewer heart-related deaths than people taking placebo. Mild blood pressure improvement too.

How to actually do this

Take 500-900 mg cocoa flavanols/day in supplement form (CocoaVia Cardio Health, Acticor, or equivalent). One serving per day, anytime, with or without food. Stick with it — benefit takes months and the COSMOS trial only saw effect over 3-4 years.

Rough cost: $25-35/month for supplement-grade products (CocoaVia, similar). Generic cocoa powder is much cheaper but flavanol content varies widely.

Worth considering if

['You have elevated blood pressure or family history of heart disease', "You're 50+ and looking for low-risk cardiovascular hedges", 'You already do the basics (no smoking, exercise, statin if indicated) and want one more lever']

Probably skip if

["You think dark chocolate counts — it doesn't (way too little flavanol per serving, plus sugar)", "You're already on optimal BP control and statin — marginal additional benefit", 'Budget-constrained — sauna or omega-3 likely deliver more per dollar']

Helps with

Heart disease

Why it works

Cocoa flavanols help your body make more nitric oxide, which relaxes your blood vessels. Better-relaxed blood vessels mean lower blood pressure and less strain on your heart over decades.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Epicatechin (the dominant flavanol) activates endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS), increasing NO bioavailability. This reduces vascular tone, improves flow-mediated dilation, and modulates platelet aggregation. Long-term: anti-inflammatory effects on vascular endothelium reduce atherogenesis.

Evidence Summary

COSMOS (n=21,442, 3.6 years, RCT) — primary endpoint missed, but secondary CV-mortality reduced ~27% (p=0.004). Multiple shorter RCTs show consistent BP-lowering (~2-4 mmHg) and improved flow-mediated dilation. Effect modest per-person but mechanism is plausible and effect size at scale is meaningful. Caveat: the supplement form (~500mg flavanols/day) used in COSMOS is not the same as eating chocolate — chocolate is mostly sugar+fat; the flavanol concentration is too low and inconsistent.

Key Studies

Effect of cocoa flavanol supplementation for the prevention of cardiovascular disease events: COSMOS ↗
RCT (placebo-controlled, double-blind) n=21442 2022 Am J Clin Nutr
Sesso HD et al.
Finding: Primary composite CV endpoint not significant (HR 0.97). Secondary CV mortality reduced 27% (HR 0.73, p=0.004). 500mg flavanols/day (with 80mg epicatechin) vs placebo over 3.6 years.
Limitations: Primary endpoint missed. Adherence ~70%. Secondary outcomes only suggestive — not regulatory-grade evidence on its own.
Cocoa as treatment for hypertension and pre-hypertension (Cochrane Review 2017, Ried) ↗
Meta-analysis (35 RCTs) n=1804 2017 Cochrane Database Syst Rev
Ried K et al.
Finding: Daily cocoa flavanols reduced systolic BP by 1.8 mmHg and diastolic BP by 1.8 mmHg vs placebo. Effect strongest in hypertensive patients.
Limitations: Short-term studies (mostly <3 months). Heterogeneous flavanol content.

Studied Dose / Protocol

500-900 mg cocoa flavanols/day (with ~80 mg epicatechin), supplement form (CocoaVia or equivalent).

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Cardiovascular mortality
  • Blood pressure
  • Flow-mediated dilation
  • Composite CV events

Possible Side Effects

  • Caffeine sensitivity (some products contain residual caffeine)

Who Should Avoid This

  • None at studied doses
