Fisetin

Senolytic flavonoid; clears senescent cells in mice. Human trials underway.

Bottom Line

Most credible 'longevity supplement' with a real mechanism. Hit-and-run dosing is biologically reasonable. Watch for the Mayo human trial readouts. Until then: low cost, low risk, plausible upside.

What this means for you

Fisetin is a plant compound (found in strawberries, in trace amounts) that selectively kills off 'zombie' senescent cells in mice — old damaged cells that hang around secreting inflammation. The Mayo Clinic is running human trials right now. It's the most credible 'longevity supplement' with a real mechanism, and the protocol is unusual: high doses for 2 days a month, not daily.

How to actually do this

  • Hit-and-run protocol: 1000–1500 mg/day for 2 consecutive days, once per month. Not daily.
  • Take with fat (olive oil, full-fat yogurt, fatty meal) — bioavailability is poor without it.
  • Look for 'novusetin' or liposomal fisetin formulations — better absorbed than plain capsules.
  • Standard caps (100–500 mg/day daily) are the alternative if you don't want pulse dosing — weaker theoretical case but sometimes used.
  • Stop and reassess if you're on anticoagulants — theoretical interaction.
  • Watch the Mayo Clinic Phase 2 trial readouts (search 'fisetin senolytic clinical trial' periodically).
  • Don't combine with active cancer treatment without oncologist sign-off.
Rough cost: $15–$40/month with monthly pulse dosing.

Worth considering if

Adults over 50 willing to bet on the senolytic hypothesis with modest cost and reasonable safety profile. People with osteoarthritis or chronic low-grade inflammation may have biggest theoretical upside.

Probably skip if

Active cancer (theoretical concerns either direction). On anticoagulants. Pregnant.

Why it works

As you age, some of your cells stop dividing but refuse to die. They sit there leaking inflammatory chemicals that age the tissue around them. Senolytics are drugs that find these zombie cells and trigger their self-destruct sequence, leaving healthy cells alone. In mice, periodic senolytic treatment delays many age-related diseases. Fisetin is the cleanest natural-product senolytic identified so far. The 'two-day pulse' protocol mimics how it was used in the mouse studies.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Selectively induces apoptosis in senescent cells in vitro and in vivo (mice). Senescent cells accumulate with age and secrete pro-inflammatory factors (SASP); clearing them rejuvenates tissues in animal models.

Evidence Summary

Yousefzadeh et al. (2018) screened 10 flavonoids for senolytic activity; fisetin was most potent. Late-life dosing extended median mouse lifespan ~10%. Human trials at Mayo Clinic (frailty, COVID, kidney disease) ongoing; preliminary signals on inflammation markers. Typical 'hit-and-run' protocol: 20mg/kg/day for 2 days, monthly. Bioavailability low; absorption window debated.
