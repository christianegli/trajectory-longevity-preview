Bottom Line

The poster child for hype outpacing evidence. Skip it. If you want polyphenols, eat berries, dark chocolate, EVOO and green tea — same molecular family, food matrix included.

What this means for you

Resveratrol is the famous red-wine compound that started the modern longevity-supplement industry. After 20 years of research, the evidence in humans is genuinely disappointing. The original sirtuin-activation story turned out to be a lab artifact. The bioavailability is terrible. Skip it. If you want polyphenols, eat berries, dark chocolate, EVOO, and drink green tea.

How to actually do this

  • Skip the supplement.
  • If you want polyphenols, get them from food: berries, dark chocolate (70%+), EVOO, green tea, red onions, walnuts. Same molecular family, with the food matrix that actually works.
  • Don't drink wine 'for the resveratrol' — the alcohol cost (cancer, sleep, BP) outweighs any polyphenol benefit a hundredfold.
  • If you're taking resveratrol now: it's not dangerous to continue, just probably not doing much.
  • Avoid high doses if on anticoagulants or hormone-sensitive cancer history (mild estrogenic activity at high doses).
Rough cost: $15–$50/month wasted.

Why it works

When researchers proposed in the 2000s that resveratrol activates 'longevity genes' (sirtuins), the supplement industry took off. Subsequent careful work showed the lab assay was misleading: resveratrol doesn't actually activate sirtuins directly in physiological conditions. It does have some antioxidant and AMPK-modulating effects, but the bioavailability is so poor that meaningful blood levels are hard to achieve. Multiple human trials have shown disappointingly small or zero effects on the markers of aging. The hype outpaced the data, and the data has not caught up.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Originally proposed as a SIRT1 activator. Subsequent work showed direct sirtuin activation is artifactual under standard assay conditions. Has antioxidant and AMPK-related effects. Bioavailability is poor.

Evidence Summary

Sinclair lab's 2003 Nature paper launched the field. Subsequent RCTs in humans largely showed no significant longevity-relevant outcomes; some metabolic benefit in T2DM. ITP showed NO mouse lifespan extension at any tested dose. The SIRT1 mechanism was largely retracted/revised. Bioavailability remains a barrier (sub-1% in most preparations).
