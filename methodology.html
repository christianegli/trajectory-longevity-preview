Methodology

Two scores

Every intervention is graded on two independent dimensions:

Effectiveness (1–10)

Size of the longevity effect if it's real. Independent of how confident we are.

  • 1–3 — Marginal or biomarker-only signal.
  • 4–6 — Modest disease-specific or surrogate endpoint improvement.
  • 7–8 — Meaningful all-cause mortality or healthspan signal.
  • 9–10 — Robust species-extending in mammals OR strong human RCT mortality reduction.

Certainty (1–10)

How confident we are the effect is real in humans. Independent of size.

  • 1–3 — In-vitro, lab worm, or theoretical only.
  • 4–5 — Mouse/rodent studies, mechanism plausible, no human data.
  • 6–7 — Human cohort/observational data, small RCTs.
  • 8–9 — Multiple human RCTs, consistent direction.
  • 10 — Large mortality RCTs, regulatory consensus.

Why split them?

Many interventions have large effect sizes that are uncertain (e.g. rapamycin: dramatic in mice, not yet proven in humans). Others have certain effects that are small (e.g. low-dose aspirin: well-studied, modest cardiovascular benefit, GI risk). A single composite score hides this. Two scores don't.

Evidence levels (A–E)

  • A — Multiple human RCTs with mortality/major-event endpoints. Regulatory consensus.
  • B — Human RCTs with biomarker or disease-specific endpoints, or strong cohort evidence.
  • C — Small human studies, observational data, mechanism plausible.
  • D — Mouse/mammalian studies, no human longevity data.
  • E — In-vitro / lab worm / theoretical mechanism only.

What gets included

An intervention makes the list if it has any serious longevity-relevant evidence — mammalian lifespan extension, human mortality RCT, or strong mechanistic + epidemiological signal. Things with only in-vitro evidence are excluded unless they're being widely taken anyway (so you can see why we rate them low).

What this is not

Not medical advice. Not a recommendation. Not exhaustive. The longevity literature is contradictory and moves fast. Use this as a map for talking to your physician, not a substitute.

Editorial policy

Every intervention page is reviewed by Trajectory editorial against the rubric above. We work from primary sources — RCTs, meta-analyses, mortality cohorts — and we cite them on the page, with the finding and the limitation. No story is ever told without the trial behind it being one click away.

  • Review cadence. Every item carries a last_reviewed date and a next review due date six months out. When new trial data lands, scores can move down as well as up.
  • Inclusion. An intervention enters the catalogue if it has any serious longevity-relevant human or mammalian evidence, or if it's being widely used and people deserve a graded read.
  • Removal. An intervention gets removed only if a higher-quality study supersedes the entire evidence base, or if a safety signal makes it actively harmful to recommend.
  • Score changes. Score changes are logged in What's new with the trigger study and the rationale.
  • Corrections. If a study link is broken, a dose is wrong, or a citation misrepresents a study's finding, write to editorial@trajectory.care — we'll fix and mark the correction.
