The quest gives you 5–12 things, not 73

#methodology#wizard#philosophy

The catalogue has 73 interventions. The wizard hands you somewhere between 5 and 12. That's intentional.

Most longevity tools fail at this step. They take what they know about you and spit out a list of everything plausibly relevant. You end up with 40 items, decision fatigue, and you do nothing. The wizard's job is to give you the things that move the needle for you, in priority order, with explicit guidance on how to start.

Here's how it does that.

Two passes, not one

When you finish the questions, the wizard runs two passes over the catalogue.

Pass 1: Leverage points. What are the 1–3 biggest gaps in your current setup? If you don't sleep, sleep is a leverage point. If you smoke, quitting is the leverage point — bigger than anything else combined. If your VO2max is low, structured cardio shows up. These get scored 70-100 priority and marked leverage:true.

The leverage logic is straightforward: it looks for the biggest expected delta in your situation. Someone with great cardio fitness who doesn't smoke and sleeps well doesn't get those things rebadged as "leverage" — they're already doing them. The wizard isn't trying to reach a target list size; it's trying to find the actual gaps.

Pass 2: Quick wins. High-evidence, low-effort interventions that match your goals and don't conflict with your conditions. Vitamin D if you live somewhere grey and haven't tested. Creatine if you do any resistance training. Omega-3 if you don't eat fish twice a week. Each scored 28-50 priority based on how strongly the evidence matches your specific situation.

Caps by appetite

You told the wizard whether you want a small / medium / large stack. That sets a hard cap:

  • Small (5 items): Foundation only. The 5 things doing the most work.
  • Medium (8 items): Foundation + the 1–3 conditions where you'd benefit most.
  • Large (12 items): Wider net, including some Tier-3 (frontier) items if you opted in.

Items above the cap don't make it into the result, even if they technically match. This is the curation — the willingness to not mention something rather than make the list look comprehensive.

Three result buckets

Everything that survives gets sorted into three groups:

Leverage points (sage). Ranked at the top. Each comes with explicit coach text — why this matters for you specifically — and a starter protocol. Not "consider 1-3g daily" but "Start with 2g EPA-rich omega-3 daily, with food, for 8 weeks. Track mood weekly with PHQ-2."

Quick wins (sky). Lower priority, lower friction. Vitamin D, creatine, dietary fibre, walking habit. The "if you're going to do anything beyond the foundations" tier.

Discuss with doctor (clay). Anything prescription, anything that needs labs, anything with real interaction risk. Statins for someone with elevated CV risk who isn't already on one. Metformin for pre-diabetes. GLP-1s for severe obesity. The wizard doesn't gate these — it surfaces them with explicit framing as conversations to have.

What we exclude on purpose

If the wizard would have recommended something but a contraindication fired, it shows up in a separate flagged section with the reason. Pregnancy alone bans 20 slugs. Anticoagulants ban aspirin, curcumin, D+Q, fisetin. CKD blocks creatine, SGLT2s, high-protein protocols. Cancer history blocks rapamycin and alcohol.

This is more useful than silently dropping items. If you take warfarin and the wizard would normally recommend curcumin, you should know curcumin would otherwise have been on your list — that helps you ask the right question of your doctor about whether the bleeding-risk concern applies to your specific dose and target.

Adaptive framing

If the wizard finds you're already doing most of the big stuff — say you're a 35-year-old non-smoker with great cardiovascular fitness, no conditions, and a Mediterranean diet — it doesn't pretend to find leverage points that aren't there. The result page reframes:

You're already doing the big stuff. Here are the smaller refinements that might matter at the margin.

That's the honest output for someone who's optimised. It produces 2-3 small additions, not 12 items of busywork.

What the wizard doesn't do

A few things deliberately not in scope:

It doesn't replace a doctor. If something on your list is a prescription drug, the wizard says so explicitly and frames it as a conversation, not a recommendation.

It doesn't use any black-box AI. The whole thing is deterministic JavaScript. Same inputs → same output. We can audit the rules. So can you.

It doesn't send your data anywhere. It's 100% client-side. The wizard runs in your browser and never makes a network request with your answers. If you close the tab, it's gone.

It doesn't pretend to know your labs. If you don't know your ApoB, it doesn't ask you to guess. It uses the rest of your inputs (age, family history, conditions, lifestyle) to estimate cardiovascular concern and surfaces "talk to your doctor about ApoB" as an action.

The honest test

We tuned the wizard with a hostile audit: 8 personas across the difficult cases. 80-year-old frail patient on 5 meds. 25-year-old healthy athlete. 32-year-old trying to conceive. 55-year-old cancer survivor with depression. Each ran through the rules. Each was checked against the question: did the wizard give them sensible advice for their situation, with the right contraindications, without being either preachy or comprehensive?

When the audit found mistakes, we fixed them. Depressed patients now see omega-3 surfaced with EPA-specific protocol notes. Sleep-deprived patients see a bright-light morning quickwin. Low-output personas (already optimised) see the adaptive headline instead of an apologetic "only 2 items."

What it costs

Free. No login. No email capture. No upsell to a coach. The wizard is a static page running JavaScript in your browser. The catalogue is open data; if you want to read every item's evidence summary directly, you can do that without ever taking the quest.

We built the wizard because most people don't want to read every item's evidence summary. They want to know what to actually do, given who they are. This gives them that, in 8 minutes.

Take the quest at /assessment.html if you haven't yet. It runs offline after the page loads — works on planes.
