Lab tests worth getting (and ones that aren't)

#methodology#labs#practical

You can spend $5,000 a year on labs at a longevity clinic and still not know much that actually matters. You can spend $200 on the right panel and know enough to make 80% of the relevant decisions. Here's our take on what's worth measuring.

The principle: a test is worth running if and only if the result changes what you'd do. If your behaviour is the same whether the number is high, normal, or low, the test is theatre.

What's worth getting (and roughly how often)

Annual bloodwork

Lipid panel — full, including ApoB. ApoB is a better cardiovascular predictor than LDL. Most standard panels don't include it; ask for it. If your ApoB is high, it changes whether you're a statin candidate. If it's low, the rest of your CV risk picture is reassuring. Standard lipid (total cholesterol, HDL, LDL, triglycerides) is fine but ApoB adds real signal.

Fasting glucose + HbA1c. Pre-diabetes is silent and reversible. The earlier you catch it, the easier the lifestyle response (weight, exercise, time-restricted eating) is sufficient. If HbA1c crosses 5.7%, the conversation about metformin or GLP-1s becomes appropriate.

hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein). Persistent elevation suggests systemic inflammation that may not be obvious from symptoms. Doesn't tell you the source — but it's the trigger to look for one (periodontal disease, NAFLD, autoimmune issues, chronic infection).

Vitamin D (25-OH-D). Common deficiency. Below 30 ng/mL means supplement; below 20 means supplement and re-test. Above 40 means stop supplementing — extra doesn't help.

Vitamin B12. Especially important if vegetarian, on metformin, or over 60 (gastric absorption declines with age). Below 200 pg/mL means supplement; the symptoms of deficiency (fatigue, neuropathy) are non-specific until late.

TSH (thyroid). Subclinical hypothyroidism is common, especially in women over 50. TSH > 4 with normal free T4 means watch and re-test; TSH > 10 means treat. Cheaper to catch early than to chase fatigue and weight gain through other tests.

Complete blood count + iron studies (ferritin, transferrin saturation). Iron deficiency without anaemia is common in menstruating women and vegetarian men. Hemochromatosis (iron overload) is also common in northern European descent and silently destructive. Either matters.

Liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT). NAFLD is common (40% of US adults) and reversible. Persistent ALT elevation in someone with elevated BMI is a strong nudge toward time-restricted eating, weight loss, and reducing alcohol. GGT is the most sensitive to alcohol intake.

Kidney function (creatinine, eGFR). Affects dosing of essentially every medication and supplement. Once a year minimum for adults; more often after 60.

That panel — done annually — is roughly $150-300 at a regular lab. It's the foundation. Every decision in this catalogue is more accurate if you have these numbers.

Every 2-5 years (depending on results)

Coronary calcium score. Non-contrast CT scan, ~$150-300, 5 minutes. Quantifies actual atherosclerosis in your coronary arteries. A score of 0 means very low CV risk for the next 10 years. A score >100 means established disease and changes the conversation about statins, BP targets, and aspirin. Better than any blood marker for predicting whether you'll have a heart attack in the next decade.

DEXA scan (body composition). Once at 40-50 to establish baseline, then every 2-5 years. Measures bone density (key for osteoporosis screening, especially women post-menopause), visceral fat, and limb lean mass. Visceral fat is more harmful than subcutaneous; the DEXA quantifies both.

Colonoscopy (or stool DNA test) at 45+. Standard of care; the most evidence-supported cancer screening we have. If you're over 45 and haven't done either, do this before you do most things on this site.

Mammography for women, low-dose CT for current/former heavy smokers. USPSTF guidelines apply.

Periodically when relevant

Fasting insulin / HOMA-IR. Insulin resistance precedes elevated glucose by years. If you're metabolically suspicious (high triglycerides, low HDL, central adiposity) but glucose is normal, fasting insulin will often catch it. Useful for guiding the metabolic-foundation interventions (TRE, exercise, GLP-1 considerations).

Lp(a). Lipoprotein(a) is genetic, doesn't respond to diet/exercise/statins much. Test once in your life (level doesn't change). High Lp(a) is independent CV risk; affects whether you're aggressive on the rest of CV management.

Homocysteine. If elevated, B-vitamin status matters more (MTHFR variant + low folate/B12 picture). Cheap, interpretable, only worth running once if normal.

What's not worth getting (or worth being cautious about)

Tons of vitamins and minerals on a single panel. Most micronutrient panels are sales tools more than diagnostic tools. The ones with strong evidence (D, B12) are above. Vitamin C, E, K1, magnesium, zinc — supplementing without testing is fine if you're not pathologically deficient; testing without specific indication usually doesn't change much.

Gut microbiome tests. Currently $200-400 of theatre. The science is real but the consumer reports don't yet generate actionable recommendations beyond "eat more fibre and fermented foods" — which we already recommend. Wait for clinical microbiome testing to mature.

Hormone panels for asymptomatic adults. Total testosterone, estradiol, cortisol salivary curves, DHEA-S — interesting numbers, rarely actionable in healthy people. Get tested when there are symptoms, not because you're optimising.

Telomere length. Lab-to-lab variation is large; the relationship between telomere length and longevity outcomes is weaker than the marketing suggests; the test rarely changes behaviour. Skip.

Most "biological age" tests. Several companies (TruDiagnostic, GlycanAge, others) sell DNA-methylation or glycan-based "biological age" tests for $200-500. The signal is real (methylation-age differences predict mortality at the population level), but inter-test variability is large, intra-individual reproducibility is mediocre, and the actionable advice doesn't differ from what's already obvious from your basic numbers (CV risk + glucose + fitness). Possibly useful for tracking interventions over years; rarely justifies the cost as standalone screening.

Whole-body MRI screening (Prenuvo etc.). $2,500. Identifies incidental findings that often turn out to be benign and trigger expensive follow-up. The evidence that whole-body screening MRI reduces mortality vs targeted screening is absent. The harms (overdiagnosis, anxiety, cascading imaging) are real. Decline unless your individual risk profile justifies it.

Allergy panels for unexplained symptoms. IgG food sensitivity tests are particularly notorious — IgG to food is the normal response to eating that food, not pathology. The tests sell elimination diets that temporarily make people feel better via placebo and dietary attention, then create unnecessary food fears.

How to interpret results

A few patterns that come up:

Trends matter more than single points. Your ApoB rising from 80 to 95 over three years is more informative than any single value at the high or low end. Always compare to your own previous results before comparing to population reference ranges.

Optimal isn't the same as normal. "Normal" reference ranges describe the middle 95% of the population — including a lot of metabolically unwell people. Optimal values for many markers (HbA1c, hs-CRP, ApoB, vitamin D, B12) are tighter than the lab's reference range. Doctors who specialise in preventive cardiology or metabolic health usually use tighter targets than primary care.

One value doesn't make a diagnosis. A high CRP in isolation isn't a problem; a high CRP that persists across two months with no obvious source is a problem. Re-test borderline values before reacting.

Labs are inputs to decisions, not the decision itself. A normal lab doesn't mean you can skip the foundations (sleep, exercise, diet). An abnormal lab doesn't mean you need a supplement to fix it; usually it points to a behavioural or treatment intervention.

What to skip your way to

Most of the things on this site are recommendable without lab tests. Don't smoke. Move daily. Sleep enough. Eat plants and protein. Resistance training. Social connection. None of these require bloodwork to start.

The labs above are useful because they help calibrate the medication and targeted intervention layers — should you take a statin, do you need vitamin D, is metformin reasonable. They're not foundational; they're a refinement.

If you've never had this panel, start there. Then decide.
