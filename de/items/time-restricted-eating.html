Time-Restricted Eating

Compressed eating window. Modest metabolic benefit; mortality data thin.

Bottom Line

Probably useful as eating-discipline structure. Not magic — calorie quality and quantity dominate. Skipping breakfast is fine; skipping dinner may be metabolically better.

What this means for you

Eating in an 8–10 hour window each day (skip breakfast OR skip dinner) gives modest metabolic benefits and many people find it useful as a discipline structure. It's not magic — total calorie quality and quantity still dominate. There's a slight signal that eating earlier in the day is metabolically better than eating later.

How to actually do this

  • Pick an 8–10 hour eating window and keep it consistent. Common choice: 11am–7pm or 10am–6pm.
  • If possible, skip dinner instead of breakfast — early eating is metabolically slightly better.
  • Drink water, plain tea, or black coffee outside the window. Anything with calories breaks the fast.
  • Don't compress your normal calories into a binge. Aim for the same total food, different timing.
  • Keep protein intake high (1.6 g/kg bodyweight). Tighter windows risk muscle loss without enough protein.
  • Don't combine with extreme calorie restriction unless you know what you're doing.
  • If you have an eating disorder history, skip this entirely. The structure can become disordered fast.
Rough cost: Free.

Worth considering if

People who like rules and structure, people with mild metabolic concerns, people who already snack late at night and want a wind-down cue.

Probably skip if

Eating disorder history (any). Pregnancy. Athletes during heavy training blocks. Type 1 diabetes (medication timing complications). Underweight individuals.

Helps with

Diabetes / metabolic Chronic inflammation

Why it works

Your body has a daily rhythm — insulin sensitivity, digestion, hormone production all peak at certain times. Eating in a compressed window aligns intake with that rhythm and gives you a long nightly fasting state, when cells switch from growth mode to cleanup-and-repair. The simpler effect: fewer hours of eating means fewer total calories for most people, without needing willpower.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Eating only within a 6–12h daily window. Aligns intake with circadian metabolism, allows nightly fasting state (autophagy, ketone production, insulin reset). Most popular forms: 16:8 (16h fast / 8h eat) or 14:10.

Evidence Summary

TREAT trial (Lowe et al. 2020) showed 16:8 produced minimal weight loss vs 3 meals/day if calorie-matched, raising the question of whether calorie restriction (not timing) is doing the work. Other RCTs show modest improvements in glucose, BP, lipids when window is tight (8h or less). Whether timing OR calorie reduction is the active ingredient is debated. NHANES data suggest 16:8 may correlate with HIGHER CV mortality (debated).

Key Studies

Effects of Time-Restricted Eating on Weight Loss and Other Metabolic Parameters (TREAT) ↗
RCT n=116 2020 JAMA Internal Medicine
Lowe et al.
Finding: 16:8 produced 0.94 kg weight loss vs 0.68 kg in 3-meal control. Difference not statistically meaningful.
Limitations: Free-living calorie estimates; short duration.
Calorie Restriction with or without Time-Restricted Eating in Weight Loss (Liu et al.) ↗
RCT n=139 2022 NEJM
Liu et al.
Finding: TRE + calorie restriction = calorie restriction alone for weight loss over 12 months.
Limitations: Calorie deficit may dominate.

Studied Dose / Protocol

8-10h eating window, with eating earlier in day shown more metabolically favorable.
