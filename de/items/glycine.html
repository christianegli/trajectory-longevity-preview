Glycine

Cheap amino acid with small but consistent RCT evidence for improving sleep onset and quality.

Bottom Line

Cheap, safe, modest sleep benefit. Worth trying if sleep latency is your bottleneck. Magnesium glycinate gives you both at once. Don't expect dramatic results — this is a small effect.

What this means for you

A cheap amino acid (a building block of protein, like in collagen) that helps you fall asleep faster. Small studies show 3g before bed cuts sleep onset time and improves sleep quality. Effect is modest but real and consistent across studies.

How to actually do this

3 g glycine powder (a rounded teaspoon) dissolved in water, 30-60 minutes before bed. Or take magnesium glycinate (300-400 mg of magnesium gives you ~2g glycine). Slightly sweet taste.

Rough cost: $8-15/month for bulk glycine powder. Magnesium glycinate ~$15/month.

Worth considering if

['You take >30 minutes to fall asleep most nights', 'You wake up feeling unrefreshed', 'You want a cheaper, safer alternative to melatonin', 'You already need magnesium — get the glycinate form']

Probably skip if

['You sleep fine — no upside', 'You have a serious sleep disorder (apnea, RLS) — fix the underlying issue first']

Helps with

Cognitive decline

Why it works

Glycine drops your core body temperature slightly by widening skin blood vessels. A small drop in core temperature is one of the strongest signals your body uses to fall asleep — it's why a warm bath an hour before bed helps.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Glycine acts on NMDA receptors as a co-agonist and on glycine receptors as an inhibitory neurotransmitter. Pre-sleep oral dose causes peripheral vasodilation, dropping core body temperature ~0.2°C — a major thermoregulatory cue for sleep onset. Also crosses the blood-brain barrier and may modulate suprachiasmatic nucleus activity.

Evidence Summary

Multiple small RCTs (n=10-30) by Yamadera/Inagawa/Bannai consistently show 3g glycine before bed reduces sleep latency, improves subjective sleep quality, and reduces next-day fatigue. Mechanism (core temp drop) confirmed via thermography. Effect smaller than melatonin for circadian shifts but better tolerated and no morning grogginess. Long-term safety data limited but glycine is a normal dietary amino acid (~2g/day from food).

Key Studies

The sleep-improving effects of glycine ingestion before bedtime in human volunteers ↗
RCT (crossover, polysomnography) n=19 2007 Sleep Biol Rhythms
Yamadera W, Inagawa K, Chiba S, Bannai M, Takahashi M, Nakayama K
Finding: 3g glycine before bed reduced subjective sleep latency, improved sleep quality (PSG-confirmed slow-wave sleep increase), reduced next-morning fatigue.
Limitations: Small sample. Subjective endpoints. Industry-funded (Ajinomoto).
Subjective effects of glycine ingestion before bedtime on sleep quality ↗
RCT n=11 2006 Sleep Biol Rhythms
Inagawa K, Hiraoka T, Kohda T, Yamadera W, Takahashi M
Finding: 3g glycine improved subjective sleep quality and reduced sleepiness/fatigue scales next day in subjects with mild insomnia complaints.
Limitations: Tiny sample. Subjective only. Same research group (industry-funded).

Studied Dose / Protocol

3 g (one rounded teaspoon of powder) 30-60 minutes before bed.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Sleep latency
  • Sleep quality (PSG)
  • Next-day alertness

Possible Side Effects

  • Mild stomach or gut upset at high doses
  • Slightly sweet taste

Who Should Avoid This

  • None known at studied doses
