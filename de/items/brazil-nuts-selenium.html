Brazil Nuts (Selenium)

Cheapest reliable selenium source. 1-2 nuts/day matches RDA. Don't exceed — selenium toxicity is real.

Bottom Line

Cheap correction for a real deficiency. 1-2 Brazil nuts daily covers the RDA reliably and uses the food matrix the body evolved with. Don't take selenium on top, don't go higher than 2 nuts. Test plasma selenium if you're uncertain or take other selenium-containing supplements. The supplementation story is U-shaped — correct deficiency, don't oversupplement.

What this means for you

Selenium is an essential trace mineral that's hard to get reliably from food in many regions where soil is selenium-poor. Brazil nuts are the unusual exception — a single nut can contain 50–500 micrograms of selenium (the RDA is 55 µg, the upper safe limit is 400 µg). One or two nuts daily covers the RDA cheaply. Selenium is a cofactor for selenoproteins that protect cells from oxidative damage, support thyroid function, and regulate immune response. Don't go higher than 2-3 nuts daily — selenium toxicity (selenosis) causes hair loss, brittle nails, and neurological symptoms.

How to actually do this

1-2 Brazil nuts per day, not more. Variability is huge — Brazilian-soil-grown nuts can have 5-10x more selenium than those grown elsewhere. If supplementing instead, 100-200 µg selenium daily (selenomethionine or selenium yeast forms preferred over selenite). DON'T take Brazil nuts AND a selenium supplement simultaneously — easy to overshoot. Test plasma selenium if uncertain (target 100-130 µg/L).

Rough cost: $5-10/mo

Worth considering if

Adults in selenium-poor regions (much of Europe, parts of US Midwest, China) without regular fish/shellfish intake. People with autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's): adequate selenium status moderates anti-TPO antibodies. Vegans (selenium intake from plant sources is highly variable).

Probably skip if

Anyone already taking a multivitamin or supplement with selenium (most contain 50-200 µg). People with normal/high plasma selenium (>120 µg/L) — additional intake may increase prostate cancer risk per SELECT findings. Children: dose differently and don't approach 1 nut/day.

Helps with

Immune aging

Why it works

Selenium gets incorporated into a small group of proteins called selenoproteins. These do specific jobs: glutathione peroxidase neutralises peroxide damage, iodothyronine deiodinases activate thyroid hormones, thioredoxin reductase manages oxidation in the cell. When selenium is deficient, these systems work poorly. When you have enough, adding more doesn't help — and at high levels it becomes toxic.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Selenium is incorporated as selenocysteine into ~25 selenoproteins via UGA codon recoding using SECIS elements. Key selenoproteins: GPx1-4 (glutathione peroxidases — peroxide reduction), iodothyronine deiodinases DIO1-3 (T4→T3 conversion), thioredoxin reductases TXNRD1-3 (redox regulation, ribonucleotide reductase regeneration). Plasma selenium plateau ~120 µg/L; above this, additional selenium increases urinary excretion and accumulates in toxic forms (selenium-glutathione complexes). The relationship between selenium status and cancer risk is U-shaped: deficiency increases prostate and other cancers, supraphysiologic supplementation may also increase risk (SELECT 2011).

Evidence Summary

Selenium has a clear deficiency-correction story and a more complicated supplementation story. Rayman 2012 (Lancet) review: in genuinely selenium-deficient populations (Keshan disease in China — historic; Iodine Global Network maps modern deficiency), correction reduces specific endpoints (Keshan cardiomyopathy, viral susceptibility). Above sufficiency, the evidence flips. SELECT (Lippman 2009 + Klein 2011, JAMA): 35,533 men randomised to selenium 200 µg/day, vitamin E, both, or placebo. Trial stopped early. Long-term follow-up (Klein 2011) found selenium *increased* prostate cancer in men with high baseline selenium. NPC trial (Clark 1996, JAMA) showed earlier selenium reduction in cancer mortality, but the cohort was selenium-deficient — context-dependent. Watanabe 2020 review of selenium in Hashimoto's thyroiditis: 200 µg/day for 3-6 months reduced anti-TPO antibodies in deficient/sufficient adults. Schomburg 2019 review of selenium in critical illness: low selenium status predicts mortality. The clinical translation: correct deficiency, don't oversupplement.

Key Studies

Effect of selenium and vitamin E on risk of prostate cancer (SELECT, Klein 2011) ↗
RCT, 4 arms n=35533 2011 JAMA
Klein et al. (SELECT investigators)
Finding: Selenium 200 µg/day did not reduce prostate cancer overall; in subgroup with high baseline selenium, selenium supplementation INCREASED prostate cancer risk. Trial demonstrated supraphysiologic supplementation has costs.
Limitations: Selenium-replete US population at baseline; results don't apply to deficient populations.
Selenium and human health (Rayman 2012) ↗
Comprehensive review 2012 Lancet
Rayman M.
Finding: Selenium is essential. Deficiency causes specific diseases (Keshan, Kashin-Beck, AIDS progression). Supplementation above sufficiency offers diminishing returns and may increase risks. Plasma 100-130 µg/L is target.
Limitations: Review, not primary trial.
Selenium in Hashimoto's thyroiditis (Watanabe 2020) ↗
Systematic review of 9 RCTs 2020 Endocrine Connections
Watanabe et al.
Finding: Selenium 200 µg/day for 3-6 months reduced anti-TPO antibodies in Hashimoto's thyroiditis patients across multiple trials. Effect on T4/T3/TSH less consistent.
Limitations: Antibody marker, not symptom-based; mostly Italian/European cohorts; some industry funding.
Selenomethionine consumption from Brazil nuts in healthy adults (Thomson 2008) ↗
RCT n=60 2008 American Journal of Clinical Nutrition
Thomson et al.
Finding: 2 Brazil nuts daily for 12 weeks raised plasma selenium more effectively than 100 µg selenomethionine supplement. Demonstrates the food-vs-pill bioavailability advantage.
Limitations: New Zealand cohort (selenium-poor soil); brief duration.

Studied Dose / Protocol

1-2 Brazil nuts/day (~50-200 µg selenium); 100-200 µg selenium supplement (selenomethionine or selenium yeast)

Possible Side Effects

  • Excess: hair loss, brittle nails, garlic breath, stomach or gut upset, peripheral neuropathy. Reflective of plasma >150 µg/L. Recoverable on discontinuation.

Who Should Avoid This

  • Already taking multivitamin with selenium. Recently diagnosed prostate cancer (high-dose selenium controversial; consult oncologist). Children at adult doses.
