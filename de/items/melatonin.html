Melatonin

Sleep regulator with broader antioxidant + mitochondrial effects. Lower doses better than commonly sold.

Bottom Line

Cheap, generally safe, possibly beneficial beyond sleep. Use the lowest effective dose (often 0.3-0.5mg) — bigger isn't better.

What this means for you

Melatonin isn't just a sleep aid — it's a master signal for your body's daily rhythm and a surprisingly potent antioxidant. Most over-the-counter doses (3–10 mg) are 10–30x higher than what works. Try 0.3–0.5 mg first; bigger doses can cause grogginess and aren't more effective. Cheap, generally safe, useful for jet lag and shift work.

How to actually do this

  • Start low: 0.3–0.5 mg, 30–60 min before target bedtime. Most people don't need more.
  • Buy it where you can get a low dose. Most US bottles are 3–10 mg — break them or buy 0.3 mg specifically (Life Extension and a few others stock low doses).
  • Take it the same time each night to anchor your rhythm.
  • For jet lag: 0.5 mg at the new bedtime for 3–5 nights starting the night you arrive (not the night before).
  • If you wake groggy, your dose is too high.
  • Skip if you're on warfarin (interaction). Caution with autoimmune conditions (theoretical immune-modulation).
  • Don't give to kids without a pediatrician — long-term effects on developing endocrine system are unstudied.
Rough cost: $3–$10/month.

Worth considering if

Adults over 50 with declining sleep quality. Travelers crossing time zones. Shift workers. Anyone whose 'sleep onset' is the main problem (not maintenance).

Probably skip if

Pregnant or trying to conceive. On warfarin. Severe autoimmune disease (talk to doctor first). Children unless prescribed.

Cognitive decline Mitochondrial decline Other neurodegenerative

Why it works

Your brain's pineal gland releases melatonin when it gets dark, which is your body's signal that it's time to shift into sleep-and-repair mode. Production drops dramatically with age — older adults make a fraction of what teenagers do. Beyond sleep, melatonin also acts as a free-radical scavenger inside mitochondria, where most cellular damage happens. Many of the effects attributed to it require very small doses; megadosing may actually disrupt your natural rhythm.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Pineal hormone regulating circadian rhythm. Also a potent free-radical scavenger and supports mitochondrial function. Endogenous production declines significantly with age.

Evidence Summary

Strong evidence for sleep latency in jet lag and shift work; modest in primary insomnia. Higher doses (3-10mg) shown in some trials to reduce mortality risk in elderly cohorts and benefit COVID outcomes. Most US supplement doses (3-10mg) are 10-30x physiological; 0.3-0.5mg may be more appropriate for sleep.

Finding: Modest improvement in sleep latency (-7 min) and total sleep time (+8 min).

Studied Dose / Protocol

0.3-10mg before bed. Lower doses (0.3-1mg) often as effective as higher.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Sleep quality
  • Mortality (small RCTs)
  • Inflammatory markers

Possible Side Effects

  • Vivid dreams
  • Morning grogginess at high doses
  • Hormonal effects at very high doses (theoretical)

Who Should Avoid This

  • Autoimmune disease (theoretical immune-modulation)
  • Pregnancy
  • On warfarin
