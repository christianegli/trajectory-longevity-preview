SGLT2 Inhibitors (Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin)

Cardio-renal protection in diabetes, heart failure, and CKD — even without diabetes.

Bottom Line

Among the most exciting cardiology drugs of the past decade. Direct longevity-relevant outcomes in non-diabetics. Off-label longevity use is increasingly discussed but should involve a physician familiar with the class.

What this means for you

SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin / Jardiance, dapagliflozin / Farxiga) are originally diabetes drugs that turned out to dramatically reduce death from heart failure and slow kidney decline — even in people who don't have diabetes. They're among the most exciting cardiology drugs of the last decade. Off-label longevity use is increasingly discussed but should involve a doctor familiar with the class.

How to actually do this

  • Ask your doctor — this is prescription. Easier to get if you have type 2 diabetes, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease.
  • Empagliflozin 10–25 mg/day or dapagliflozin 5–10 mg/day. Once daily, morning.
  • Drink more water than usual. Mild diuretic effect is normal but dehydration is a real risk, especially in summer or with stomach bugs.
  • Practice good genital hygiene — these slightly raise yeast-infection risk in both sexes.
  • Stop the drug if you're going to be NPO for surgery (last dose 3 days before). Restart after.
  • Don't take it if you have type 1 diabetes — risk of euglycemic ketoacidosis.
  • Brief illness with vomiting/diarrhea: pause it temporarily.
Rough cost: $300–$600/month list price; insurance often covers for diabetes/heart-failure indications. Few generic options yet.

Worth considering if

People with type 2 diabetes, heart failure (any ejection fraction), CKD with proteinuria. Off-label: metabolic syndrome with cardiovascular risk after physician discussion.

Probably skip if

Type 1 diabetes. Recurrent UTIs or genital fungal infections. Severe renal impairment. Frail elderly with hypotension risk.

Helps with

Heart disease Diabetes / metabolic

Why it works

Your kidneys normally reabsorb almost all the sugar that leaks into urine, sending it back to your blood. These drugs block one of the reabsorption transporters, so excess sugar (and a bit of sodium and water) leaves in the urine. The mild calorie loss helps weight, the diuretic effect helps blood pressure and heart failure, and there's a separate metabolic effect — your body shifts toward burning fat and producing ketones — that seems to protect heart muscle directly.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Inhibits sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 in proximal tubule → glycosuria + natriuresis → mild diuresis, calorie loss, ketone production. Independently improves cardiac and renal outcomes.

Evidence Summary

EMPA-REG OUTCOME (2015) showed 38% CV death reduction with empagliflozin in T2DM. DAPA-HF and EMPEROR-Reduced extended benefit to heart failure with reduced ejection fraction WITHOUT diabetes. DAPA-CKD showed renal + mortality benefit in CKD without diabetes. ITP showed canagliflozin extends male mouse lifespan ~14%.

Key Studies

Empagliflozin, Cardiovascular Outcomes, and Mortality in T2DM (EMPA-REG OUTCOME) ↗
RCT n=7020 2015 NEJM
Zinman et al.
Finding: 38% reduction in CV death, 32% in all-cause mortality, in T2DM with established CVD.
Limitations: Diabetic, high-risk population.
Dapagliflozin in Patients with Heart Failure and Reduced Ejection Fraction (DAPA-HF) ↗
RCT n=4744 2019 NEJM
McMurray et al.
Finding: 26% reduction in CV death or HF hospitalization, regardless of diabetes status.
Limitations: HFrEF only at time of trial.
Canagliflozin extends lifespan in genetically heterogeneous male mice (ITP) ↗
RCT (mice) n=700 2020 JCI Insight
Miller et al.
Finding: 14% increase in median lifespan in males. No effect in females.
Limitations: Mouse only; sex-specific.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Empagliflozin 10-25mg/day, dapagliflozin 5-10mg/day.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • CV mortality
  • All-cause mortality
  • HF hospitalization
  • Renal function decline

Possible Side Effects

  • Genital fungal infections
  • urinary tract infection risk
  • Volume depletion in elderly
  • Euglycemic ketoacidosis (rare)

Who Should Avoid This

  • Active urinary infection
  • Severe kidney problems (some)
  • type 1 diabetes (off-label, diabetic ketoacidosis risk)
