Taurine

Bottom Line

Big mouse signal, conflicting human signal. Cheap, well-tolerated, plausible upside. Modest hedge. Don't expect mouse-scale longevity in humans.

What this means for you

A 2023 study made headlines: taurine supplementation extended lifespan dramatically in mice and improved many aging markers. Human data is more mixed — some cohort studies suggest a benefit, others don't. It's cheap, well-tolerated, and has a real biological rationale. Modest hedge; don't expect mouse-scale results in humans.

How to actually do this

  • Dose: 3–6 g per day, split into 2–3 doses with meals. The mouse study used the equivalent of ~3 g for a human.
  • Powder form is cheapest — measure with a kitchen scale. It's nearly tasteless.
  • Pair with magnesium for theoretical synergy in cardiovascular and nervous-system effects.
  • Don't get it from energy drinks — the doses are tiny and the sugar/caffeine load defeats the purpose.
  • Skip if you have bipolar disorder (theoretical concern about effects on mood regulation).
  • Reassess after 3–6 months; if no biomarker or subjective improvement, reasonable to drop it.
Rough cost: $10–$25/month for bulk powder.

Worth considering if

Adults over 50, particularly with cardiovascular risk factors or low energy. Vegans and vegetarians (you get less from food).

Probably skip if

Bipolar disorder. People on bromocriptine. Pregnancy (no data).

Helps with

Heart disease Mitochondrial decline Diabetes / metabolic Frailty

Why it works

Taurine is an amino acid your body uses for several maintenance jobs: making bile acids that help digest fat, calming overactive cells, supporting your mitochondria, and acting as an antioxidant. Your levels naturally drop dramatically as you age — by 80 you have roughly 20% of what you had at 18. The theory is that restoring taurine to youthful levels helps the systems that depend on it.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Conditionally essential amino acid involved in bile acid conjugation, antioxidant defense, calcium signaling, and mitochondrial function. Levels decline ~80% with age in humans.

Evidence Summary

Singh, Maraini et al. (Science 2023) showed taurine supplementation extends mouse lifespan ~10-12%, monkey healthspan markers, and correlates with healthier aging in humans. Major caveat: ATBC observational data shows higher serum taurine correlates with HIGHER all-cause mortality in older men, contradicting the simple 'low taurine = bad aging' story. Human RCTs on glucose, BP, and exercise show modest benefit.

Mondul et al.
Finding: Higher serum taurine associated with HIGHER all-cause mortality in older male smokers (HR ~1.3).
