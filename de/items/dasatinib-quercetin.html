Dasatinib + Quercetin (D+Q Senolytic)

Mayo Clinic's pioneering senolytic combination. Promising mouse data, early human trials in specific diseases. NOT validated as a healthy-aging intervention. Don't try at home.

Bottom Line

DO NOT self-administer. The mouse data is genuinely exciting and the Mayo trials are real, but dasatinib has serious side effects that need physician monitoring and we don't know yet if intermittent dosing in healthy people produces net benefit. If you want senolytic exposure: fisetin (already in catalogue) is the safer evidence-mining-stage choice. Quercetin alone is also safe but its solo senolytic activity is weak. Watch the trial pipeline — D+Q for healthy aging will be answered in the next 5-10 years.

What this means for you

An experimental combination of a chemotherapy drug (dasatinib) and a natural flavonoid (quercetin) being tested at the Mayo Clinic as the first 'senolytic' — a drug class designed to kill off zombie cells that refuse to die and drive inflammation. The mouse data is exciting (mice live ~36% longer in late-life dosing). Early human trials in specific diseases (lung fibrosis, diabetic kidney disease) show signal. But dasatinib has serious side effects — pleural effusions, blood-cell drops, cardiac issues. Do NOT try this on your own.

How to actually do this

DO NOT self-administer. Watch for trial enrollment if you have idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, diabetic kidney disease, or are eligible for the Mayo healthy-aging trials. If you want senolytic exposure now: stick with fisetin (in catalogue) which has a much better safety profile. Discuss with a longevity-focused physician if you have specific senescence-driven disease (IPF, frailty, diabetic complications).

Rough cost: Not applicable for self-purchase. Trial enrollment is the realistic access path. (Dasatinib is ~$8000/month brand, generics available for CML treatment.)

Why it works

As you age, more and more of your cells get damaged but refuse to die. Instead they sit there leaking inflammatory chemicals that age the tissue around them. These 'zombie cells' (senescent cells) are thought to drive diseases like Alzheimer's, arthritis, and fibrosis. Dasatinib + quercetin temporarily knock these zombie cells out, letting your body clean up. The 'hit-and-run' part is interesting: you only take it for 2-3 days at a time, and the effects last for weeks.

For the curious — technical mechanism

Dasatinib inhibits BCR-ABL and SRC-family tyrosine kinases, including ephrin receptors and pro-survival kinase signaling in senescent cells. Quercetin inhibits BCL-2 family anti-apoptotic proteins (BCL-XL, BCL-W) and modulates PI3K/AKT and HIF-1α. Together they trigger apoptosis in senescent cells while sparing healthy cells, which use different pro-survival pathways. Hit-and-run kinetics: brief exposure clears cells; new senescent cells re-accumulate over weeks.

Evidence Summary

Strong mouse data (Xu 2018 Nat Med, Justice 2019): D+Q reduces senescent cell burden, improves physical function, extends lifespan modestly. First human pilot in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (Justice 2019, n=14): physical function improved at 1 week post-dose. AFFIRM-LITE trial (n=12, 2022) in diabetic kidney disease: senescent cell burden reduced. NIH-funded Mayo trials are running for Alzheimer's and frailty. Dasatinib is a real chemotherapy drug with serious side effects (pleural effusions, cytopenias, cardiac). DO NOT take this for general anti-aging — risk-benefit is unproven and the dasatinib side effect profile is real.

Key Studies

Senolytics improve physical function and increase lifespan in old age ↗
Mouse studies 2018 Nat Med
Xu M, Pirtskhalava T, Farr JN, Weigand BM, Palmer AK, Weivoda MM, Inman CL, Ogrodnik MB, Hachfeld CM, Fraser DG, Onken JL, Johnson KO, Verzosa GC, Langhi LGP, Weigl M, Giorgadze N, LeBrasseur NK, Miller JD, Jurk D, Singh RJ, Allison DB, Ejima K, Hubbard GB, Ikeno Y, Cubro H, Garovic VD, Hou X, Weroha SJ, Robbins PD, Niedernhofer LJ, Khosla S, Tchkonia T, Kirkland JL
Finding: D+Q intermittent dosing in old mice reduced senescent cell burden, improved walking speed, hand grip strength, treadmill endurance, and median lifespan extended ~36% in late-life dosing. Effects persistent after dosing stopped (hit-and-run kinetics).
Limitations: Mouse only. Lifespan extension figure debated. Translation to humans uncertain.
Senolytics in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis: results from a first-in-human, open-label, pilot study ↗
Open-label pilot n=14 2019 EBioMedicine
Justice JN, Nambiar AM, Tchkonia T, LeBrasseur NK, Pascual R, Hashmi SK, Prata L, Masternak MM, Kritchevsky SB, Musi N, Kirkland JL
Finding: D 100mg + Q 1250mg for 3 days (3 cycles over 3 weeks) in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis: 6-min walk distance, gait speed, and chair-rise improved at 1 week post-dose. Senescent cell biomarkers reduced.
Limitations: Open-label, no placebo, n=14. Disease-specific (IPF), not healthy aging.

Studied Dose / Protocol

Dasatinib 100mg + quercetin 1000-1250mg, 2-3 consecutive days every 2-4 weeks (intermittent 'hit-and-run' dosing). NOTE: Dasatinib is a prescription chemotherapy drug. This is an experimental research protocol, not a consumer regimen.

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • Senescent cell burden (multiple tissues)
  • Physical function (walking speed, grip)
  • Lifespan (mouse only)
  • Disease-specific endpoints (IPF, diabetic kidney, hematopoietic stem cells)

Possible Side Effects

  • Dasatinib: pleural effusions (5-25%), cytopenias, fluid retention, cardiac arrhythmia, stomach or gut bleeding, severe drug interactions
  • Quercetin alone: very well-tolerated

Who Should Avoid This

  • Anyone not enrolled in a clinical trial under physician supervision
  • On blood thinners
  • Active cancer (unless prescribed for that specific condition)
  • Cardiac arrhythmia
  • Pleural disease or pulmonary hypertension
