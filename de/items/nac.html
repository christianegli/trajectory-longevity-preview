N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC)

Glutathione precursor with strong evidence for COPD exacerbations and acetaminophen overdose. Modest evidence for general antioxidant benefit.

Bottom Line

Solid evidence for COPD/chronic bronchitis. Plausible but unproven for general anti-aging via glutathione. Cheap, safe at studied doses. Worth considering if you have chronic respiratory issues; less clear value for healthy people. The newer GlyNAC research (with glycine) shows more promise — see GlyNAC entry.

What this means for you

An amino acid that your body uses to make glutathione, which is your body's most important antioxidant. Best evidence: it cuts COPD flare-ups by about 25-30% in people with chronic lung disease. Originally developed (and still used) as an emergency antidote for Tylenol overdose. For healthy people, the longevity evidence is weaker — combined with glycine (see GlyNAC) shows more promise.

How to actually do this

Standard adult dose: 600 mg twice daily with food, or 1200 mg once daily. For COPD: 600 mg twice daily long-term. Take with food to minimize GI upset. The unpleasant sulfur smell is normal — choose enteric-coated capsules if odor bothers you.

Rough cost: $8-15/month for generic NAC 600mg capsules.

Worth considering if

['Diagnosed COPD or chronic bronchitis with frequent exacerbations', 'You want a glutathione precursor and are 60+', 'PCOS (modest evidence for menstrual regulation)', 'Heavy acetaminophen user (theoretical liver protection)']

Probably skip if

["You're healthy with no respiratory issues — modest benefit", "You're already on inhaled mucolytics", 'You have asthma — discuss with doctor (rare bronchospasm risk)']

Helps with

Immune aging

Why it works

Your cells use glutathione to neutralize damage from pollution, alcohol, exercise, and aging. As you age, glutathione levels drop. NAC gives your body the raw material to make more glutathione. It also breaks up sticky mucus, which is why it's used in lung diseases.

For the curious — technical mechanism

NAC is rapidly hydrolyzed to L-cysteine, the rate-limiting amino acid for glutathione (GSH) synthesis. Glutathione (a tripeptide of glutamate-cysteine-glycine) is the major intracellular antioxidant, peaking in liver and lung. NAC also breaks disulfide bonds in mucus glycoproteins (mucolytic) and inhibits NF-κB activation (anti-inflammatory). May modestly chelate metals.

Evidence Summary

Cochrane meta-analysis (8 RCTs, n=>3,000 COPD patients): NAC 1200mg/day reduced exacerbation rate by 25-30%. PANTHEON trial (n=1006, 2014): similar finding. Strong evidence in respiratory conditions. Smaller evidence base for psychiatric uses (OCD, addiction) and PCOS. Glutathione precursor role makes it plausible as anti-aging support but no mortality trial. Generally well-tolerated; rare hypersensitivity reactions.

Key Studies

Mucolytic agents versus placebo for chronic bronchitis or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease ↗
Meta-analysis (≥30 RCTs) n=13000 2015 Cochrane Database Syst Rev
Cazzola M, Calzetta L, Page C, Jardim J, Chuchalin AG, Rogliani P, Matera MG
Finding: Mucolytics (mostly NAC at 1200 mg/day) reduced exacerbations and slightly improved quality of life in chronic bronchitis/COPD vs placebo. NNT ~8 to prevent one exacerbation per year.
Limitations: Heterogeneous formulations. Not all subjects had spirometry-confirmed COPD.
Effect of N-acetylcysteine in COPD exacerbations (PANTHEON): a randomised, double-blind placebo-controlled trial ↗
RCT (1-year) n=1006 2014 Lancet Respir Med
Zheng JP, Wen FQ, Bai CX, Wan HY, Kang J, Chen P, Yao WZ, Ma LJ, Li X, Raiteri L, Sardina M, Gao Y, Wang BS, Zhong NS
Finding: NAC 600mg twice daily reduced exacerbation rate by 22% vs placebo over 1 year in moderate-severe COPD. Most benefit in patients with frequent prior exacerbations.
Limitations: Single-country trial (China). Did not show forced expiratory volume improvement.

Studied Dose / Protocol

600-1800 mg/day in divided doses (typically 600mg 2-3×/day).

Longevity Endpoints Evaluated

  • COPD exacerbations
  • Glutathione status
  • Acetaminophen toxicity (clinical use)

Possible Side Effects

  • stomach or gut upset (most common)
  • Sulfur-like odor (taste) in some products
  • Rare hypersensitivity

Who Should Avoid This

  • Asthma (rare bronchospasm with inhaled form; oral generally safe)
  • Pre-surgery (theoretical blood-thinning effect)
